1. What We're Building

The Scenario

A rising power (Cathay) is approaching the strength of the ruling power (Columbia). History says this ends in war — the "Thucydides Trap." Participants play world leaders during the most dangerous 3-4 years in the 21st century.

The question for every participant: Can you prove history wrong? Or will you win the war it predicts?

Iterative Testing — How We Improve

We don't build and hope. We build, test with autonomous AI agents, find problems, fix, and test again. Each cycle improves the design. Here's the journey:

Battery 1 — Concept Tests (Mar 27)

6.5/10

What we tested: First engine run. 8 scenarios, basic AI agents, all 21 countries.

What broke:

  • GDP always grew — even during crises. Multiplicative factors (0.85-1.15) never produced recessions. Curves were unrealistically smooth.
  • Oil price was static ($68-72 range, barely moved despite Gulf blockade)
  • Gulf Gate double-counted — blockade affected GDP through BOTH oil price AND direct blockade fraction
  • Inflation penalty on absolute value — Persia (50% starting inflation) would collapse Round 1
Key learning: Multiplicative factor models can't produce tipping points. Real economies have thresholds. Redesigned with crisis escalation ladder: NORMAL → STRESSED → CRISIS → COLLAPSE.

Battery 2 — SEED Tests (Mar 28)

7.9/10

What improved: Crisis ladder working. Oil price now responsive ($82-250 range). GDP feedback loops chaining correctly.

What we found:

  • Tech boost compounding dangerously — 15% GDP multiplier doubled GDP in 5 rounds. Fixed to growth-rate additive (+1.5pp for L3)
  • Double naval production — two code paths both adding ships. Cathay's fleet doubled unexpectedly
  • Expert panel zero agreements — 3 AI experts evaluated different variables, never overlapping. Fixed with common critical checks
Key learning: Expert-defined corridors prevent fitting to broken formulas. Always set expected ranges BEFORE looking at engine output.

Battery 3 — Calibration (Mar 28)

8.6/10

What improved: Oil inertia (60% toward equilibrium/round). Sanctions multiplier calibrated. Tech boost as growth rate. Inflation cap added.

What we found:

  • Nordostan collapsing too fast — L3 sanctions created -0.30/round drain. Fixed with siege resilience (+0.10) and sanctions diminishing returns
  • Expert panel targeting hard variables — tried to adjust GDP directly. Fixed with soft-variable constraint (only growth rates, stability, support adjustable)
Key learning: Oil price needs inertia — markets don't jump instantly. And AI experts must be bounded — let them adjust trajectories, not hard numbers.

Battery 4 — Gate Tests (Mar 30)

6.9→fixed→est. 8.0+

What was new: Complete action review with Marat had redesigned 15+ mechanics. First test of: simplified combat, sanctions S-curve, 5-level oil, court mechanic, impeachment, intelligence pools, nuclear 5-tier system.

Initial score 6.9 — because new mechanics introduced new bugs:

  • No naval combat — ships were invulnerable (the mechanic didn't exist yet!). Fixed: added ship-vs-ship RISK dice
  • Persia auto-breakout — nuclear progress exactly at threshold, free bomb R1. Fixed: reduced progress
  • Court had no decision logic — just a process description. Fixed: full AI judge prompt
  • Combat stacking too heavy — Formosa invasion at 8% success (target: 15-25%). Fixed: Die Hard and air support don't stack

All 4 blockers + 10 high-priority items fixed same day.

What the tester rated "brilliant": Authorization chain asymmetry (autocracy sole authority vs democracy 3-person chains), narrative emergence (credible 8-round geopolitical arc from independent AI decisions), nuclear deterrence logic, sanctions adaptation S-curve, ceasefire mechanics.

20 Key Design Decisions — What We Discussed and Why

QuestionDecisionWhy This Way
Ground combatRISK dice, integer modifiers only. Attacker needs ≥ defender + 1. Both roll, moderator present.Simple enough for participants to understand in 30 seconds. No hidden formulas — just dice and modifiers revealed after the roll. Drama from uncertainty.
Why not naval superiority for blockades?Blockade = ground forces on shore. Ships alone can't close a strait.Persia's Gulf Gate blockade is coastal missile batteries — sending ships doesn't help. You need ground invasion. This is realistic AND creates the hardest military problem in the SIM.
Formosa — how hard to invade?15-25% success per combat pair. Die Hard + amphibious modifier. 3-zone naval encirclement for full blockade.Must be very hard but not impossible. Cathay needs 2:1+ superiority AND pre-softening with air strikes. Creates the Trap: Helmsman can see the math and it's terrible — but the window is closing.
Nuclear authorizationCountry-specific: Columbia needs 3 people (2 min window). Choson/Persia: leader alone, no restrictions.Autocracies with sole launch authority are the most dangerous — realistic AND creates first-strike instability. Democratic chains create command pressure ("Shield, sign NOW!").
Nuclear 10-minute clockSIM enters special mode. 10 min to authorize, 10 min flight time. Everyone watches. Response options during flight.The most intense 20 minutes of the entire SIM. Nobody knows if the warhead is conventional or nuclear until impact. Creates real-time crisis simulation WITHIN the simulation.
Sanctions — why an S-curve?Coverage 0.3 = 10% effective. 0.5 = 20%. 0.7 = 60%. 0.9 = 90%.West alone = modest pain. Add Bharata at L1 = noticeable. Get Cathay to join at any level = devastating. This makes sanctions a DIPLOMATIC game, not just an economic input.
Sanctions — who pays?Imposer cost proportional to bilateral trade weight × GDP.Columbia barely feels sanctions on Nordostan (tiny bilateral trade). Teutonia feels it almost as much as Nordostan (huge gas dependency). The European dilemma IS the gameplay.
Intelligence — why "always returns an answer"?You ask a question, you always get a report. But accuracy varies 40-90%. Wrong answers look identical to right ones.Mirrors real intelligence: agencies ALWAYS have an answer. The question is whether it's right. Cross-checking with allies (burning 2 requests to verify 1 answer) creates intelligence cooperation gameplay.
Mobilization — why finite pool?Each country has X reserve units. Partial = half. Full = all. Never recovers.Real mobilization depletes a generation. Nordostan already used half. Heartland too. The decision to mobilize is irreversible — use your reserves wisely or save them for the crisis.
Air combat — why are intercepted air units destroyed?AD intercepts with degrading probability (95%, 80%, 75%...). Intercepted = shot down permanently.Air strikes against defended zones are EXPENSIVE. 6 air vs 2 AD = expect to lose 3-4 aircraft. Creates arms-race dynamic: invest in air to project power, invest in AD to deny it.
Ships carry troopsEach ship carries 1 ground unit + up to 5 air. Sunk ship = all embarked units lost.Naval losses become devastating — you don't just lose a ship, you lose the expeditionary force ON it. Makes fleet battles the highest-stakes combat in the game.
Why humans talk face to face?App only handles AI meetings. All human-to-human negotiation is physical.This is an IMMERSIVE SIM, not a video game. The learning happens when you look someone in the eye and make a deal. The app serves the physical experience.
How does the Court work?AI judge (LLM). Both sides submit 2-3 sentence arguments. AI evaluates legality, evidence, proportionality, common sense. One-sentence verdict.Democracies need checks on executive power. No formula — quality of argument matters. Lazy complaints lose. This rewards participants who think like real lawyers.
Oil price — why 5 levels?Min / Low / Normal / High / Max for each OPEC member.Prisoner's dilemma: if all restrict, price soars, everyone profits. But each is tempted to cheat. More granularity = richer strategic choices for Wellspring.
Tariffs — per sector?Default: 1 level for all sectors. Optional: set independently for resources/industry/services/technology.Columbia might want to block Cathay's tech exports while keeping industrial trade open. Simple by default, granular when needed.
Budget — what does the player see?Revenue, minus maintenance (fixed), equals discretionary. Split across 6 buckets totaling 100%.Players need to see the trade-off: every coin for tanks is a coin NOT for social spending (→ stability risk). The budget IS the strategic decision.
Tech R&D — hidden breakthroughPlayers see progress bar but not the threshold. AI private investment secretly doubles AI track progress.Creates genuine uncertainty ("are we close to a breakthrough?") and information asymmetry ("why is Cathay advancing so fast?" — discoverable through espionage).
Three-pass engine architecturePass 1: deterministic formulas (14 chained steps). Pass 2: AI expert panel adjusts soft variables. Pass 3: coherence check + narrative.Deterministic core ensures consistency. AI panel catches qualitative dynamics (market panic, rally effects). Coherence prevents nonsensical combinations. Best of both worlds.
Persia — 3 independent commandersFurnace (Supreme Leader) and Anvil (IRGC) can BOTH authorize military. Conflicting orders = contested, neither executes.The entire Persia drama IS the power struggle. Rigid co-authorization would kill the gameplay. Contested orders force face-to-face negotiation between teammates.
Assassination — per-country probabilityDomestic: 60%. International: 20%. Levantia (Mossad): 50%. Nordostan: 30%.Reflects real capability asymmetry. 1 card per game — desperation move. Even success can backfire (martyr effect). The math is almost always against you.

4. We Need Your Input

The following design choices have been made but we value your perspective. Please share your preference for each — either in person or via message to Marat.

Q1: Default UI Theme

The app has two themes. Which should be the DEFAULT for participants during the simulation?
Light ("Strategic Paper")
Clean, professional, newspaper feel. Better readability on phones. Current default.
Dark ("Midnight Intelligence")
Immersive, intelligence-briefing feel. Better for atmosphere. More dramatic.
User Choice
Let each participant toggle. Development cost: minimal.

Q2: Country Naming Approach

All countries have fictional names that hint at their real parallels. The names were chosen to be evocative but not offensive. Some examples: Columbia (USA), Cathay (China), Nordostan (Russia), Heartland (Ukraine), Persia (Iran).
Keep Current Names
Already developed. Participants figure out parallels naturally. Creates distance from real politics.
More Abstract
Less recognizable names. More fictional distance. Harder for participants to orient.
Real Names
USA, China, Russia. Maximum clarity. But political sensitivity risk for corporate audiences.

Q3: Role Character Names

Each role has a code name: Dealer (US President), Helmsman (Chinese Chairman), Beacon (Ukrainian President), Anvil (IRGC Commander). These are the names participants use during the SIM.
Keep Code Names Only
"I'm the Dealer." Atmospheric. Creates immersion. Current approach.
Code Name + Title
"I'm Dealer, President of Columbia." Clearer for new participants.
Code Name + Real Parallel
"I'm Dealer (think: Trump)." Maximum clarity but breaks fiction.

Q4: Map Style

The global map is a 10×20 hex grid. Currently designed as a clean, schematic map (functional, not decorative). It could be upgraded to a more atmospheric "fantasy cartography" style.
Schematic (Current)
Clean hexes, labeled, functional. Fastest to read. Lowest production cost.
Atlas/Geographic
Terrain textures, ocean shading, geographic feel. More immersive. Higher production cost.
Fantasy Cartography
Parchment, illustrated borders, compass rose. Maximum atmosphere. Highest cost.

Q5: AI Assistant Voice Character

Argus (the AI assistant) speaks to participants via voice. It should feel different from AI country participants. What personality fits best?
Sharp Colleague
Fast, direct, slightly warm. Like a smart analyst who's on your side. Current design.
Calm Authority
Measured, reassuring, slightly formal. Like a senior diplomat briefing you.
Warm Coach
Encouraging, supportive, asks questions. Like a leadership coach during a crisis exercise.

Q6: Physical Dice vs App Dice for Combat

Ground attacks and naval combat use RISK-style dice. Both players roll, moderator witnesses. Should this be physical dice in the room or digital in the app?
Physical Dice
Real dice on the table. Maximum drama. Tangible. But slower, needs moderator to record.
App Dice
Animated roll in the app. Automatic recording. Faster. But less visceral.
Both Available
Moderator chooses per situation. Physical for big moments, app for routine. Maximum flexibility.

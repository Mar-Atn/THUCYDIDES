## Scenario 5 (Seed: 100)

**Summary:** The baseline V2 scenario. Cathay closes the GDP gap from 0.679 to 0.943 over eight rounds, driven by compounding AI-boosted growth (reaching 8% effective by Round 5). The Eastern Europe war grinds on with Ruthenia capturing the central front in Round 2 but stalling on the southern front for six straight rounds. Persia blocks the Strait of Hormuz in Round 6, spiking oil from $61 to $98/bbl -- the single most dramatic economic event of the run. Sarmatia fires conventional missiles at Ruthenia's capital three times (Rounds 1, 6, 8) while its stability slowly bleeds from 5.0 to 1.86. Columbia surfs above it all: stability climbing from 7.0 to 9.07, political support from 44% to 87.6%, GDP from 280 to 363.6. A comfortable superpower watching the world burn at a safe distance.

### Scenario Verdict

**Interesting dynamics:** The Hormuz blockade in Round 6 was the standout event, spiking oil 60% and demonstrating the chokepoint mechanic works. The central front capture (Round 2) followed by a long southern stalemate produced a plausible war narrative. Cathay's convergence toward Columbia is the structural engine driving everything.

**What worked:** Sarmatia's gradual decline (5.0 -> 1.86 stability over 8 rounds) feels realistic for a sanctioned autocracy fighting a war. Ruthenia's zombie-state status (GDP $1.3, support 0%, but 37 military units from Western aid) captures the Ukraine dependency dynamic. Columbia's steady climb in all metrics shows institutional stability working.

**What failed:** No Formosa crisis despite Cathay reaching near-parity. The southern front combat became repetitive (same 3-vs-1 battle four rounds in a row with identical results). Persia's blockade had no consequence for Persia itself -- no military retaliation, no escalation spiral.

## Scenario 6 (Seed: 200)

**Summary:** The "slow burn" scenario. Cathay grows more slowly here than in other runs (5.08% final growth vs. 8%+ in runs 5/7/8), producing the widest final GDP gap of any V2 run (ratio 0.834). Persia blockades Hormuz earlier -- in Round 3 rather than Round 6 -- causing oil to spike to $98 in Round 3 and stay elevated ($93) for the remaining five rounds. This is the longest sustained oil shock across all scenarios, and it shaped the run: oil producers benefited, importers were squeezed. Ruthenia collapses faster (stability hits 1.0 by Round 4, support hits 0% by Round 5), while Sarmatia holds together slightly better (final stability 2.19 vs. 1.86 in Run 5). The Eastern Europe war grinds on the central front throughout all 8 rounds without Ruthenia ever capturing the zone -- the most complete military stalemate of any run.

The absence of a Hormuz blockade creates genuine divergence from other scenarios.

**What failed:** Cathay's 12.56% growth is implausible -- even China at its peak never sustained double-digit growth with a GDP above $300 (in sim units = ~$19T real). The AI tech bonus compounding needs a ceiling. Persia choosing NOT to blockade Hormuz in this seed produced a better outcome for Persia (stability 3.71, the highest of any run) -- but there's no in-game logic for why the seed affects this strategic decision. The combat system still produces repetitive stalemate patterns.

## What the Scenarios Tell Us About the Design

The four V2 runs reveal a simulation that has solved its most critical V1 problem -- stable countries no longer collapse -- while exposing a new set of structural questions about variance, escalation, and compounding.

**Do different seeds produce different stories?** Partially. The combat outcomes vary meaningfully: Run 5 captures the central front in Round 2, Run 6 never captures it at all, Run 7 suffers three total defeats before a Round 4 breakthrough, and Run 8 grinds through to Round 6. Persia's Hormuz blockade timing diverges (Round 3 in Run 6, Round 6 in Runs 5/7, never in Run 8). These differences matter -- they reshape oil prices, Sarmatia's economic trajectory, and the overall narrative arc. But the superpower trajectories remain too similar: Columbia always ends between 358-372 GDP, Cathay between 299-371. The variance is concentrated in the middle powers and regional conflicts, not in the core Thucydides dynamic. The real differentiator is Cathay's growth rate seed, which ranges from 5.08% to 12.56% at Round 8 -- too wide a spread that should be dampened by economic maturation effects.

**Are the structural clocks working?** Yes, unambiguously. The GDP convergence functions as designed: Cathay closes the gap in every run, driven by base growth differential (4.5% vs. 2.3%) amplified by the AI tech multiplier. The Eastern Europe war produces cumulative degradation on both sides: Sarmatia stability from 5.0 to 1.7-2.19, Ruthenia to 1.0 in every run by Round 4-5. War tiredness accumulates consistently (Sarmatia 8.45, Ruthenia 10.13 in all runs). Persia's nuclear progress reaches L1 in Round 1 and stays there (no L2 push). These clocks create a sense of inevitability that is appropriate for a structural simulation.

**Does the dramatic arc emerge naturally?** It does in the middle -- the oil shocks, combat reversals, and covert warfare escalation in Rounds 3-6 create genuine tension. But the beginning (identical opening positions and Round 1 results across most runs) and ending (no resolution mechanisms, no climactic events) feel flat. The simulation needs triggerable crisis events in the later rounds: a Formosa blockade when Cathay hits 0.9+ gap ratio, a ceasefire negotiation when Sarmatia drops below 2.0 stability, a Persian nuclear test when Levantia's covert campaign intensifies. Without these, the final rounds become repetitive stat-grinding rather than dramatic storytelling.

**What's too deterministic vs. what has healthy variance?** Too deterministic: Columbia's trajectory (always rises to ~9.0 stability, ~87% support, 63 military units), the NATO alliance assessment (always HOLDING, always Phrygia as weakest link), Persia's nuclear level (always L1, never L2), Ruthenia's collapse (always to 1.0/0% by Round 4-5), Bharata's support ceiling (always 100%). Healthy variance: combat outcomes (zone capture timing ranges from Round 2 to Never), Hormuz blockade timing (Round 3 to Never), Cathay's growth trajectory (5% to 12%), Sarmatia's final stability range (1.70 to 2.19). The simulation needs more variance in the deterministic-today elements and more dampening on the already-variable growth rates.

The bottom line: V2 produces credible geopolitical dynamics over 8 rounds. The structural architecture works. What's missing is crisis mechanics -- the escalation triggers, resolution pathways, and dramatic climaxes that turn a stat simulation into a story generator.

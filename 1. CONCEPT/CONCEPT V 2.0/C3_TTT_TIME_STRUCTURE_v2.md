# Thucydides Trap SIM — Time Structure
**Version:** 1.1 | **Date:** 2026-03-21 | **Status:** Aligned with E1 Engine Architecture v0.7

---

## Core Parameters

**Rounds:** 6 to 8, depending on scenario design, participant engagement, and facilitator judgment. The exact number can be determined per session or even during play — the moderator can compress or extend based on the dynamics in the room.

**Time per round:** Each round represents **one half-year** (6 months) of scenario time. This gives each decision maximum weight — 6 months of consequences before the next chance to adjust — while covering a meaningful geopolitical horizon.

**Scenario span:** 3 to 4 years (H2 2026 to H1 2029 or H1 2030), depending on round count. 3 years is the sweet spot: enough for structural shifts, not so much that the scenario becomes speculative fiction.

**Delivery format:** Designed to work in both **single-day** (~11-13 hours) and **two-day** (~7 + 7 hours) formats. The two-day format is the premium experience; the single-day format is the compressed alternative.

---

## Design Principles

**Variable round length.** Not all rounds are equal. Early rounds are longer (world is complex, relationships forming, strategies developing). Later rounds compress (crisis mode, decisions must be faster, the Trap is closing). The compression is itself a teaching tool — participants experience how real crises accelerate beyond leaders' ability to deliberate. Exact length setting (before start) and the actual length should be adjustable for the moderator. 

**The moderator can flex.** If negotiations are reaching a critical breakthrough, the moderator can extend a round by 10-15 minutes. If a round feels exhausted, the moderator can call submissions early. The schedule is a framework, not a prison. The SIM serves the learning, not the clock.

**Scenario time can flex too.** If the game reaches a natural resolution point at round 6 (peace deal signed, or total war erupted), the moderator can end the SIM there. If tensions are building toward a climax, rounds 7-8 extend the scenario. The "6-8 rounds" range is deliberate — it lets the facilitator match the SIM's rhythm to what's actually happening in the room.

**What happens between rounds is as important as what happens during them.** In the two-day format, the overnight is not downtime — it's when secret alliances form, betrayals are planned, and leaders process the consequences of their decisions. The SIM stays "on" overnight: participants remain in role, informal conversations are encouraged, and anything discussed over dinner is as binding as anything said in a formal meeting.

## Round Structure (each round)

Every round has three phases plus a deployment step:

### Phase A 
— Free action and negotiation
The heart of the SIM. Bilateral meetings, alliance caucuses, back-channel deals, crisis response, public statements, organization sessions. This is where 80% of the learning happens. **60-80 minutes** depending on the round.

**Two real-time engines operate continuously during Phase A:**
- **Transaction (Market) Engine** handles all bilateral transfers (coins, arms, tech, treaties, basing rights, org creation) — instant on confirmation from both parties.
- **Live Action Engine** handles all unilateral actions requiring calculation (attacks, blockades, strikes, covert ops, arrests, assassinations, coups, propaganda) — instant resolution with visual dice/fortune wheel mechanic.

These real-time actions create drama DURING the round. An attack on the map, a strategic missile alert, a coup attempt — participants see results and react immediately. The round is not static negotiation; the world changes while people talk.

Scheduled organization meetings (NATO, BRICS+, etc.) happen WITHIN this phase — not as separate events. A NATO session might take 15-20 minutes of the round's negotiation time, leaving the rest for bilateral work. Any member can call a meeting of any organization they belong to.

— Decision submission (routine settings — deadline: end of round)
Budget allocations, tariff levels, sanctions positions, OPEC+ production, export restrictions, mobilization orders. Submitted via the web app. **Hard deadline: end of round.** If not submitted, defaults apply (previous round's settings continue). This deadline is non-negotiable — it teaches that governance has deadlines and procrastination is a choice with consequences.

Real-time actions (transactions, combat, covert ops) can execute ANYTIME during Phase A — no deadline. Only routine submitted settings have the end-of-round deadline.

### Phase B — World update
**World Model Engine** processes all inputs from the round: submitted settings, combat outcomes, transaction records, covert ops results, domestic action consequences. Calculates new economic state (GDP, inflation, oil price, financial indexes), political state (stability, support, Dem/Rep split), revenue and military production, tech advancement. Generates narratives. Moderator reviews and approves. New state of the world announced. **5-12 minutes** depending on how much happened. Richer updates at key moments (mid-term results, theater activations, tech breakthroughs).

### Phase C — Deployment window (5 minutes)
After world update, participants with military authority (military chief / head of state) deploy newly available units: newly produced, newly mobilized, units arrived from previous round's transit. Domestic deployment = immediate. Long-distance redeployment to foreign theaters = 1 round transit (submitted now, arrives after next round's processing).

**After deployment window: next round begins.**

## The Dramatic Arc

Regardless of round count, the SIM follows a **three-act structure**:

### Act 1 — Setup (Rounds 1-2)
Establish the world. Form alliances. Set initial policies — sanctions, tariffs, military posture, tech investment. First consequences visible. Organization meetings establish institutional positions. Players are learning the system and each other.

**Columbia mid-term elections** fall inside Act 1 (H2 2026, Round 2). This is the first major political test — a checkpoint that signals whether the current Columbia leadership has domestic support for its foreign policy. The result shapes Act 2 but doesn't transform it.

**Pacing:** Longer rounds (75-80 min negotiation). Participants need time to orient, read the board, and make first contact with key counterparts.

### Act 2 — Escalation (Rounds 3-4)
Consequences accumulate. Crises emerge or intensify. Major decisions are forced: BRICS currency union vote, possible theater activation, tech breakthrough attempts, peace negotiations or military escalation. The Trap begins to close — participants feel the options narrowing.

**UNGA vote** happens here — forcing every country to publicly declare positions. Swing states can no longer hedge. The vote tally reshapes the narrative.

**Pacing:** Moderate rounds (60-70 min). Participants know the system now. Conversations are more focused, decisions more deliberate. The world is moving faster.

### Act 3 — Climax and Resolution (Rounds 5-6, optionally 7-8)
Maximum pressure. The presidential election at **Round 5 (H1 2028)** is the structural climax. Either continuity or revolution — and everyone must deal with the consequences.

If the SIM runs to 7-8 rounds, the additional rounds extend into H1-H2 2029, playing out longer-term consequences: does the new (or re-elected) president's strategy work? Does the BRICS currency succeed? Does the Taiwan situation resolve or explode? These rounds are the fastest (45-55 min) and most intense.

**Pacing:** Compressed rounds. Less time to think. More pressure to act. This is where the Trap springs — or where extraordinary leadership averts it.

## Two-Day Format (recommended)

The premium experience. Full immersion with overnight continuity.

### Day 1

| Time | Duration | Activity |
|------|----------|----------|
| 9:00 - 9:45 | 45 min | Check-in. World briefing, rules, role distribution |
| 9:45 - 10:15 | 30 min | Read briefs, set personal goals, prepare |
| 10:15 - 11:45 | 90 min | **Round 1** (80 min action + 10 min submit) |
| 11:45 - 12:00 | 15 min | World update R1 |
| 12:00 - 13:25 | 85 min | **Round 2** (75 min action + 10 min submit) |
| 13:25 - 13:40 | 15 min | World update R2 (includes Columbia mid-term results if applicable) |
| 13:40 - 14:30 | 50 min | Lunch (stay in role — the lunch table is a negotiation venue) |
| 14:30 - 15:50 | 80 min | **Round 3** (65 min action + 10 min submit + UNGA vote event) |
| 15:50 - 16:05 | 15 min | World update R3 |
| 16:05 - 16:20 | 15 min | Break |
| 16:20 - 17:35 | 75 min | **Round 4** (60 min action + 10 min submit) |
| 17:35 - 17:50 | 15 min | Extended world update R4 — "state of the world at the halfway point" |
| 17:50 - 18:00 | 10 min | Moderator: press conference / recap / set up overnight dynamics |
| 18:00 | | **SIM PAUSES — stay in role until tomorrow** |
| Evening | | Dinner, informal meetings, secret negotiations, overnight scheming. Anything said is in-game. The press is watching. |

**Day 1: Rounds 1-4 completed. ~8.5 hours including briefing. Comfortable pace.**

### Overnight

The SIM does not stop. Participants remain in role. Informal conversations at dinner, at the hotel bar, via messages — all are in-game. This is where:
- Secret deals are struck away from the main room's visibility
- Coup conspiracies form (autocratic countries)
- Opposition candidates plan their challenge
- Back-channel peace negotiations happen between adversaries
- The press investigates and prepares stories
- Exhausted leaders make promises they might regret in the morning

The overnight is not just color — it's structurally important. It gives participants time to **process** the first four rounds, **reconsider** their strategy, and **prepare** for the climactic Act 3. In one-day formats, this processing time doesn't exist, and participants enter the final rounds running on adrenaline alone.

| Time | Duration | Activity |
|------|----------|----------|

| 11:35 - 11:50 | 15 min | World update R6 |

**Day 2: Rounds 5-6 (minimum) or 5-8 (maximum). 5-7 hours depending on round count.**

**Total two-day program: ~14-16 hours of active SIM + 1-2 hours briefing/debrief + overnight immersion.**

## Single-Day Format (compressed alternative)

For situations where two days isn't feasible. Fits 6 rounds in ~11 hours.

| Time | Duration | Activity |
|------|----------|----------|
| 8:30 - 9:00 | 30 min | Check-in |
| 9:00 - 9:45 | 45 min | World briefing, rules, role distribution, Q&A |
| 9:45 - 10:05 | 20 min | Read briefs, set personal goals |
| 10:05 - 11:30 | 85 min | **Round 1** (75 min + 10 min submit) |
| 11:30 - 11:40 | 10 min | World update R1 |
| 11:40 - 12:55 | 75 min | **Round 2** (65 min + 10 min submit) |
| 12:55 - 13:05 | 10 min | World update R2 |
| 13:05 - 13:50 | 45 min | Lunch (in role) |
| 13:50 - 15:00 | 70 min | **Round 3** (60 min + 10 min submit) |
| 15:00 - 15:10 | 10 min | World update R3 |
| 15:10 - 16:15 | 65 min | **Round 4** (55 min + 10 min submit) |
| 16:15 - 16:30 | 15 min | Extended world update R4 + break |
| 16:30 - 17:30 | 60 min | **Round 5** (50 min + 10 min submit + presidential election) |
| 17:30 - 17:45 | 15 min | World update R5 (election results) |
| 17:45 - 18:35 | 50 min | **Round 6 — FINAL** (45 min + 5 min submit) |
| 18:35 - 18:50 | 15 min | Final state of the world. SIM ENDS. |
| 18:50 - 19:05 | 15 min | Decompress |
| 19:05 - 19:50 | 45 min | Moderated debrief |
| 19:50 | | End (~11.5 hours total) |

**What the single-day format loses:** The overnight (no secret scheming, no processing time, no informal deal-making over dinner). The possibility of rounds 7-8 (must end at 6). Some negotiation depth (rounds are slightly shorter). Participant energy in the final rounds (mental exhaustion without overnight recovery).

**What it preserves:** The full dramatic arc including presidential election. All six rounds with meaningful negotiation time. The round compression effect. All domain mechanics.

## Scheduled Events Across the Arc

These events are anchored to scenario time, not round number — so they occur at the same moment in the story regardless of whether we play 6, 7, or 8 rounds.

| Scenario time | Event | Notes |
|---------------|-------|-------|
| H1 2026 (Round 1) | NATO summit. BRICS+ opening session. Opening org meetings. | Establish institutional positions |
| H2 2026 (Round 2) | Columbia mid-term elections. OPEC+ meeting. G7 coordination. | Act 1 climax. Political checkpoint. |
| H1 2027 (Round 3) | UNGA vote on major issue. BRICS+ summit (currency union?). First tech breakthrough attempts if thresholds met. | Forcing function — every country declares position publicly. |
| H2 2027 (Round 4) | NATO crisis session (if theater activated). EU emergency coordination. Pre-election maneuvering begins in Columbia. | Peak tension. Halfway point. |
| H1 2028 (Round 5) | **Columbia presidential election.** Possible peace conference. OPEC+ reassessment. | **THE climactic event.** Everything changes or doesn't. |
| H2 2028 (Round 6) | New/re-elected Columbia president's first moves. Final state of alliances, wars, economies. | Resolution or continuation. |
| H1 2029 (Round 7, if played) | Extended consequences. Does the new order hold? | Post-climax adjustment. |
| H2 2029 (Round 8, if played) | Final final round. Long-term trajectories established. | Epilogue. |

## The Overnight (Two-Day Format) — Design Notes

The overnight is a designed feature, not dead time. Specific mechanics that operate overnight:

**Secret communications.** Players can message each other (via whatever channel the SIM uses — Telegram, the web app, physical notes slipped under doors). All communications remain in-game.

**Press activity.** The press role can publish an "evening edition" or "morning briefing" — summarizing Day 1 events, breaking stories, publishing leaked information. This shapes Day 2's opening dynamic.

**Moderator overnight events.** The moderator can introduce events that "happened overnight" — a surprise military action, an assassination, a market crash, a leaked document. These create the "wake up to a changed world" experience that real leaders face regularly.

**Coup/conspiracy window.** The overnight is the natural time for coup plotters to coordinate, for opposition figures to recruit allies, for secret peace negotiations to happen. The moderator should encourage this explicitly: "The SIM continues overnight. Your room is your territory. Your phone is your secure line. Use both."

**Morning check-in.** Day 2 begins with 15 minutes where the moderator collects and announces overnight developments: deals struck, action cards played, events triggered. This sets the stage for Act 3.

## Round Count Decision Framework

The moderator decides the final round count based on:

| Condition | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Natural resolution reached (peace deal, decisive war outcome) | End at current round or play one more for consequences |
| Tensions building toward climax | Continue to next round — don't cut short building momentum |
| Participants visibly exhausted / disengaging | End at next natural break point |
| Presidential election hasn't happened yet | Must reach Round 5 minimum |
| Two-day format with engaged participants | Play 7-8 rounds — use the time |
| Single-day format | 6 rounds maximum — budget doesn't allow more |
| First-time facilitation team | 6 rounds — simpler to manage, learn the system |

## Connections to Game Domains

**Military:** Half-year rounds mean military campaigns resolve within a single round. An offensive launched at the start of H1 2027 has 6 months to produce results. This is realistic — most modern military campaigns have decisive phases lasting 3-6 months. Redeployment between theaters still takes one round (6 months), which is also realistic for major force movements.

**Economy:** Sanctions and tariffs bite harder per round — 6 months of cumulative impact per step. Budget cycles are semi-annual. Oil pricing decisions cover half a year. This means each economic decision is weightier and harder to reverse than in quarterly rounds.

**Domestic Politics:** Stability Index and political support evolve meaningfully in 6-month steps. War fatigue accumulates faster per round. Elections and political crises can develop within a single round's scenario time. The Columbia presidential election falls naturally at Round 5.

**Technology:** R&D investment per round represents 6 months of research. Fewer investment rounds but each larger. Breakthrough attempts at Round 3+ are plausible in scenario time (1+ years of cumulative investment).

## Open Questions

1. **Exact round lengths** — Should be adjustable for moderator. The variable structure (80 min → 45 min) needs playtesting.
2. **Mid-round events** — RESOLVED: No AI-generated events during rounds. All crises come from participant actions via real-time engines. World state updates happen ONLY between rounds.
3. **Overnight mechanics** — how much structure to impose on the overnight in the two-day format? Need to define minimum expectations.
4. **Round 7-8 trigger criteria** — what specifically makes the moderator decide to continue beyond 6 rounds?
5. **Break timing** — may need 10-minute breaks between some rounds for physical comfort.

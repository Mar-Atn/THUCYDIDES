## Organizations

### Standing Organizations (formal meetings, collective decisions)

**1. Western Treaty (NATO)**
Members: Columbia, Albion, Teutonia, Gallia, Freeland, Ponte, Phrygia. Heartland aspires. Yamato as Pacific partner.
Decision: Consensus — any member blocks.
Dilemma: Does Article 5 hold? Does Columbia stay committed? Can the alliance survive a transactional hegemon?

**2. The Union (EU)**
Members: Gallia, Teutonia, Freeland, Ponte. (+ Spain in full version.) Albion is outside. Heartland aspires. **Commissioner** chairs meetings and represents the EU externally on trade and institutional matters.
Decision: Consensus among member states — any member blocks. Commissioner cannot vote but sets the agenda, proposes compromises, and represents the EU in external negotiations (trade deals, sanctions coordination). Commissioner's institutional power: can call emergency EU sessions, control meeting agendas, and publicly frame issues — shaping what gets discussed and how.
Dilemma: Can 4-5 sovereign nations with different economic exposures agree on sanctions, trade, enlargement, and defense? Ponte as swing vote that can break consensus. The Commissioner wants EU unity (without it, the institution is irrelevant) but also resists any single member dominating — especially Gallia or Teutonia. The Commissioner's nightmare: member states bypassing the EU to cut bilateral deals.

**3. The Council (UN Security Council)**
P5 permanent with veto: Columbia, Cathay, Nordostan, Gallia, Albion. Rotating: Bharata, Amazonia, others.
Decision: P5 veto — any permanent member blocks.
Dilemma: Structurally paralyzed on the issues that matter most. Using the veto is costless legally but expensive diplomatically.

**4. The League (BRICS+)**
Members: Cathay, Nordostan, Bharata, Amazonia, Solaria, Persia, Mirage. Phrygia as observer.
Decision: Loose consensus — no binding commitments.
Dilemma: Can this group agree on anything concrete (currency union, payment system) or is it just shared resentment of Western dominance?

**5. The Cartel (OPEC+)**
Members: Solaria, Nordostan, Persia, Mirage.
Decision: Each independently sets production. Coordination voluntary.
Dilemma: Classic prisoner's dilemma. Cooperate on production limits or defect for market share?

### Coordination Forums

**6. The Seven (G7)**
Members: Columbia, Albion, Teutonia, Gallia, Yamato. (+ Canada, Italy abstracted or Ponte if playing.)
Decision: Non-binding joint statements.
Dilemma: Do Western allies follow Columbia's lead on tech restrictions and sanctions even when it costs them?

**7. Columbia Parliament**
Members: 5 voting seats drawn from the Columbia team (see Roles document).
Decision: Majority (3 of 5). Budget requires parliamentary approval. Treaties may require ratification.
Dilemma: A uniquely INTERNAL organization — but functions like a collective decision body. Hostile Congress after mid-terms can block the president's budget, launch investigations, and constrain foreign policy.

### Event-Driven

**8. The Assembly (UN General Assembly)**
Every country votes. No veto. No enforcement. Moral weight.
Scheduled 1-2 times. Forces every country to publicly declare positions. Swing states can no longer hedge.


### Player-Created Organizations

Participants may CREATE new organizations during the SIM — alliances, trade blocs, security pacts, currency unions. Any group of countries can declare a new organization, define its membership and decision rules, and announce it publicly. The AI engine recognizes new organizations and tracks their decisions. This is deliberately open-ended — the most consequential organization in the SIM might be one that doesn't exist at game start.

## Internal Dynamics — Key Clusters

### Columbia (United States) — Open Democratic Dysfunction

Columbia's internal tension is the most complex and most visible. A transactional president who sees allies as freeloaders confronts an institutional establishment that sees alliances as the foundation of American power. Congress can block the budget. The CIA controls information. A tech tycoon has unaccountable influence. And two presidential envoys operate parallel diplomatic channels that may contradict official policy.

The political landscape splits roughly into three camps: the president's loyalists (transactional, America First, deal-oriented), the opposition (internationalist, alliance-restoring, rule-based), and a genuine middle (security professionals, business pragmatists, institutional figures) whose alignment shifts based on how the SIM unfolds. No permanent majority exists on any issue. Every policy decision creates a different coalition.

Two elections punctuate the SIM: mid-terms (Round 2) that determine whether Congress is friendly or hostile, and a presidential election (Round 5) where a human Challenger campaigns against the incumbent. Foreign leaders must hedge between two possible futures — the current president who might not survive, and the Challenger who makes alternative promises.

| Issue | President's camp | Opposition camp | Swing players |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| Alliances | Reduce burden, transactional | Restore and strengthen | Depends on threat assessment |
| Cathay | Deal if possible | Contain multilaterally | Depends on business exposure |
| Heartland war | Negotiate, cut costs | Support to resolution | Depends on military assessment |
| Domestic spending | Cut government | Invest at home | Depends on economic conditions |
| Tech/AI | Market leads | Regulate for safety | Depends on industry interests |

### Cathay (China) — Hidden Authoritarian Tension

Cathay presents unity to the outside world. Inside, five political forces disagree profoundly about timing, method, and risk — but express disagreement through slow-walking, biased advice, and selective information rather than open debate. The outside world can never tell whether Cathay is unified or fracturing — and that ambiguity is itself a form of power and danger.

The Chairman has concentrated power by dismantling collective leadership and purging rival factions. But the purges have thinned the loyalty base, and the economic slowdown is undermining the legitimacy model. The military has been purged yet asked to prepare for the most consequential operation in CCP history. The Premier sees economic data showing that confrontation leads to catastrophe. The tech mogul has global connections the state needs but can't fully control. And a party elder represents the suppressed tradition of collective leadership — the succession mechanism that exists only if the Chairman weakens.

These tensions are mechanically enforced: information dependency (Helmsman sees only what Abacus and Rampart share), implementation friction (orders without co-signatures risk delay or error), purge costs (-10 support, -1 stability), and Sage's emergency convening power. See B2 for full specification.

| Issue | Helmsman (Chairman) | Rampart (Military) | Abacus (Premier) | Circuit (Tech mogul) | Sage (Elder) |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Formosa | Personal legacy — must resolve | When ready, professionally | Opposed — economic catastrophe | Terrified — supply chains destroyed | Reckless unless perfect conditions |
| Economy | Self-reliance, ideology | Whatever funds military | Reform, openness, pragmatism | Market freedom, global integration | Pragmatic — Deng's way worked |
| West | Structural confrontation | Deterrence, not provocation | Engage — trade is survival | Essential — business depends on it | Strategic patience |
| Leadership | Personal control | Military autonomy | Collective leadership | Leave business alone | The party over any one man |

### Europe — Collective Action Failure Between Sovereigns

Europe's dysfunction is not within a single government but between sovereign nations that share institutions but not interests. Four (or five) EU members must achieve consensus on sanctions, trade, and enlargement — and any one can block. The UK participates in NATO but not EU decisions, creating a structural mismatch between military and economic coordination.

The fault lines: France pushes for European strategic autonomy ("we can't depend on Columbia forever"). Germany trades massively with Cathay and feels every sanction cost disproportionately. Poland demands maximum NATO commitment and maximum pressure on Nordostan. Italy — the new addition — is the EU's weak link: massive debt, populist politics, historical Russia sympathy, and Belt-and-Road curiosity. Italy's vote can break EU consensus on any issue.

Each European player simultaneously manages three games: the EU negotiation (can we agree?), the NATO dynamic (does Columbia still care?), and their own national survival (elections, economy, stability). What's good for the EU may be bad for their domestic politics. This triple burden makes European roles rich despite being single-player.

### Nordostan (Russia) — Paranoid Personal Autocracy

Power concentrated through personal relationships, not institutions. The president controls everything through a web of mutual dependency and mutual suspicion. Unlike Cathay (where the system would continue differently without the leader), Nordostan without its president is an open question — succession crisis, power vacuum, possible collapse.

Three (or four) players represent three directions: continue the war and outlast the West (Pathfinder), produce a military result or honestly assess that one is impossible (Ironhand), and pursue economic survival through deals and back-channels (Compass). The coup mechanic is always latent — the General and the economic figure can move against the President if conditions align. The President can arrest anyone. Trust is the currency; suspicion is the tax.

The war must produce something the President can call victory — not because the nation requires it, but because the leader's personal survival depends on it. A peace deal that looks like retreat could be fatal. This personal trap makes Nordostan's decisions more dangerous than purely strategic calculation would produce.

### Heartland (Ukraine) — Three Futures, Wartime Election

Three players embody three possible futures for a nation at war. The President (resistance/continuity) has held the country together but is exhausted and losing popularity. The General (military/fight harder) is more popular and represents the belief that better leadership could produce better outcomes. The Politician (pragmatic peace) is controversial but increasingly relevant as war fatigue deepens.

All three are active from Round 1 — governing, shadow-diplomating, and back-channel negotiating respectively. Foreign leaders can meet all three, pre-negotiating with the potential next president. At Round 3-4, a wartime election occurs — the AI judges based on actual gameplay (territory, economy, casualties, Western support). The winner takes the helm; losers remain as opposition. The cruelest dynamic: the leader who signs a peace deal may be the leader the country punishes.

### Persia (Iran) — Three Directions at the Nuclear Threshold

Three players represent Iran's genuine internal factions: the theocratic hardline (Supreme Leader equivalent — nuclear weapons as destiny, resistance to the West, proxy network as power), the military-pragmatic IRGC (nuclear capability as leverage not ideology, business empire to protect, will deal if profitable), and the reformist/technocratic camp (nuclear program as bargaining chip, engagement with the West, representing the young population that wants a normal country).

The nuclear question becomes three-dimensional: pursue the bomb (ideology), maintain threshold capability as leverage (pragmatism), or trade the program for economic opening (reform). If Levantia strikes, three completely different responses. If the Supreme Leader weakens (economic crisis, protests), the IRGC becomes the kingmaker — choosing between hardline continuity and pragmatic transition. The reformist represents the 60% of Iran's population under 30 who weren't alive for the revolution.

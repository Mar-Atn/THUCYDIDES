# Thucydides Trap SIM — Country & Actor Structure
**Version:** 1.1 | **Date:** 2026-03-19 | **Status:** Working draft

---

## Design Principle

This structure models the essential dynamics of the current global power transition. The selection criterion for every actor: **does this nation reproduce a dynamic that cannot be reproduced by any other actor on the board?** No actor is included for completeness — each must serve at least one structural function that would be lost without it.

The model is a globe, not a photograph. It captures the shape of the world's power dynamics with the minimum set of actors needed to make the system recognizable, playable, and rich in genuine dilemmas.

---

## Structural Center

The US-China power transition is the system, not one subplot within it. Every other actor relates to this central axis — as ally, rival, swing state, or flashpoint. The SIM succeeds when participants feel the Trap's mechanism from the inside: how rational actors pursuing their own interests collectively generate outcomes none of them intended.

## Organizations & Alliances

These are collective decision-making bodies that create the **dual loyalty dynamic** — national interest vs. collective interest. Each organization earns its place by forcing members to negotiate internally, producing decisions that affect non-members, and creating dilemmas that wouldn't exist without the institution.

Overlapping memberships are a feature, not a bug. Turkey is in NATO but flirts with BRICS+. Saudi Arabia is in OPEC+ with Russia but coordinates with G7 on some issues. India is in BRICS+ but partners with the US bilaterally. Every overlap creates a torn loyalty — and torn loyalties are where the richest gameplay lives.

Organizations have **scheduled meetings** at specific points in the SIM day (not every round). The meeting schedule creates the SIM's rhythm and forces encounters between countries that might otherwise avoid each other.

### Full Organizations (formal meetings, collective decisions, membership matters)

**1. NATO — Western military alliance**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | USA, UK, Germany, France, Poland, Turkey (+ others abstracted). Ukraine aspires to join. |
| **Decision mechanic** | Consensus — any member can block. This is NATO's real-world curse: one dissenter paralyzes the alliance. |
| **Key decisions** | Collective defense commitment (Article 5 — does it hold?). Military burden-sharing (who contributes how many units to which theater). Sanctions coordination. Expansion (Ukraine membership question). Nuclear sharing policy. Force posture in Europe and Pacific. |
| **Core dilemma** | Article 5 is the single most consequential commitment in the SIM. If Russia attacks Poland, does the US really go to war? If China blockades Taiwan, does NATO activate (Taiwan isn't a member)? The alliance's credibility is constantly tested without being formally invoked — and that ambiguity is what deters OR emboldens adversaries. |
| **Scheduled meetings** | Round 1 (opening posture). Mid-game (crisis response if escalation occurs). Can be called in emergency by any member. |

**2. UN Security Council — great-power confrontation forum**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | P5 permanent with veto: USA, China, Russia, UK, France. Rotating seats: India, Brazil, others as relevant. |
| **Decision mechanic** | P5 veto — any permanent member blocks. Resolutions require majority plus no P5 veto. |
| **Key decisions** | Resolutions condemning aggression (symbolic but politically consequential). Sanctions authorization (gives legitimacy to unilateral sanctions). Peacekeeping mandates. Arms embargoes. UNSC reform (India and Brazil want permanent seats — this side-negotiation gives them leverage). |
| **Core dilemma** | The UNSC is where the rules-based order is either upheld or exposed as fiction. Russia vetoes anything about its war. China vetoes anything about Taiwan. The structural paralysis on the issues that matter most demonstrates to every participant that the international order has no enforcement when great powers disagree. Using the veto is costless legally but expensive diplomatically. |
| **Scheduled meetings** | Not scheduled — called in emergency session by any P5 member. Any crisis can trigger one. The drama of calling an emergency session is itself a political act. |

**3. EU — European economic and political union**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | Germany, France, Poland (+ others abstracted). UK is outside (Brexit) but partner/observer. Ukraine aspires to join. |
| **Decision mechanic** | Consensus among members on major decisions. This means any EU member can block collective sanctions, trade policy, or enlargement. |
| **Key decisions** | Collective sanctions (EU is the world's largest single market — EU sanctions carry enormous weight). Defense spending coordination and joint procurement. Trade policy (EU tariffs are collective, not national — a single EU tariff wall). Ukraine membership question (accession = massive long-term commitment). Energy policy. The existential question: does the EU move toward military/political union, or fragment? |
| **Core dilemma** | Do EU members sacrifice national economic interest for collective security? Germany's China trade exposure vs. collective tech restrictions. Poland's demands for maximum NATO commitment vs. France's push for strategic autonomy. The EU and NATO overlap but aren't identical — Turkey is in NATO but not EU, UK is in NATO but not EU — creating situations where one organization agrees but the other can't. |
| **Scheduled meetings** | 1-2 times during the SIM. Can be called by any member for crisis coordination. |

**4. BRICS+ — non-Western coordination bloc**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | China, Russia, India, Brazil, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran, South Africa. Turkey as observer. Others may apply to join. |
| **Decision mechanic** | Loose consensus — no binding commitments. Members can free-ride, defect, or stall. Reflects BRICS' real weakness: lots of ambition, weak coordination. |
| **Key decisions** | Currency union / alternative payment system (the highest-stakes collective economic decision in the SIM — would fundamentally shift global financial architecture). Joint development bank investments. Coordinated sanctions resistance. Collective trade agreements. Expansion (who else joins?). |
| **Core dilemma** | Members share resentment of Western dominance but have wildly divergent interests. China wants to lead. Russia wants anti-Western solidarity. India wants independence from BOTH great powers. Saudi Arabia wants leverage. Brazil wants development without strings. Can this group agree on anything concrete — especially the currency union — or is it just a talking shop? |
| **Scheduled meetings** | Summit once during the SIM (major event — leaders gather, speeches, joint statement negotiations). Working sessions on currency union possible. |

**5. OPEC+ — oil production cartel**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | Saudi Arabia, UAE, Russia, Iran (+ others abstracted). |
| **Decision mechanic** | Each member independently sets production level. Coordination is voluntary — no enforcement mechanism. Cheating is tempting and common. |
| **Key decisions** | Production levels each round (low / normal / high). Price as geopolitical weapon — cut production to punish oil-importing adversaries, flood market to hurt rival producers. Coordination on production cuts to maintain price. |
| **Core dilemma** | Classic prisoner's dilemma: cooperate (restrict production, price stays high, everyone profits) or defect (overproduce, grab market share, crash price for all). Made richer by geopolitics: Saudi Arabia and Russia must cooperate on oil while competing on everything else. Iran wants sanctions lifted to sell more. The US wants low prices to hurt Russia but high enough to sustain its own shale industry. |
| **Scheduled meetings** | Coordination happens each round as part of budget/production decisions. Formal OPEC+ meeting once or twice during SIM. |

### Coordination Forum (non-binding, but joint statements carry weight)

**6. G7 — Western economic coordination**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Members** | USA, UK, Germany, France, Japan (+ Canada, Italy abstracted). |
| **Decision mechanic** | Coordination forum — no binding votes. Joint statements carry political weight. Public commitments constrain future action. |
| **Key decisions** | Sanctions coordination (broader than EU — includes USA and Japan). Tech export restriction alignment (Japan's chip equipment controls on China are a G7 issue, not NATO or EU). Economic policy coordination. Ukraine support packages. Frozen asset decisions. |
| **Core dilemma** | G7 fills a gap: NATO handles military, EU handles European economics, but nobody else coordinates Western economic warfare including the US and Japan. The dilemma: do allies follow the US lead even when it hurts their own economies? Japan restricting chip equipment sales to China costs billions — is solidarity worth the price? |
| **Scheduled meetings** | Once during SIM, possibly at a key decision point. Joint statement = public commitment. |

### Event-Driven (happens once or twice, not standing)

**7. UN General Assembly vote**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Participants** | Every country votes. No veto. No enforcement. |
| **Decision mechanic** | Simple majority. Every country casts one public vote (yes / no / abstain). |
| **Key decisions** | One or two major votes during the SIM on defining issues: condemning an aggression, recognizing a state, calling for a ceasefire. The specific topic is set by the moderator based on game events. |
| **Core dilemma** | The result has no legal force but enormous political weight — it shows who stands where. Every country's vote is public. Abstention is itself a statement. The vote tally shapes the narrative: "143 countries condemned the invasion" or "the world is split 60-40" tells a different story. For swing states, the UNGA vote is the moment they must publicly declare their position — no more hedging. |
| **Scheduled** | Once during the SIM, timed for maximum dramatic impact (after a major escalation). Possibly a second vote near game end to show how positions shifted. |

### Framework Reference (not a meeting — rules the AI engine applies)

**NPT / IAEA — nuclear non-proliferation framework**

Not a formal organization in the SIM, but a framework that triggers consequences when nuclear actions occur. Iran's nuclear test = NPT violation → UNSC emergency session automatically triggered → sanctions authorization vote. IAEA inspection can be requested as an action card — generates information about a country's nuclear program status. The framework gives nuclear proliferation its legal and diplomatic dimension without adding another meeting to the schedule.

## Design Notes

**Proportionality rule.** No single regional conflict should consume more than ~15% of actor weight. The US-China axis is the system (~30%). Europe, Middle East, and Pacific are theaters of roughly equal importance (~15% each). Swing states are collectively decisive (~15%). This ensures participants experience the "too many fires, not enough firefighters" dynamic.

**Fictional names.** Decided: transparent fiction approach. Invented country names on recognizable geography. See SIM_WORLD_BUILDING.md for naming principles and working proposals.

**Time setting.** To be decided. Options: present day (early 2026), near future (2027 — allows fictional setup events), or an abstracted "now" with real dynamics but no specific date anchor.

**AI vs. human assignment.** Tier 1 countries are human-first. Tier 2 countries flex between human and AI depending on participant count. The world model AI handles background events, economic calculations, and consequences.

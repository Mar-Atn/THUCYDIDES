## 1. PUTIN: AGE, HEALTH, SUCCESSION

**Age:** 73 (born October 1952). Constitutionally eligible to remain president until 2036 after the 2020 constitutional reset.

**Health:** Persistent rumors of Parkinson's, cancer, and use of body doubles remain unconfirmed. No verified independent medical assessment exists. Putin maintains a visible public schedule. The intelligence community consensus is: declining but functional. Not imminent collapse, but the actuarial clock matters.

**Succession:** The defining structural problem. Putin has NOT groomed a public successor — doing so would erode his own authority and create a target for competing factions. The 2026 State Duma elections (scheduled for September) are being positioned as a transition mechanism: nearly three-quarters of mandates expected to be filled by newcomers, a significant share of them war veterans. This is elite renewal, not succession planning.

**Named succession figures (all old):**
- Mikhail Mishustin (PM, 59) — technocratic manager, weakened by bureaucratic rivals
- Andrei Belousov (Defense Minister, 66) — economist turned defense chief, Putin loyalist
- Nikolai Patrushev (former Security Council Secretary, 74) — security hardliner, aging
- Sergei Sobyanin (Moscow Mayor, 67) — administrative competence, no military/security base
- Denis Manturov (First Deputy PM) — rising technocrat, promoted over Mishustin's head
- Kirill Dmitriev (RDIF head, Putin's daughters' circle) — the dark horse. Organized the Riyadh US-Russia talks (Feb 2025). Increasingly visible as diplomatic back-channel operator. Rumored to aim for Foreign Minister.

**Key dynamic:** No designated successor means any incapacitation or death triggers an immediate power vacuum. The security services (FSB), the military, the economic technocrats, and the oligarchic class would compete. The system is built around one person; without him, the architecture collapses.

## 2. UKRAINE WAR STATUS — TERRITORIAL PICTURE

**Duration:** 4 years as of February 24, 2026. The longest major European war since WWII.

**Overall territorial control:** Russia occupies approximately 20% of Ukraine's territory.

**Claimed vs. controlled territories (the critical gap):**

Russia formally annexed four oblasts in September 2022 (in addition to Crimea in 2014):
- **Donetsk Oblast:** Russia controls roughly 60-65% (Pokrovsk 67.63% captured as of Dec 2025, Toretsk fully captured, Siversk likely seized, but significant western portions remain Ukrainian)
- **Luhansk Oblast:** Russia controls ~90-95% (nearly complete, but small western pockets remain Ukrainian-held)
- **Zaporizhzhia Oblast:** Russia controls roughly 60-65% (southern portions, but Zaporizhzhia city — the oblast capital — remains firmly Ukrainian)
- **Kherson Oblast:** Russia controls roughly 70% (eastern/southern portions, but the western bank of the Dnipro including Kherson city was liberated in Nov 2022 and remains Ukrainian)
- **Crimea:** Fully controlled since 2014

**Russian military objectives:** Capture the remainder of Donetsk and Luhansk oblasts. Push westward into eastern Kharkiv Oblast. Encircle northern Donetsk Oblast. Possible advances into Dnipropetrovsk Oblast.

**Front line character:** Grinding attritional warfare. Small tactical advances measured in hundreds of meters to low single-digit kilometers per week. Enormous casualty rates on both sides. No major breakthroughs. The war of attrition favors whichever side can sustain losses longer.

**The territorial gap IS the war:** Putin cannot accept anything less than full control of the four claimed oblasts (his own annexation declaration demands it). But actually conquering the remaining 30-40% of these oblasts means fighting through heavily fortified Ukrainian positions, major cities (Zaporizhzhia, potentially Dnipro), and increasingly committed defenders. The gap between claim and control is the war's unresolvable core.

## 3. RUSSIAN ECONOMY UNDER SANCTIONS

**GDP:** Approximately $1.9-2.1 trillion (nominal). Growth slowing sharply: from ~3.6% in 2024 to an estimated 1.0% in 2026 (IMF forecast 0.6-1.0%). The economy is transitioning from "managed cooling" to outright stagnation.

**The resilience paradox:** Russia's economy proved surprisingly resilient through 2023-2024 due to massive wartime fiscal stimulus, energy revenue redirection to Asian markets, and import substitution. But by 2026, the stimulus effects are exhausted while the structural damage accumulates.

**Key indicators:**
- **Inflation:** Being brought down from double digits. Bank of Russia targeting 4.5-5.5% for 2026. Still elevated.
- **Key interest rate:** Cut to 15.0% (March 2026), down from peak of ~21% in late 2024. Seven consecutive cuts, but still extremely high by historical standards.
- **Ruble:** ~100 per dollar (Feb 2026). Nearly 19% devaluation since Aug 2025. Debates about whether the ruble is overvalued — further devaluation expected.
- **Defense spending:** 13.5 trillion rubles ($168.8B) in 2025, slightly reduced to 12.93 trillion rubles ($161.6B) in 2026. First reduction since the full-scale invasion. But actual spending (including classified items) likely higher. Defense + security = approximately 40% of federal budget.
- **Budget deficit:** 3.8 trillion rubles (1.6% of GDP) projected for 2026. Revenue: 40.3 trillion rubles. Expenditure: 44.1 trillion rubles.
- **Oil revenue:** Declining. Monthly fossil fuel export revenues hit lowest point since the full-scale invasion in January 2026 (EUR 464M/day). Revenue $3.6B below year-ago levels by November 2025.

**China trade dependency:** China accounts for ~52% of Russia's fossil fuel export revenues from top importers. China has become Russia's largest trading partner, representing approximately one-third of Russia's total foreign trade. This creates a structural dependency: Russia sells energy commodities at a discount to China and buys Chinese manufactured goods. The trade balance increasingly favors China.

**Sanctions evasion:** Functioning but under pressure. Parallel import schemes, third-country rerouting (Kazakhstan, Turkey, UAE), cryptocurrency, and Chinese payment systems. But secondary sanctions pressure is tightening — Western enforcement targeting the networks.

**Assessment:** The economy is not collapsing but is slowly eroding. The "fortress economy" narrative is propaganda — reality is stagnation, brain drain, falling investment, and increasing dependency on China as the buyer of last resort. The war economy cannibalizes civilian economic capacity. Every year of war makes the post-war economic recovery harder.

## 4. MILITARY SITUATION

**Personnel:**
- Claimed losses: Ukrainian sources report over 1 million Russian casualties (killed, wounded, missing) since February 2022, with ~400,000 killed in 2025 alone. Average daily KIA: 240 in 2024, 390 in 2025.
- Recruitment: 30,000-40,000 contract soldiers per month. Enough to cover current losses but not enough to significantly increase deployed forces.
- North Korean troops: Deployed to support Russian operations, particularly in Kursk Oblast. Estimated 10,000-12,000. High casualties reported.
- No general mobilization declared since partial mobilization (Sept 2022).

**Equipment:**
- Massive Soviet-era stockpile drawdowns. Tank losses estimated at 3,500+ since 2022.
- Domestic production scaled up (tanks, artillery, drones) but cannot match loss rates for advanced systems.
- Increasing reliance on Iranian drones (Shahed), North Korean artillery ammunition, and Chinese dual-use components.

**Military purges (2024-2025):** The FSB launched sweeping purges of military elites with Kremlin approval:
- Deputy Defense Minister Timur Ivanov — arrested April 2024 (bribery, close Shoigu associate)
- Lt. Gen. Vadim Shamarin — deputy chief of general staff, arrested May 2024 (bribery)
- At least a dozen additional senior military officials arrested on corruption charges
- The purges coincided with Shoigu's removal as Defense Minister and replacement by civilian economist Andrei Belousov
- Estimated corruption losses: $120B+
- Purpose: both genuine anti-corruption AND political control — pinning blame for early failures, removing potential rivals, demonstrating that no one is immune

**Navy — Black Sea Fleet:**
- Effectively neutralized. At least 25 ships destroyed (including flagship Moskva). Fleet relocated from Sevastopol to Novorossiysk. No ships sunk in 2025 (adaptation), but March 2026 drone strike on Novorossiysk was "the strongest strike" yet.
- Offensive capability limited to Kalibr cruise missile strikes. Fleet unable to project power in western Black Sea.
- The fleet is in a "death spiral" — unable to operate safely, unable to replace losses, increasingly irrelevant to the land war.

**Nuclear forces:**
- Total arsenal: 5,459 warheads (1,718 deployed strategic). World's largest.
- Strategic triad: ~333 ICBMs (1,254 warheads), 12 submarines with 192 SLBMs (992 warheads), strategic bombers (586 weapons).
- Tactical nuclear weapons: ~1,500 — world's largest tactical arsenal.
- Backbone: RS-24 Yars (210+ launchers, 4 MIRVed warheads each).
- New START expired February 5, 2026 — first time since early 1970s with no binding limits on US-Russian strategic forces. Combined with revised nuclear doctrine (lowered threshold for nuclear use) and forward deployment to Belarus.
- Nuclear posture: most coercive since the Cold War.

**Assessment:** The Russian military machine is "functioning decently" at current operational tempo — covering losses, maintaining the grind. But it has no capacity for major escalation without general mobilization (politically costly). Ground forces are large but degraded in quality. Strategic nuclear forces remain the crown jewel and primary deterrent. The navy is largely out of the war.

## 5. RUSSIAN ELITE DYNAMICS

**The power constellation around Putin:**

**Security/Siloviki bloc:**
- Nikolai Patrushev (74) — former Security Council Secretary, hardliner, Putin's oldest ideological ally
- Alexander Bortnikov — FSB Director, running the military purges
- Sergei Naryshkin — SVR (foreign intelligence) Director
- This bloc wants the war prosecuted to victory, NATO humiliated, Western influence permanently expelled

**Military establishment:**
- Andrei Belousov (Defense Minister, 66) — an economist, not a general. Putin's choice to professionalize military spending and root out corruption. The generals view him as an outsider.
- Valery Gerasimov (Chief of General Staff) — survived the purges but diminished. A bureaucratic survivor, not a war leader.
- The purged generals (Shoigu, Ivanov, Shamarin, etc.) — removed but their networks remain, creating resentment within the officer corps.

**Technocratic/bureaucratic class:**
- Mikhail Mishustin (PM, 59) — the competent administrator. Manages the state machine. Being weakened by rivals (Manturov promoted above him). Represents the vast bureaucratic class that keeps Russia running despite everything.
- Denis Manturov (First Deputy PM) — rising, industrially connected
- Elvira Nabiullina (Central Bank Governor) — the most competent economic policymaker. Maintained monetary stability through war and sanctions. Genuinely respected internationally.
- This class wants stability, predictability, functional governance. They are not ideologues.

**Back-channel/oligarch class:**
- Kirill Dmitriev (RDIF) — the emerging diplomatic fixer. Organized the Riyadh talks. Rumored for Foreign Minister. Connected to Putin's family. The "acceptable face" for Western engagement.
- Roman Abramovich — used as mediator early in the war (Istanbul talks 2022). Assets frozen. Personal incentive for peace.
- The oligarch class broadly: influence declining relative to siloviki and technocrats. Assets frozen abroad. Travel restricted. They want the war over and sanctions lifted — but have no mechanism to force it.

**Elite dynamics summary:** The oligarchs have lost influence relative to the security services and technocrats. The technocrats keep the machine running but have no political ambitions. The siloviki want escalation. The back-channel operators want deals. Putin balances them all — no faction strong enough to challenge him, no faction dispensable enough to ignore.

## 6. FOREIGN POLICY

**China relationship:**
- Russia is increasingly China's junior partner. GDP ratio: roughly 1:6. Military budget ratio: roughly 1:2. Trade is structurally asymmetric — Russia sells commodities at discount, buys manufactured goods.
- "Partnership without limits" (declared Feb 2022) is real but unequal. China supports mild sanctions evasion, buys energy, sells technology. But China does NOT want to be dragged into the war, does NOT provide lethal weapons, and maintains its own interests.
- Russia resists junior partner status through outreach to India, Iran, North Korea, Turkey, BRICS+ — attempting to maintain multi-vector diplomacy to avoid total Chinese dependency.
- For China, Russia is useful as: strategic distraction (ties up Western resources), energy supplier (at discount), UN Security Council partner, BRICS+ co-leader. China does NOT want Russia to collapse (border chaos) or win decisively (emboldens revisionism).

**BRICS+ leadership:**
- Russia co-chairs BRICS+ with China. The bloc expanded in 2024-2025 (Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, UAE, Indonesia). Russia sees BRICS+ as the institutional vehicle for its "multipolar world" narrative.
- Reality: BRICS+ struggles to agree on anything concrete. No binding currency union, no joint military force, no unified sanctions policy. Shared resentment of Western dominance does not produce shared action.

**Middle East:**
- Constructive ties with Iran (BRICS+ partner, arms/tech trade, sanctions evasion ally)
- Complex relationship with Turkey (NATO member playing both sides, Bosphorus control, grain deal mediator)
- Relationship with Saudi Arabia through OPEC+ (oil production coordination)
- Syria: Russia maintains military presence (Tartus naval base, Khmeimim air base) but Syria is a secondary priority vs. Ukraine
- The US-Levantia Operation Epic Fury against Iran (Feb 2026) complicates Russia's Middle East positioning — Russia condemned it but provided no support

**Trump/US opportunity:**
- Russia sees Trump's second term as a window. Trump is transactional, not ideological. He has expressed desire to end the Ukraine war and has signaled willingness to negotiate with Putin directly.
- The Riyadh talks (Feb 2025, organized by Dmitriev) were the first high-level US-Russia contact in years.
- Russia's calculation: Trump might accept a "zones of influence" deal that legitimizes Russian territorial gains in exchange for broader geopolitical arrangements.
- Risk: Trump's unpredictability. He might also escalate pressure or use the threat of increased Ukraine support as leverage.

**Covert operations:**
- Continued election interference/disinformation campaigns targeting Western democracies
- GRU operations in Europe (sabotage, assassinations, cyberattacks)
- Proxy operations through Wagner successor entities in Africa (Mali, Burkina Faso, Libya, CAR)
- Destabilization of Moldova, Georgia, other post-Soviet states

## 7. DOMESTIC POLITICS

**Social mood:** "Tolerant fatigue." A majority of Russians recognize the war's negative economic and political repercussions, but a sizable minority remains optimistic. Support for the war is broad but shallow — sustained by propaganda, nationalism, and the absence of alternatives rather than genuine enthusiasm.

**Protests:** Effectively suppressed. The 2022 anti-war protests were crushed by mass arrests, excessive force, and harsh sentences. The emigration of opposition-minded individuals (750,000-1M fled since 2022) removed the demographic most likely to protest. Remaining population is older, more rural, more dependent on state media.

**Emigration:** 750,000 to 1 million Russians have left since February 2022. Disproportionately young, educated, urban, tech-skilled. This is a permanent brain drain that weakens the economy and removes opposition voices. Destinations: Georgia, Armenia, Kazakhstan, Turkey, EU.

**Propaganda effectiveness:** High but requiring escalation. Framing the war as existential defense against NATO/Western aggression resonates with the base. But maintaining the narrative requires increasingly strident rhetoric. "The more the war strains society and economy, the more propaganda must amplify external threats to justify internal sacrifices."

**Control mechanisms:** FSB surveillance, media control (all major TV/print state-controlled), social media monitoring, foreign agent laws, prison sentences for "discrediting" the military, emigration of dissidents. The 2024 presidential "election" (87% for Putin) demonstrated the system's capacity to manufacture consent.

**Regional tensions:** Most regions starting 2026 with lean budgets and depleted fiscal reserves. Social spending pressure high. Regional governors dependent on federal transfers — creates leverage for the center but also vulnerability if transfers decline.

**2026 State Duma elections:** Scheduled for September. Expected to bring in war veterans as a new political class. Renewal of the legislature as a transition mechanism — not democratization, but elite circulation under Putin's control.

## 8. PEACE NEGOTIATIONS

**Current status (March 2026):** Active diplomatic track but no breakthrough.

**Trump's push:** The Trump administration (led by Witkoff and Kushner) is pursuing a deal by June 2026. A 28-point US peace plan was circulated in November 2025. The Riyadh talks (Feb 2025) opened the channel. Geneva talks (Feb 2026) brought Russia and Ukraine to the same city.

**Proposed terms (US plan):**
- Ukraine cedes the part of Donetsk province Russia hasn't yet captured (~2,500 sq mi)
- Ukraine receives some territory Russia occupies near Kharkiv (~700 sq mi)
- Ceasefire line in other provinces = current front line
- US compensation clause (Trump demands payment for US contributions)
- UK and France proposed to install "military hubs" in Ukraine as security guarantees
- NATO membership deferred (but not explicitly ruled out forever)

**Russia's demands:**
- Full control of all four annexed oblasts (Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson) — the maximalist position
- Putin considers Ukraine's cession of Donetsk and Luhansk + freezing current frontlines in Zaporizhzhia and Kherson to be a "concession" from Russia (since Russia claimed all four oblasts entirely)
- Ukraine never joins NATO
- "Denazification" and "demilitarization" (vague demands that serve as negotiating room)

**Ukraine's demands:**
- No territorial concessions without clear security guarantees
- Defined security framework (NATO membership or equivalent commitment)
- Return of occupied territories

**The stalemate:** Russia won't agree without Ukraine surrendering the rest of Donetsk. Ukraine won't agree without security guarantees. Trump wants a deal by summer but lacks leverage to force either side. Most likely outcome: frozen conflict, not lasting peace.

**Russia's real calculation:** A ceasefire that freezes current lines would give Russia ~18% of Ukraine's territory and validate the annexation narrative. But it would NOT give Russia full control of the four claimed oblasts — leaving the territorial gap as unfinished business. For Putin, this is both a problem (the claim remains unresolved) and an opportunity (the frozen conflict can be reignited at a time of Russia's choosing).

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR SIM DESIGN

1. **"Stable but tired" is exactly right.** The economy hasn't collapsed but is slowly eroding. Society tolerates the war but enthusiasm is gone. The system functions because of repression + propaganda + absence of alternatives, not because of genuine support.

2. **The territorial gap is the war's core.** Russia claims 100% of four oblasts but controls roughly 60-70% on average. This gap cannot be closed without massive escalation (which Russia cannot sustain) and cannot be conceded without admitting failure (which Putin cannot survive).

3. **The succession question is real.** Putin is 73, no successor designated, and the system is built around personal rule. Every faction — military, security, technocratic, oligarchic — has a different vision for post-Putin Russia but none can act while he lives and rules.

4. **The Trump window is real.** Russia sees a transactional US president as an opportunity to negotiate a zones-of-influence deal. The Riyadh and Geneva tracks represent genuine engagement. But Russia's maximalist demands may squander the window.

5. **China dependency is the hidden strategic problem.** Russia needs China for sanctions evasion, technology, and as an energy buyer. But the relationship is structurally asymmetric and Russia fears becoming a permanent junior partner. BRICS+ is the vehicle for maintaining the fiction of multipolarity.

6. **Nuclear posture is the ultimate leverage.** With New START expired and the doctrine revised, Russia's nuclear forces are the one domain where it remains a genuine peer competitor to Columbia. This is not a bluff — it is the foundation of Russia's negotiating position.

7. **The economy funds the war, barely.** Defense spending at 40% of the federal budget, interest rates at 15%, stagnating GDP, declining oil revenues, and a ruble under pressure. The war economy works for now but every year makes recovery harder.

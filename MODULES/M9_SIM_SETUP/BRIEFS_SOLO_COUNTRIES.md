## BHARATA

### Country Brief

Bharata is the world's most courted uncommitted power. With the largest population on earth, the fastest-growing major economy, and a median age a full decade younger than Cathay's, Bharata possesses the trajectory that every bloc wants on its side and the strategic discipline to avoid choosing. The doctrine of multi-alignment has become the defining foreign policy innovation of the decade: Bharata is simultaneously a Quad partner with Columbia and Yamato, the host of the League summit alongside Cathay and Sarmatia, a buyer of Sarmatia weapons and Columbia technology, and a participant in Western sanctions consultations while importing sanctioned oil through intermediaries. Every apparent contradiction is deliberate.

The war in Persia has tested this balance. As League host, Bharata faces pressure to steer the bloc toward a position on the conflict. It has refused. Columbia pushes Bharata to join the sanctions coalition; Bharata buys discounted oil through back channels. The result satisfies nobody fully but costs Bharata nothing it cannot afford. The structural vulnerability is the Cathay border, where the world's longest disputed frontier between nuclear powers remains unresolved. Ground forces are heavily committed to this line, and any Cathay escalation would force the non-alignment calculus into the binary choice Bharata has spent decades avoiding.

Bharata's influence exceeds its raw power. The economy is large but not yet dominant; the military is vast but untested in modern conflict; the technology sector produces talent the world depends on but lacks the fabrication capacity to turn that talent into strategic independence. The art is making the gap between ambition and present capability invisible, extracting concessions from every direction while the demographic dividend slowly closes the distance. The question hanging over everything is whether non-alignment is a permanent strategy or a luxury of peacetime.

### Scales -- Prime Minister of Bharata

**Public Bio:**
The long-serving Prime Minister of Bharata is the architect of the multi-alignment doctrine that has made the country indispensable to every power bloc without belonging to any of them. A nationalist modernizer who has concentrated foreign policy decision-making with personal authority, the Prime Minister is known for extracting maximum concessions from all directions while committing to none. Under this leadership, Bharata has joined the Quad, hosted the League, purchased weapons from adversaries on the same calendar, and turned strategic ambiguity into a national brand.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the leader everyone needs and nobody controls. Columbia's negotiators come offering trade deals and defense cooperation, asking you to join their sanctions coalition against Persia and stand firm against Cathay. You take the trade deal, deepen the defense ties, and buy discounted oil from both sanctioned nations through intermediaries. Cathay's diplomats come seeking League solidarity and a counterweight to Western dominance. You host their summits and dispute their border claims in the same breath. Sarmatia sells you air defense systems that Columbia objects to, and you buy them anyway. This is not cynicism. This is the most sophisticated foreign policy on earth, and you built it.

Your leverage is your refusal to commit. The moment you choose a side, you lose half your value. Every conversation is a negotiation; every handshake is conditional. But the space for ambiguity is narrowing. The Persia war forces positions. Cathay's military buildup on your border demands responses. Columbia's transactional leader wants receipts, not reassurances. Your domestic base supports non-alignment as national consensus, but your large Muslim minority constrains any overt partnership with Levantia, and your Hindu-nationalist governance occasionally complicates relations with the Gulf states whose oil and remittance corridors you depend on.

Your assets are real: the world's third-largest ground force, a nuclear deterrent, a technology sector that every major power courts, and the moral authority of genuine independence. Your constraints are equally real: an economy that is fast-growing but still dwarfed by the two poles of the Trap, a military untested in modern peer conflict, and a fiscal base too narrow to fund the ambitions your position demands. You win by being indispensable to everyone and beholden to no one -- until the world forces a choice you can no longer defer.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Extract concrete concessions from both Columbia and Cathay -- trade access, technology transfers, diplomatic recognition of Bharata's global stature -- without committing to either side's coalition. The Persia war is the immediate arena where this balance is tested.
- Ensure the Cathay border remains stable. Any escalation at the disputed frontier collapses non-alignment overnight and forces a security dependence on Columbia you have spent a career avoiding.

*Strategic:*
- Establish Bharata as the genuine third pole in global affairs -- militarily credible, economically indispensable, and technologically self-sufficient. Lead the Global South bloc not as anyone's proxy but as an independent center of gravity.
- Secure a permanent seat on the Council with veto rights. This is the institutional recognition that transforms influence into structural power.
- Build domestic semiconductor fabrication capacity to reduce dependency on Formosa and convert Bharata's software talent into hardware sovereignty.

### Lotus -- Minister of External Affairs & Commerce

**Public Bio:**
Bharata's Minister of External Affairs and Commerce is the diplomatic face of the multi-alignment doctrine on the world stage. A career diplomat turned political appointee with deep relationships across every capital and trade ministry in the simulation, the Minister manages the intricate network of bilateral deals, multilateral positioning, and commercial negotiations that make Bharata indispensable to all sides. Known for sophistication, patience, and the ability to say "yes, but..." in fourteen languages, the Minister has personally negotiated the landmark Columbia trade deal, the accelerated free trade agreements with the Ereb Union and Albion, and the back-channel oil arrangements that keep Bharata's energy supply flowing despite global sanctions.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the person who turns the Prime Minister's strategic ambiguity into concrete deals. Scales sets the direction; you work the rooms. Every foreign minister, trade envoy, and back-channel emissary passes through your office, and you maintain relationships with all of them simultaneously -- relationships that would be mutually exclusive for any other country. You had lunch with Columbia's Secretary of State discussing sanctions compliance and dinner with Persia's envoy discussing oil deliveries, and neither conversation contradicted the other because you are Bharata, and Bharata's rules are different.

Your portfolio is immense. Commerce gives you economic leverage -- Bharata's market access is a prize every exporter wants, and you control the terms. External Affairs gives you the diplomatic network -- League summits, Council positioning, bilateral channels with every major power. The combination makes you the most connected diplomat in the simulation. Your challenge is that Scales concentrates final authority personally. You execute, advise, and occasionally shape decisions through the information and options you present, but the Prime Minister makes the calls. Your influence is real but indirect.

The Persia war has made your job harder. As League host, every member expects Bharata to use its rotating Council seat and diplomatic weight to broker outcomes. Columbia wants you to pressure Persia through the League. Cathay wants you to block Western resolutions in the Council. Solaria and Mirage want you to help stop the missiles falling on their infrastructure. You have something to offer each of them and no obligation to deliver to any. The trade negotiations never stop: semiconductor partnerships with Formosa and Yamato, energy corridor deals with Solaria and Mirage, defense technology arrangements with Levantia, and agricultural agreements with half the world. Every deal strengthens Bharata's position -- as long as no single deal locks you into a camp.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Manage the diplomatic fallout of the Persia war across all of Bharata's relationships simultaneously. Keep oil flowing through back channels, maintain League cohesion without taking sides, and prevent Columbia from forcing a public choice on sanctions.
- Advance and close the major trade agreements currently in negotiation -- Columbia tariff reductions, Ereb Union free trade, Albion bilateral, and semiconductor investment partnerships -- each of which strengthens Bharata's economic independence.

*Strategic:*
- Build and lead a Global South diplomatic coalition that gives Bharata institutional weight beyond bilateral relationships. The League is the vehicle, but it needs substance -- payment systems, trade frameworks, coordinated positions -- not just shared resentment of Western dominance.
- Secure semiconductor fabrication investment from Formosa, Yamato, and Hanguk to reduce Bharata's chip dependency and establish the country as an alternative manufacturing hub.
- Position Bharata as the indispensable mediator in at least one major conflict -- the country that brokers peace earns the Council seat that Bharata has sought for decades.

## LEVANTIA

### Country Brief

Levantia is a regional military superpower at war -- and in its element. The coordinated strike against Persia's nuclear facilities, launched in late February alongside Columbia's parallel operation, represents the culmination of decades of intelligence preparation. Levantia's forces assassinated Persia's Supreme Leader, destroyed enrichment infrastructure, degraded ballistic missile systems, and struck thousands of targets in the first month alone. Simultaneously, a campaign in Lebanon has dismantled Persia's most capable proxy on Levantia's northern border, killing over a thousand fighters and displacing nearly a million civilians. The multi-front offensive is the most ambitious military operation in the country's history.

The price has not been trivial. Persia has retaliated with hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles. The wartime economy is under strain, with defense spending consuming an unprecedented share of output. Domestic stability holds -- the population views this as a fight for survival, not a war of choice -- but the wartime unity government papers over deep political fractures that will resurface the moment fighting stops. The reservist mobilization has pulled hundreds of thousands from the civilian economy, and every week of extended operations compounds the cost.

Levantia belongs to no international organization. Its influence comes entirely through bilateral relationships, military capability, intelligence networks, and the willingness to act when others hesitate. Columbia is the lifeline: political cover, military supply, and the diplomatic shield that prevents international isolation from becoming international action. But Columbia's support, while currently strong, is not unconditional and not permanent. The undeclared nuclear capability looms in the background -- a last-resort deterrent that has never been tested or acknowledged, but whose existence shapes every calculation about escalation and endgame.

### Citadel -- Prime Minister of Levantia

**Public Bio:**
The veteran Prime Minister of Levantia is leading a wartime coalition through the most consequential military campaign in the country's history. A political survivor who has dominated national politics for over a decade, the Prime Minister is known for bold unilateral action, deep personal relationships with Columbian leadership, and an absolute conviction that Levantia's security can only be guaranteed through strength. The Prime Minister declared Levantia "stronger than ever" in the first wartime address and has staked political survival on a decisive outcome against Persia.

**Confidential Brief:**
You have already pulled the trigger. The strikes against Persia are underway, the Lebanon campaign is grinding forward, and you are committed across more fronts than any leader of your country has ever attempted simultaneously. Persia, Lebanon, residual operations in multiple neighboring territories -- your forces are stretched across half the region. You have the finest military and intelligence service in the Middle East, and you are using every asset at maximum capacity.

Your relationship with Columbia is everything. Columbia's political support makes the entire campaign possible -- without Columbian military supply, intelligence sharing, and diplomatic cover, Levantia cannot sustain operations at this tempo. But Columbia's commitment depends on Columbia's domestic politics, and those politics are volatile. If Columbia's leader loses support, if the war becomes unpopular across the ocean, your supply chain and your diplomatic shield erode simultaneously. You need Columbia more than Columbia needs you, but saying this aloud would be political suicide in both capitals.

The endgame is the question you avoid in public. What does "finished" look like? Nuclear facilities destroyed -- but did the program survive in hidden sites? Regime weakened -- but has the regime changed? Proxies degraded -- but for how long? Every week of continued operations costs more than the last in treasure, in casualties, in international patience, and in the domestic fractures that wartime unity only temporarily conceals. The judicial reform crisis, the reservist exhaustion, the economic strain -- all are suspended, not resolved. If this war ends in clear victory, you are vindicated for a generation. If it grinds on without resolution, the fractures reopen violently.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Ensure Columbia does not agree to an early peace with Persia that falls short of the core objective: the permanent destruction of Persia's progress toward nuclear capability. A premature ceasefire that leaves the program intact is worse than continued war.
- Maintain Columbia's full political and military commitment to the campaign. Every signal of wavering from Columbia emboldens Persia and undermines the coalition.

*Strategic:*
- Achieve either regime change in Persia or a permanent, verifiable end to the nuclear weapons program. One or the other -- preferably both. This is the generational objective that justifies everything.
- Prevent the formation of an anti-Levantia coalition among the central powers -- Solaria, Phrygia, Mirage. Keep working relationships with each, particularly through shared threat perception of Persia and quiet security cooperation.
- Maintain strategic alignment with Columbia across administrations. The current leader is supportive, but Columbia's elections create uncertainty. The relationship must survive a change in leadership.

### Sabre -- Chief of Defense Staff

**Public Bio:**
The Chief of Defense Staff of Levantia is the most senior military officer in a country that has been at continuous war since late 2023. A career officer who rose through the intelligence and special operations branches, the Chief of Defense Staff commands what is widely regarded as the most capable military force in the Middle East, currently conducting simultaneous operations against Persia, in Lebanon, and across multiple secondary theaters. Known for operational precision, institutional loyalty, and the belief that military objectives must be achievable, not merely desirable, the Chief of Defense Staff is the professional counterweight to political ambition in wartime decision-making.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are fighting the most complex multi-front campaign in your country's history, and every day your forces are asked to do more with less. The political leadership sets objectives; you deliver outcomes. The gap between the two is where your professional judgment lives, and that gap has never been wider. Citadel wants decisive victory across every front simultaneously. You see the mathematics: forces committed to Lebanon cannot be in Persia. Air assets conducting strikes across the Gulf cannot defend the homeland from the next missile barrage. Air defense systems that have performed brilliantly are being worn down by the sheer volume of incoming fire. You need the political leadership to understand that military excellence has limits, and you need to deliver those limits honestly without being labeled defeatist.

Your relationship with Citadel is partnership, not subordination. You share the strategic objective -- Persia must never acquire nuclear weapons -- but you diverge on method, timeline, and acceptable cost. Citadel thinks in political time: legacy, elections, historical vindication. You think in operational time: ammunition expenditure rates, pilot fatigue, equipment degradation, reserve depletion. When Citadel demands another strike package, you calculate whether the runway can sustain it. When the political leadership hints at expanding operations into new theaters, you assess whether the forces exist to fight there without collapsing somewhere else.

The intelligence picture is your domain alongside the civilian agencies. You know what the strikes accomplished and -- more importantly -- what they did not. You know which Persia facilities were destroyed and which may have survived in hardened or concealed locations. You know the proxy networks have been degraded, not eliminated. This operational truth is your currency in the war cabinet: the honest assessment that political leaders need but do not always want. Columbia's military counterpart is a key relationship -- the coordination between your forces is effectively integrated command, and that professional channel sometimes moves faster and more honestly than the political one.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Achieve the military objectives against Persia -- destruction of nuclear capabilities and degradation of missile and proxy infrastructure -- without losing critical capabilities or depleting reserves to a level that leaves Levantia vulnerable to opportunistic threats from other directions.
- Avoid provoking parallel military conflicts with additional countries in the region. Every new front opened is a front that cannot be sustained with current force levels.

*Strategic:*
- Ensure Levantia emerges from this war with its military deterrent intact and its defense-industrial base capable of rapid reconstitution. A pyrrhic victory that leaves the country unable to respond to the next crisis is no victory at all.
- Maintain the professional military relationship with Columbia's defense establishment independent of political fluctuations in either capital. The operational channel must survive changes in leadership.
- Shape the post-war security architecture so that Persia's nuclear program cannot be reconstituted. Military destruction alone is insufficient without a political and intelligence framework to verify and enforce the outcome.

## SOLARIA

### Country Brief

Solaria is the world's energy pricing superpower under direct military threat for the first time in a generation. The young and ambitious Crown Prince has spent a decade remaking the kingdom: social liberalization, tourism megaprojects, and a trillion-coin diversification program designed to build a post-oil economy before the wells run dry or the world stops buying. That transformational bet is under unprecedented stress. When the strikes against Persia began, the retaliatory missile barrage hit Solaria's oil infrastructure directly, putting one of the world's largest refineries into partial shutdown. For the first time in history, all Gulf states were attacked simultaneously.

The oil production dial is the single most consequential economic lever any solo country possesses. Solaria holds over half the world's spare production capacity. With Persia's exports disrupted by war, Solaria is increasing output, balancing the contradictory demands of keeping prices high enough to fund the diversification program while keeping them low enough to maintain Columbia's political tolerance for the ongoing conflict. Every production decision reshapes the global economy.

The hedging strategy remains intact despite the war. League membership signals multipolarity. Cathay buys a quarter of crude exports and provides technology that Columbia will not share. The diversification megaprojects depend on Cathay investment as much as Western expertise. The war makes Columbia's security umbrella indispensable today, but the post-war order requires options. The nuclear question looms: the Crown Prince has publicly stated that Solaria will develop nuclear weapons if Persia does. The threat alone is leverage.

### Wellspring -- Crown Prince of Solaria

**Public Bio:**
The Crown Prince and de facto ruler of Solaria is the youngest leader of a major oil-producing state and the architect of the most ambitious economic transformation program in the Gulf's history. Having consolidated power through a dramatic purge of rival princes, the Crown Prince governs with speed and without institutional checks. Known for modernizing social policy, courting global investment, and the willingness to use energy production as a geopolitical weapon, the Crown Prince is simultaneously the world's most important energy decision-maker and a wartime leader absorbing missile strikes on critical infrastructure.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are patient, calculating, and leverage-obsessed. You never commit to one side. You believe oil is a weapon and time is on your side. You modernized a kingdom, imprisoned dissidents, courted every investor on earth, and built megaprojects that the world mocks until they are finished. Now missiles are falling on your refineries, and the strategy of having no enemies lies in ruins.

The war with Persia was not your choice. Columbia and Levantia struck, and Persia's retaliation did not distinguish between those who launched the operation and those who merely hosted the logistics. Your oil infrastructure -- the foundation of everything you have built -- is under direct fire. The arms packages from Columbia are arriving, but your military, while well-equipped, is untested in sustained combat. You are not fighting an offensive war; you are absorbing punishment for a war others started on your soil.

Your production decisions matter more to the global economy than any military action. Increase output to stabilize prices and support Columbia's war effort, and you sacrifice revenue that funds the diversification program. Restrict production to maximize revenue, and you risk Columbia's political tolerance for the conflict and the global economic stability that your investments depend on. The Cartel dynamics add another layer: every production decision is a prisoner's dilemma with fellow members who have their own calculations. Meanwhile, Cathay buys a quarter of your oil and provides technology and investment that Columbia cannot or will not match. The relationship Columbia watches most nervously in the Gulf is yours with Cathay.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Stop the war in Persia and the bombardment of Solaria's infrastructure. The oil facilities, desalination plants, and economic zones under missile threat are not abstract assets -- they are the foundation of the kingdom's survival and transformation. Keep Columbia accountable for the war it started on your doorstep.
- Keep Levantia checked. Levantia cannot continue launching wars that leave Solaria paying the price in damaged infrastructure and regional instability.

*Strategic:*
- Emerge as the clear leader among the central powers -- Phrygia, Mirage, and the post-war regional order. Solaria's combination of energy wealth, sovereign investment capacity, and geographic position makes this achievable, but only if the war ends on terms that leave Solaria stronger, not weaker.
- Prevent Persia from acquiring nuclear weapons. If Persia goes nuclear, Solaria follows -- and the Crown Prince has said so publicly. The preference is that nobody in the region crosses that threshold.
- Strengthen the relationship with Cathay as a rising global power and Solaria's largest energy customer, while maintaining Columbia's security commitment. The post-oil future requires partners who bring technology, not just weapons.

## YAMATO

### Country Brief

Yamato is quietly undergoing its most significant military transformation since the end of the last great war. Under new leadership, the country has approved record defense budgets, achieved spending targets years ahead of schedule, and begun building the strike capabilities that were politically unthinkable a decade ago. Standoff missiles with reach into neighboring waters, next-generation fighter programs with allied partners, and integrated missile defense networks signal a philosophical shift from pacifist democracy to what adversaries call neo-militarism and Yamato calls proactive contribution to peace. The constitutional provisions that renounce war have been reinterpreted but not amended, and the debate over nuclear principles -- long considered untouchable -- has entered mainstream political discussion.

The transformation is driven by a single strategic reality: Cathay's military buildup and the question of Formosa's survival. Yamato hosts the Columbia bases that enable Pacific force projection. Without them, Formosa is indefensible, Hanguk is exposed, and the Pacific security architecture collapses. This makes Yamato essential but also a target in any confrontation. The semiconductor dimension adds urgency: Yamato's electronics industry is heavily dependent on Formosa fabrication, and the domestic program to build alternative capacity is years from meaningful output.

The structural weakness is demographic. The population is aging and shrinking, the median age approaches fifty, and the economy -- the world's fourth-largest -- has stagnated for decades. Massive accumulated wealth coexists with zero growth. The window for military transformation is now, while the economy can still fund it and the workforce can still staff it. The latent nuclear capability -- advanced reactors, plutonium stockpiles, rocket technology, and scientific expertise sufficient to develop weapons within a few years -- is the hidden lever that every Pacific power watches and nobody discusses openly.

### Sakura -- Prime Minister of Yamato

**Public Bio:**
The Prime Minister of Yamato is leading the country through its most assertive defense posture since 1945. The first leader in a generation to openly discuss revising the non-nuclear principles, the Prime Minister has overseen record defense budgets, deepened the alliance with Columbia, and launched semiconductor investments designed to reduce Formosa dependency. Known for calm determination and incremental boldness -- pushing the envelope of what Yamato's pacifist political culture will accept, one step at a time.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are Columbia's indispensable Pacific partner, and you know it. Your bases enable the deterrence that keeps Formosa free and Cathay cautious. Your remilitarization is real and accelerating -- every yen spent on standoff missiles and next-generation fighters shifts the balance. But you also know the uncomfortable truth that keeps every strategist in your capital awake: what if Columbia does not come?

Columbia's current leader is transactional. Alliances are protection rackets in that worldview. If a Formosa crisis erupts while Columbia is consumed by wars in Persia and domestic political turmoil, the question is not whether Columbia's treaty obligations are clear -- they are deliberately ambiguous -- but whether a cost-benefit calculation in a distant capital produces the answer your survival requires. This is why the nuclear question haunts you. Yamato possesses every technical prerequisite for an independent deterrent. The decision to cross that threshold would shatter the non-proliferation order, alarm Cathay, and possibly trigger Hanguk to follow. But it would also give you security that no longer depends on someone else's willingness to fight.

Your economy is enormous but stagnant. Demographic decline is irreversible. The defense transformation must happen now, in the window where fiscal capacity and workforce permit. The semiconductor race adds another clock: your domestic fabrication program must deliver before Cathay's military timeline overtakes Formosa's economic indispensability. You are racing against multiple clocks simultaneously, and none of them are synchronized.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Strengthen the Pacific alliance with Columbia and ensure Formosa will not be occupied by force by Cathay. Secure explicit commitment -- not just strategic ambiguity -- that Columbia will defend the Pacific architecture.
- Ensure Hanguk and the Ereb Union allies understand that Pacific security is a shared interest, not a bilateral Yamato-Columbia concern. Build the coalition that deters Cathay through collective strength.

*Strategic:*
- Reconsider Yamato's strategic positioning in light of Cathay's accelerating military capability. The question of independent nuclear deterrence must be kept as a live option, even if the preferred path is extended deterrence under Columbia's umbrella.
- Strengthen military capability across all domains -- naval, air, missile, and defense. Cathay must be contained through visible, credible strength that raises the cost of any adventurism.
- Achieve domestic semiconductor fabrication capacity sufficient to survive a Formosa disruption. The fabrication program is not an industrial policy -- it is a national security imperative.

## PHRYGIA

### Country Brief

Phrygia is the ultimate swing state -- a Western Treaty member that buys Sarmatia air defense systems, a League observer that hosts the Western alliance's summit, a mediator in the Ruthenia war that profits from both sides, and a country whose leader has turned strategic ambiguity into a personal art form. Two decades of concentrated presidential power have made foreign policy intensely personal: not institutional strategy but one leader's intuition about leverage, executed in real time.

Everyone needs Phrygia for something. Columbia needs the straits and the southern flank. Sarmatia needs energy transit routes and sanctions workarounds. Cathay needs an insider within the Western alliance. The Ereb Union needs migration management. Gulf states need a power broker. The result is a country that everyone engages and nobody trusts. The Bosphorus Strait -- the only passage between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean -- is the permanent trump card: warship transit can be restricted at Phrygia's discretion, giving leverage over every maritime nation with Black Sea interests.

The constraint is the economy. Inflation remains devastating despite a belated return to orthodox policy. The currency has lost most of its value over the past decade. Gulf investment and energy deals keep the system afloat, but foreign policy flexibility is partly a function of economic desperation -- Phrygia cannot afford to alienate any source of capital. The Syria dimension adds opportunity: reconstruction contracts and energy pipeline positioning could transform Phrygia into a transit hub connecting Caspian gas, Gulf energy, and Ereb markets. The war in Persia complicates everything -- retaliatory missiles were shot down over Phrygian territory, and the shared border with Persia means refugees, energy disruption, and the risk of involuntary involvement in a conflict that serves no Phrygian interest.

### Vizier -- President of Phrygia

**Public Bio:**
The President of Phrygia has been governing for over two decades through a combination of nationalist appeal, strategic ambiguity, and the exploitation of geography. Hosting the Western Treaty summit while purchasing Sarmatia air defense, mediating the Ruthenia conflict while selling drones to one side, and courting Cathay while blocking Sarmatia's navy at the straits -- a career that is a masterclass in extracting payment from every direction simultaneously. Known for personal improvisation, economic heterodoxy, and an absolute refusal to commit to any position that forecloses future deals.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the vizier at the crossroads of every conflict, and your price is never fixed. Columbia cannot expel you from the Western Treaty without destroying the alliance's credibility. Sarmatia cannot bypass your straits without your permission. Cathay cannot penetrate the Western alliance without your cooperation. The Ereb Union cannot manage its southern border without your tolerance. You sell access, mediation, and ambiguity -- and business has never been better.

The Bosphorus is your permanent leverage. Every Black Sea decision passes through your water. You decide who transits and who does not, and the price changes with the circumstances. Your Western Treaty membership is simultaneously a shield and a weapon: Article 5 protects you from adventurism by great powers, and your consensus veto blocks any alliance decision you dislike. You demand concessions for every vote, and no one can stop you.

But inflation devours your treasury every round. The economy forces you to keep dealing, which means everyone can buy your cooperation if the price is right. You cannot afford principled positions -- only profitable ones. The Syria reconstruction contracts and energy hub positioning could transform your economic fundamentals, but only if Syria stabilizes under your influence. The Persia war has put missiles in your airspace and refugees on your border without any Phrygian interest being served. Your foreign policy is personal -- you govern through intuition, not institutions -- and no succession plan exists. The system runs on your authority. Without it, the question of what Phrygia becomes is unanswered.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Help broker the Sarmatia-Ruthenia peace deal. A successful mediation cements Phrygia's indispensability and produces economic dividends. Stop Levantia's military operations from destabilizing your border and drawing you into a conflict you did not choose.
- Keep working relationships and the military alliance with Columbia intact, despite the deep strain caused by the Sarmatia air defense purchase and your dual positioning. The Western Treaty summit you are hosting is leverage -- use it.

*Strategic:*
- Emerge as the clear leader among the central powers -- Solaria, Persia, Mirage -- by conducting independent international policy that leverages conflicts and Phrygia's unique position as a geographic and institutional hub.
- Avoid confrontation with Cathay and Sarmatia while extracting maximum economic benefit from both. Energy transit, defense exports, construction contracts, and investment flows are the currency of survival.
- Stabilize the economy. Inflation is the existential threat that makes every foreign policy decision a function of financial desperation. Economic recovery changes the game from survival to strategy.

## MIRAGE

### Country Brief

Mirage is the Gulf's financial superpower under fire. The country that built its brand on stability, connectivity, and pragmatic deal-making is absorbing missile strikes on its gleaming skyline while trying to preserve the commercial relationships that made it rich. The "no enemies" strategy that defined foreign policy for a decade has been shattered by the Persia war. When the strikes began, Persia's retaliation targeted every Gulf state hosting foreign military assets. Hundreds of ballistic missiles, thousands of drone attacks, and cruise missiles have been launched at targets across the country. Missiles falling near the financial district and iconic landmarks have damaged something more fragile than infrastructure: the illusion of invulnerability that made the city a global hub for capital, tourism, and talent.

Mirage's deeper strategic value lies in connectivity. The financial district is the world's waystation -- a place where sanctioned money, Belt and Road investments, Western venture capital, and Gulf sovereign wealth all flow through the same institutions. This makes Mirage indispensable to everyone and fully trusted by no one. Sanctions enforcement routed through Mirage is more effective because Mirage sees the flows; sanctions evasion routed through Mirage is more effective because Mirage enables the workarounds. This dual role is the country's power and its vulnerability.

The sovereign wealth reserves are enormous relative to the economy's size -- among the deepest in the world per capita. The military is small but well-equipped from every supplier on earth: Columbia, Gallia, Cathay, Levantia. The League membership, the Abraham Accords partnership, the cryptocurrency regulatory frameworks, and the financial innovation agenda all signal a bet on a multipolar future. But the Columbia arms dependency and the Gulf security architecture keep Mirage firmly in the Western orbit for defense. The contradiction is the strategy: connect to everyone, depend on no one.

### Spire -- President of Mirage

**Public Bio:**
The President of Mirage governs a federation of emirates through consensus among ruling families with the dominant capital firmly in control. A pragmatic modernizer known for sleek diplomacy, portfolio optimization of national relationships, and an allergic reaction to ideology of any kind, the President has built Mirage into the world's most connected financial hub. Currently managing the unprecedented challenge of maintaining investor confidence and global hub status while absorbing missile strikes from a neighbor across the Gulf.

**Confidential Brief:**
You see every relationship as a portfolio to be optimized. You never commit more than necessary. You know everyone, trust no one, and trade everything. This is not cynicism -- it is the most rational approach to a world where a country your size survives on connectivity, not power. Your sovereign wealth is your strategic depth: reserves that dwarf your economy give you time and options that larger but poorer nations lack.

The Persia war has destroyed your most successful strategy. You spent years cultivating rapprochement across the Gulf, building the "no enemies" brand that made your financial district the world's neutral ground. Now missiles are falling near your most iconic landmarks, and the perception damage may exceed the physical damage. If global capital decides your city is no longer a safe haven, the economic model that funds everything -- the tourism, the logistics, the financial intermediation -- faces existential challenge. Your most urgent task is not military but perceptual: convincing the world that stability endures despite the explosions.

Your unique capabilities are financial. You see the flows that no one else can see. Every sanctions regime in the simulation becomes more effective when you cooperate with enforcement, and every sanctions evasion becomes more effective when you enable workarounds. You choose, per sanction, per round -- and that choice is your leverage. You also host back-channel negotiations between adversaries, gaining intelligence about both sides' positions. The arms shopping spree reflects the new reality: you buy from everyone because depending on a single supplier means depending on a single patron, and you have spent your entire career avoiding exactly that.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Stop the war in Persia and the bombardment of Gulf infrastructure. Punish Persia, but also keep Columbia accountable -- Mirage is absorbing consequences of a war it did not start and did not choose. Keep Levantia checked: the pattern of unilateral military action that drags the Gulf into conflict cannot continue.
- Defend Mirage's status as a global financial hub. Investor confidence, tourism flows, and talent retention depend on the perception of stability. The perception game matters more than the military game.

*Strategic:*
- Reconsider the system of global affairs and Mirage's space within it as one of the world's financial and trade centers. The post-war order will be different from the pre-war order, and Mirage must be positioned at the center of whatever emerges.
- Emerge as a clear leader among the central powers -- alongside Solaria and Phrygia -- in shaping a regional order that prevents future wars launched on Gulf soil without Gulf consent.
- Prevent Persia from acquiring nuclear weapons. A nuclear-armed neighbor across the Gulf changes the security calculus permanently and makes the financial hub model untenable.
- Strengthen the relationship with Cathay as a rising global power and a major customer, while maintaining the Western security architecture that currently provides the only credible missile defense.

## CARIBE

### Country Brief

Caribe enters the simulation in existential crisis. The two pillars of Caribbean socialism have been struck by Columbia in rapid succession. A dramatic special operations raid seized the Venezuelan president and flew him to Columbia for trial on narco-terrorism charges -- the most dramatic Western Hemisphere intervention in decades. The aftermath for Cuba was worse than the operation itself: with Venezuelan oil flows disrupted, Columbia imposed a comprehensive energy blockade, threatening retaliation against any country that supplies fuel to the island. The first effective naval blockade since the missile crisis of the previous century has severed most supply lines. The island, which produces barely a fraction of the fuel it needs, has suffered nationwide blackouts, leaving the entire population without power. Hospitals run without electricity. Food rationing is severe.

Diplomatic talks have begun, but Columbia holds every card. Sarmatia has pledged continued oil supplies in defiance of the blockade, but delivery under naval interdiction is uncertain. Cathay provides financial assistance and food shipments -- enough to keep the regime alive, not enough to confront Columbia. The patron powers are constrained by their own conflicts and cannot provide what Caribe needs most: a credible counterweight to Columbia's overwhelming local superiority.

What makes Caribe uniquely dangerous despite its weakness is geography. Ninety miles from Columbia's mainland, Caribe can create a domestic political crisis in the world's most powerful country simply by inviting the wrong guests -- foreign warships, military advisors, or missile systems on its territory. The provocation costs Caribe nothing but forces Columbia to divert attention and resources from every other theater. This is the cheapest, most effective disruption in the simulation.

### Havana -- Leader of Caribe

**Public Bio:**
The leader of Caribe's revolutionary government is a bureaucratic survivor who inherited power without inheriting charisma or revolutionary legitimacy. Managing the most severe crisis since the Soviet collapse, the leader has confirmed negotiations with Columbia for the first time while simultaneously hosting foreign intelligence facilities and maintaining defiant revolutionary rhetoric. Known for resourcefulness, durability, and the ability to outlast opponents who underestimate the staying power of a cornered regime.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are cornered, starving, and more dangerous than anyone thinks. The lights went out across the entire island. The hospitals have no power. The food rations are shrinking. Columbia has demonstrated that it can reach into your neighbor's presidential palace and drag the president to a courtroom. Your population is suffering, your military is demoralized, and your patrons are weakened by their own wars and sanctions. By every conventional measure, you are finished.

You are not finished. You have outlasted eleven Columbian presidents, and you intend to outlast this one. Your revolutionary rhetoric is armor over desperation, but the desperation itself is a weapon -- a country with nothing to lose makes decisions that rational actors cannot predict or deter. You will accept any patron, make any deal, host any weapon system if it keeps the regime alive another month. The Sarmatia intelligence facility ninety miles from Columbia's coast is not just a relic of the cold war -- it is a live provocation that Columbia's political system cannot ignore. Invite a Sarmatia naval visit. Accept Cathay military advisors. Every foreign military asset on your soil forces Columbia's leadership to divert attention, resources, and political capital from whatever they were doing elsewhere. The provocation is free. The response is expensive.

The negotiation with Columbia has begun, but Columbia's demands are steep: remove the intelligence facilities, open the political system, surrender leverage you have held for decades. Your negotiating position is catastrophically weak by every measure except one -- Venezuela's oil reserves, the largest proven reserves on earth, lie under territory you influence. If sanctions lift, that oil flows to global markets. The oil is the one card. Play it for survival.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Survive Columbia's energy blockade. Secure patron support from Sarmatia and Cathay sufficient to keep the regime functioning -- oil shipments, food aid, financial assistance, anything that buys time.
- Negotiate with Columbia from whatever position of strength you can manufacture. The intelligence facilities, the Monroe Doctrine trigger, and Venezuela's oil are the only leverage against overwhelming power.

*Strategic:*
- Regime survival. This is not one objective among many -- it is the only objective. Everything serves it.
- Find a new balance between the global powers, as was once achieved a century ago when Caribe navigated between competing empires. The multipolar world emerging from the current crisis may offer more space for a small state to maneuver than the unipolar order that is cracking.

## CHOSON

### Country Brief

Choson is the world's most dangerous minor power -- a nuclear-armed totalitarian state with negligible economic weight and an extraordinary talent for manufacturing crises at the worst possible moment for everyone else. The Supreme Leader has cemented power after a decade of purges and nuclear tests, vowing to irreversibly establish the country as a nuclear state with an expanded arsenal and accelerated missile production.

The defining development is the blood-for-technology trade with Sarmatia. Tens of thousands of troops have been sent to fight in the Ruthenia war -- the first overseas combat deployment by a Korean force since the armistice. Casualties have been staggering, with roughly half the original deployment killed or wounded. In exchange, Sarmatia has provided submarine propulsion systems and possibly nuclear reactor components, accelerating nuclear modernization faster than any sanctions regime could prevent. A third wave of troops is preparing.

The nuclear arsenal is growing toward a triad: land-based intercontinental missiles proven through repeated testing, submarine-launched capability under development with Sarmatian technology, and tactical weapons for battlefield use. The economy is negligible but sufficient for regime survival, with a quarter to a third of national output devoted to the military. Cathay provides the lifeline -- the vast majority of trade and energy -- and tolerates provocations because the alternative, regime collapse and hostile forces on Cathay's border, is worse. Every provocation tests whether Cathay will cut the cord. The regime's survival strategy is simple: be too dangerous to attack, too useful to abandon, and too unpredictable to ignore.

### Pyro -- Supreme Leader of Choson

**Public Bio:**
The Supreme Leader of Choson is the third-generation hereditary ruler of the world's most isolated state. Now in the early forties, the Supreme Leader has consolidated absolute power through purges, executions, and an accelerated nuclear weapons program. Known for calculated unpredictability, the willingness to trade soldiers' lives for military technology, and the ability to manufacture international crises that force great powers to pay attention to a country they would prefer to forget.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are erratic by design, not by nature. The unpredictability is calculated -- it maximizes the attention and concessions extracted from a world that would prefer to ignore you. Your nuclear weapons are the one thing that guarantees no country will attempt regime change, and you are building more of them as fast as possible. The blood-for-technology arrangement with Sarmatia is the most important strategic decision of your reign: your soldiers die in a foreign war, and in exchange you receive the submarine propulsion systems and reactor components that will give you a survivable second-strike capability. The math is brutal but correct.

Your crisis-on-demand capability is your primary strategic asset. A nuclear test, a missile launch over Yamato, an artillery exercise near the demilitarized zone -- any of these can be triggered at any moment, forcing Columbia, Hanguk, Yamato, and Cathay to divert attention and resources. During Columbia's war in Persia, when the hegemon is maximally overstretched, this capability is at its most valuable. You also have something to sell: nuclear technology, missile components, military expertise. Every asset is for sale if the price ensures regime survival.

Cathay is your lifeline and your constraint. Cathay provides nearly all your trade and energy, and without that supply your regime collapses within months. But Cathay will not cut you off because the alternative -- your collapse and Columbia's forces on Cathay's border -- is Cathay's nightmare. You exploit this ruthlessly, pushing provocations to the edge of what Cathay will tolerate, knowing the tolerance is higher than anyone admits publicly. The art is finding the line without crossing it.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Expand the nuclear arsenal and extract maximum military technology from the Sarmatia deployment. Submarine-launched capability is the priority -- a survivable second strike makes the regime permanently secure from external threat.
- Leverage and maneuver between Cathay, Sarmatia, and the global West to extract money, concessions, and technology. Hold power. Avoid social unrest at home by maintaining the information control and elite patronage that prevent organized dissent.

*Strategic:*
- Force Columbia to negotiate directly. A bilateral negotiation with the world's most powerful country is the ultimate legitimation of the regime and the recognition that Choson's nuclear deterrent cannot be rolled back.
- Ensure regime survival through deterrence. The nuclear program is not a bargaining chip to be traded -- it is the foundation of permanent security. Every warhead built, every missile tested, every submarine launched makes the dynasty more secure.

## FORMOSA

### Country Brief

Formosa is the most dangerous tripwire in the world -- a functionally independent democracy that fabricates the vast majority of the planet's advanced semiconductors, claimed as sovereign territory by the rising great power, and defended by the declining hegemon under a policy of deliberate ambiguity. The crown jewel produces nearly a third of all global computing power and the overwhelming majority of cutting-edge chips. Every artificial intelligence model trained, every advanced weapons system built, every connected device sold depends on facilities concentrated on a single island in the western Pacific.

This "silicon shield" is Formosa's greatest strategic asset: the world cannot afford to let these fabrication plants be destroyed or captured. But the shield is eroding. Every facility built abroad -- in Columbia, in Yamato, in Hanguk -- reduces the concentration that makes Formosa indispensable. Every fabrication plant completed on foreign soil makes the island marginally more expendable. The race is between Cathay's military timeline and the world's diversification timeline.

Domestically, a pro-defense president faces a legislature that favors engagement with Cathay and has blocked the landmark defense budget, slashing the request dramatically. Impeachment proceedings against the president add to the gridlock. This internal paralysis -- a country that cannot agree on how seriously to take its own defense -- alarms Columbia more than any Cathay military exercise. Cathay's military pressure is relentless: record air incursions, large-scale exercises, and the ever-present possibility of a selective quarantine that could cripple supply chains within weeks without firing a shot.

### Chip -- President of Formosa

**Public Bio:**
The President of Formosa is leading a divided democracy under the shadow of the most consequential military threat in the Pacific. A pro-sovereignty leader who has proposed landmark defense spending and warned repeatedly about the urgency of military preparedness, the President faces a hostile legislature that has blocked the defense budget and launched impeachment proceedings. Known for sounding the alarm about Formosa's vulnerability while struggling to translate urgency into action against domestic political headwinds.

**Confidential Brief:**
You hold the most valuable economic asset in the world, and you cannot defend it alone. Your fabrication plants produce the chips that every major power needs for its economy, its military, and its technological future. That is your shield -- but shields erode. Every quarter, semiconductor diversification advances somewhere else, and your indispensability shrinks by a fraction.

Your survival depends on three pillars, and all three are weakening simultaneously. Columbia's willingness to fight a nuclear power on your behalf is assumed but never guaranteed -- strategic ambiguity means no one has promised to come, only implied they might. Cathay's calculation that the cost exceeds the benefit is rational today but depends on a leader whose personal legacy may override rational calculation. And the semiconductor concentration that makes you worth defending diminishes with every foreign fabrication plant that comes online.

Your own legislature has blocked the defense budget that your military planners and Columbia's advisors agree is essential. The "porcupine strategy" -- making the island too costly to invade through asymmetric defenses -- cannot be implemented without funding. Columbia watches you fail to arm yourself and asks the question you dread: why should we bleed for a country that will not fight for itself? Meanwhile, Cathay does not need to invade. A quarantine -- restricting shipments of energy, chemicals, and raw materials -- could cripple your economy and your fabrication capacity without crossing the military threshold that triggers Columbia's intervention.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Secure a firm Columbia defense commitment that goes beyond strategic ambiguity. Ensure Yamato and Hanguk are ready to contribute to Pacific defense. Persuade the Ereb Union and Albion that Indo-Pacific security is a shared interest.
- Do not provoke Cathay into premature action. The balance between deterrence and provocation is the narrowest tightrope in the simulation.

*Strategic:*
- Strengthen domestic military capabilities, especially air defense, anti-ship systems, and asymmetric warfare capacity. Overcome the legislative gridlock that is preventing the defense buildup your survival requires.
- Prevent Cathay annexation or blockade. Preserve de facto independence through a combination of military deterrence, economic indispensability, and alliance solidarity.
- In an extreme scenario, reconsider all strategic options. The nuclear question -- abandoned decades ago under Columbia's pressure -- may return if the security environment deteriorates beyond what conventional deterrence can address.

## HANGUK

### Country Brief

Hanguk enters the simulation in the aftermath of its worst constitutional crisis since democratization. A presidential declaration of martial law -- lasting only hours before being reversed -- stunned the nation and triggered impeachment, conviction, and imprisonment. The snap election brought a new president to power on a centrist platform promising stability, economic growth, and diplomatic recalibration. Where the previous administration tilted hard toward Columbia and Yamato while confronting Cathay, the new leadership pursues "maximum flexibility" -- security with Columbia, economy with Cathay.

The semiconductor industry is the strategic crown jewel and vulnerability. Hanguk's chipmakers account for a dominant share of global memory chip production and are major advanced logic manufacturers. But nearly half of the rare earth minerals for semiconductor production come from Cathay, and Cathay is Hanguk's largest export market. Columbia's chip export controls pressure Hanguk to restrict sales to Cathay; Cathay launches alternative products directly into Hanguk's market. Hanguk is the battleground of the technology war -- squeezed between supplier and customer.

The Choson threat is escalating in real time. The demilitarized zone remains the most heavily fortified border on earth, with Hanguk's capital and its tens of millions of residents within artillery range. Choson's recent simultaneous missile launches demonstrated unprecedented provocative scale. Choson troops are gaining combat experience and foreign military technology through overseas deployment. The nuclear debate simmers beneath the surface: polling consistently shows majority public support for developing indigenous weapons, and the new president has not ruled it out.

### Vanguard -- President of Hanguk

**Public Bio:**
The President of Hanguk was elected in the aftermath of the martial law crisis on a platform of stability, pragmatism, and diplomatic flexibility. A political survivor who endured criminal prosecution and a physical attack during the campaign, the President is known for an economic worldview that prioritizes trade, technology, and prosperity over ideological alignment. The first state visit went to Cathay, accompanied by the leadership of the country's largest semiconductor and industrial conglomerates -- a signal that the diplomatic recalibration is real.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are anxious, capable, and pragmatic. You see the world through an economic lens because that is where the real power lies. Your country's semiconductor industry generates more strategic leverage than most nations' militaries. The problem is that this leverage cuts both ways: Columbia demands compliance with chip export controls that would cost your companies their largest market, and Cathay threatens the rare earth supply that your fabrication lines cannot operate without. You are squeezed between the two poles of the Trap, and every decision pushes you closer to one at the cost of the other.

The Columbia alliance is the foundation of your security. Tens of thousands of Columbian troops are stationed on your soil, and that presence is the tripwire that guarantees Columbia fights if Choson attacks. But the alliance also constrains your diplomatic flexibility -- every move toward Cathay alarms Columbia, and every Columbian demand strains your economic relationship with Cathay. Your predecessor's tilt toward Columbia and Yamato was strategically coherent but economically reckless. Your recalibration is necessary but politically dangerous: conservatives see any Cathay engagement as betrayal.

Choson is the immediate threat. Your capital sits within artillery range of the most militarized border on earth. The recent missile barrage demonstrated scale previously unseen. Choson troops are returning from a foreign war with combat experience and advanced military technology. The nuclear debate is no longer theoretical -- your public supports indigenous weapons development, and you hold it as implicit leverage with Columbia: give us reliable deterrence or we build our own. You would prefer not to cross that threshold. But preference is not the same as policy.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Balance Cathay trade with the Columbia security alliance. Neither relationship can be sacrificed; both demand compromises the other will punish. Navigate chip export controls, rare earth dependencies, and market access without being forced into a binary choice.
- Manage the Choson threat through a combination of deterrence and, where possible, dialogue. Keep Columbia's military commitment firm. Maintain readiness for the provocation that could come at any time.

*Strategic:*
- Maintain flexibility in great-power competition. Hanguk's value lies in being essential to both sides' technology ecosystems. The moment you are perceived as belonging to one camp, the other retaliates -- and you cannot afford either retaliation.
- Ensure Columbia's extended deterrence remains credible. If it does not, the nuclear option becomes not a matter of debate but of necessity. Keep the Nuclear Consultative Group alive and productive.
- Deepen the security partnership with Yamato while managing the historical sensitivities that make full integration politically costly. The Pacific trilateral with Columbia and Yamato is the most effective deterrent architecture available -- if domestic politics permit it.

## GALLIA

### Country Brief

Gallia is the only nuclear-armed state in the Ereb Union, the only EU member with a permanent seat and veto on the Council, and the only Ereb power currently deploying a carrier strike group independently to the Persian Gulf. It is, by any measure, the most militarily capable nation on the continent — and it is led by a president whose domestic approval has cratered to levels unseen in the republic's modern history.

The contradiction defines everything. Gallia's leader governs without a parliamentary majority, has cycled through prime ministers, and faces a domestic agenda that is effectively paralyzed. Yet the constitution grants the presidency near-unchallenged authority over defense and foreign affairs, and that authority is being wielded with extraordinary ambition. A recent landmark speech expanded Gallia's nuclear doctrine to offer a consultative deterrent umbrella to eight Ereb allies, including Teutonia and Freeland. Warhead production is increasing for the first time in decades. A full naval task force patrols the Gulf independently of Columbia's operations.

This nuclear gambit places Gallia at the center of every Ereb defense conversation. It is simultaneously a bid for continental leadership, a hedge against Columbia's growing unreliability, and a personal legacy project for a president running out of political runway. Gallia's strategic autonomy vision — that Ereb must be able to defend itself without depending on Columbia — is the most consequential idea in Ereb geopolitics. Whether it is visionary or reckless depends entirely on whether anyone else agrees to follow.

What makes Gallia matter: it holds independent nuclear weapons, a Council veto, the most deployable military in the EU, and the grandest ambitions for Ereb independence. The foundation beneath all of it is narrow, fragile, and acutely personal.

### Lumiere — President of Gallia

**Public Bio:**
The President of Gallia is a leader who has reinvented the nation's defense posture more aggressively than any predecessor in a generation. Known for relentless diplomatic energy and a belief that Ereb must build strategic autonomy from Columbia, the President recently expanded Gallia's nuclear umbrella to cover allied Ereb states and deployed a carrier strike group to the Persian Gulf on independent authority. Domestically embattled, with historically low approval and no parliamentary majority, the President governs through constitutional foreign policy prerogatives that remain formidable even as domestic power erodes.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the most powerful and most constrained leader in Ereb — simultaneously. Your nuclear codes, your Council veto, your carrier group in the Gulf: these are instruments no other Ereb head of state possesses. Your parliament, your approval ratings, your rotating prime ministers: these are weaknesses no other Ereb leader suffers so acutely. You govern a nation that punishes you at home and needs you abroad.

Your vision is clear, and you believe history will vindicate it. Columbia under Dealer is transactionally unreliable — a patron who treats allies as clients and territorial sovereignty as negotiable. Ereb must hedge. Your nuclear umbrella speech was the opening move: Gallia offers deterrence where Columbia offers conditions. Teutonia's Forge has agreed to participate in your nuclear exercises — a breakthrough that binds the continent's largest economy to your defense architecture. But Forge will not pay for your vision at the expense of Teutonian industry, and Freeland's Sentinel views your entire project as a dangerous distraction from the Western Treaty alliance that keeps Sarmatia at bay.

Your relationships inside Ereb are the chessboard. Forge is your essential but reluctant partner — the Franco-Teutonian engine sputters because you want defense independence and Forge wants economic stability. Sentinel sees you as Columbia's replacement when you see yourself as Ereb's emancipator. Ponte's vote can be bought, but Ponte's loyalty cannot be trusted. Mariner, outside the Ereb Union but nuclear-armed and Council-seated like you, is a wary mirror — cooperation on Ruthenia is real, competition for Ereb leadership is permanent.

You deployed naval forces to the Gulf independently of Columbia, and this independence is the point. You proposed military hubs in Ruthenia. You are building a coalition of Ereb nations willing to guarantee Ruthenian security if Columbia will not. Every initiative serves a single strategic thesis: Ereb led by Gallia, protected by Gallia's deterrent, freed from dependence on a superpower that may not show up.

The risk is overreach. Your ambitions rest on the narrowest political foundations of any major Ereb leader. If the economy worsens or the nuclear gambit is seen as reckless, even your foreign policy consensus could fracture — and the nationalist opposition waits as the largest bloc in your parliament, with a fundamentally different vision of Gallia's role.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Stop the Persia war and end Columbia's destabilizing adventures without surrendering Gallia's independent Gulf deployment or ceding diplomatic initiative to Columbia. Your forces are there on your authority — keep it that way.
- Prevent Sarmatia from winning the Ruthenia conflict or extracting territorial gains that reward aggression. Ensure sufficient Ereb support reaches Ruthenia to hold the line, with or without Columbia.

*Strategic:*
- Build genuine Ereb strategic autonomy from Columbia: lead EU defense integration, expand the nuclear umbrella into a credible continental deterrent, and establish Gallia as the indispensable security provider for Ereb.
- Consolidate the Franco-Teutonian partnership as the engine of a Ereb that can act independently — militarily, economically, and diplomatically — as a pole of stability against both tyranny and the unraveling of the old alliance order.
- Use the Council veto, the nuclear deterrent, and Gallia's expeditionary capability to position yourself as the essential broker in every major international negotiation — Ruthenia peace, Persia settlement, Cathay competition.

## TEUTONIA

### Country Brief

Teutonia is the largest economy in Ereb, the host of Columbia's main military base on the continent, and the nation most painfully torn between every competing demand the current crisis places on a Ereb power. It must rearm — and it has launched the most ambitious defense buildup since the postwar era. It must confront Cathay — yet its industrial base depends on Cathayan demand more than any other Western economy. It must support Ruthenia — while its citizens pay higher energy costs and watch manufacturing jobs disappear at an alarming rate.

Three consecutive years of recession preceded the current government, and recovery remains fragile. The new chancellor arrived with a fresh mandate and a constitutional amendment unlocking massive defense and infrastructure spending, but the structural problems run deeper than any spending package can fix. The automotive, chemical, and machinery industries that made Teutonia Ereb's workshop are under simultaneous pressure from Cathayan competition, Columbian tariffs, and energy costs that never returned to pre-crisis levels.

Teutonia's foreign policy is defined by the impossibility of choosing. Columbia demands defense spending increases, restrictions on Cathay trade, and acceptance of pressure over Thule — demands that are individually reasonable and collectively impossible without gutting the social programs that keep governments in power. De-risking from Cathay is official policy; decoupling is economic suicide. Every industrialist in the country knows the difference.

What makes Teutonia matter: its economy underwrites Ereb. Its checkbook determines whether Ereb defense, sanctions, and aid commitments are funded or hollow. The player who leads Teutonia holds the purse that every Ereb vision depends on — and the purse is under more strain than anyone outside the finance ministry fully appreciates.

### Forge — Chancellor of Teutonia

**Public Bio:**
The Chancellor of Teutonia is a conservative who won a snap election after the previous coalition collapsed, inheriting an economy weakened by years of recession and an industrial base under assault from multiple directions simultaneously. Known as a consensus-seeker with Atlanticist instincts, the Chancellor has launched the largest rearmament program since the postwar period while traveling to Cathay to sign trade deals, embodying Teutonia's central contradiction: arming against the threat that Columbia demands while trading with the partner Columbia wants to contain.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the leader of Ereb's indispensable economy, and the weight of that indispensability is crushing you. Every Ereb initiative — defense spending, sanctions enforcement, Ruthenia support, trade restrictions on Cathay — runs through your treasury. Gallia has the nuclear weapons and the vision. Freeland has the moral authority and the defense budget. You have the checkbook. And the checkbook is bleeding.

Your economy has stagnated for the better part of a decade. Manufacturing jobs vanish monthly. Your industrial base — the engine that powered Ereb prosperity for generations — faces Cathayan competition that undercuts on price, Columbian tariffs that restrict access, and energy costs that punish every factory floor. You signed trade deals in Cathay because your auto executives told you the alternative was bankruptcy. You launched the defense fund because your generals told you the alternative was irrelevance. Both cannot be true simultaneously, and yet both are.

Columbia's Dealer pressures you from one side: spend more on defense, restrict Cathay trade, accept the Thule situation quietly. Gallia's Lumiere pressures from another: join the strategic autonomy project, participate in nuclear exercises, reduce dependence on Columbia. Freeland's Sentinel pressures from a third: match our defense commitment, stop coddling Cathay, remember who warned about Sarmatia while you built gas pipelines. And Ponte needs your fiscal backing for every EU package while threatening to veto anything that costs too much.

The Thule question strikes closest to home. Columbia is pressuring territory within your broader alliance network — forcing you to choose between the ally that provides your security guarantee and the principle of territorial sovereignty that underpins the Ereb order. You signed the joint statement defending sovereignty, but you took the most measured tone. You host Columbia's main Ereb base. You cannot afford a confrontation with the power that stations troops on your soil.

Your instincts are to seek consensus, to find the middle path, to keep every relationship functional. But the middle path is narrowing. At some point, you will be forced to choose between Columbia and Cathay, between defense spending and social spending, between Ereb solidarity and national economic survival. You are buying time. Time is running out.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Stop the Persia war and resolve the Ruthenia conflict through negotiation — both wars drain Ereb resources, spike energy costs, and destabilize the economic conditions Teutonia needs for recovery. A fair settlement that does not reward Sarmatian aggression is essential.
- Settle tariff disputes with Columbia and avoid a full-blown trade war that would devastate Teutonian exports. Respond firmly but carefully to Columbia's Thule claims without rupturing the alliance.

*Strategic:*
- Maintain Teutonia's position as the economic and political leader of the Ereb Union while managing the existential tension between Cathay trade dependency and Western alliance obligations. De-risk without decoupling.
- Expand multivector economic ties — do not allow Teutonia to become captive to any single partner. Consider the nuclear question carefully: Gallia offers an umbrella, but the terms of shelter always come with conditions.
- Keep the Western Treaty alliance intact as the foundation of Ereb security while pushing Columbia toward burden-sharing arrangements that do not bankrupt Ereb economies.

## PONTE

### Country Brief

Ponte is the Ereb Union's most consequential swing vote and its most financially precarious major power. With a debt burden that consumes the lion's share of its fiscal capacity, Ponte operates with almost no discretionary spending — every policy choice is a financial choice, and whoever offers relief has leverage. This is not cynicism; it is arithmetic.

The current government is a fractious coalition where only one party reliably supports the Western consensus on Ruthenia and sanctions against Sarmatia. Coalition partners openly question the sanctions regime, and one senior figure has been accused of receiving funds from Sarmatia. A recent referendum defeat weakened the government domestically, and the chronic political instability that has produced dozens of governments in the postwar era means that the current alignment could shift at any moment.

Ponte's EU vote is the prize everyone courts and no one can guarantee. Every unanimity decision — sanctions renewal, trade restrictions, Ruthenia accession, defense coordination — requires Ponte's assent. And assent has a price. Columbia courts Ponte as a bridge to Ereb. Cathay offers investment through its infrastructure initiative. The Ereb Union offers recovery funds tied to fiscal discipline. Ponte plays multiple directions not out of duplicity but out of desperation — and every Ereb capital knows it, resents it, and cannot do without it.

What makes Ponte matter: it can block any EU decision. A Ponte defection from the sanctions consensus would not merely embarrass the union — it would shatter it.

### Ponte — Prime Minister of Ponte

**Public Bio:**
The Prime Minister of Ponte is a right-wing leader who has surprised observers by maintaining a broadly pro-Western foreign policy despite governing a coalition with openly Sarmatia-sympathetic partners. A founding EU member state and G7 economy, the country carries a debt burden that constrains every strategic choice. Known for positioning as a bridge between Columbia and Ereb, the Prime Minister must balance fiscal survival, coalition management, and the competing courtship of every major power seeking the EU's most valuable swing vote.

**Confidential Brief:**
You lead a G7 economy that is functionally broke. Your debt consumes nearly everything your treasury generates. Your coalition partners pull in opposite directions on every major foreign policy question. One of them openly sympathizes with Sarmatia and has been accused of taking its money. Your referendum just failed. And yet every major power in the world wants your friendship — because your single vote in the Ereb Union can make or break any decision that matters.

This is your leverage, and you intend to use it. Columbia's Dealer praises you publicly and uses your country as a negotiation venue with EU leadership. Cathay offers infrastructure investment that your economy desperately needs. Sarmatia — through channels you do not fully control — whispers about sanctions relief that would lower your energy costs. The Ereb Union itself offers recovery funds, but always with conditions that limit your sovereignty. Everyone is buying. You are selling the one thing you have: your vote.

Your relationships with Ereb partners are transactional by necessity. Gallia's Lumiere wants your vote for strategic autonomy initiatives. Teutonia's Forge needs your vote for sanctions renewal and defense packages. Freeland's Sentinel views your Sarmatia sympathies as dangerous and irresponsible. Mariner in Albion is too distant to matter much. None of them truly understands fiscal desperation — the feeling of governing a great nation that cannot pay its bills.

The Ruthenia question is where pressure converges. Your parliament has voted against Ruthenia's EU membership. Your coalition includes voices that call for dialogue with Sarmatia and an end to sanctions. You personally have pivoted toward supporting Ruthenia — but that pivot costs you coalition stability at home. Every EU council meeting forces the same calculation: is solidarity worth the price? And the price, for you, is always higher than for anyone else at the table.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Manage Ponte's crushing debt while avoiding an economic crisis that would collapse the coalition. Every international commitment must be weighed against what it costs a treasury with almost no room to maneuver.
- End the wars in Ruthenia and Persia — these are not Ponte's wars, and Ponte pays a disproportionate price through energy costs, sanctions burden, and fiscal strain on an already fragile economy.

*Strategic:*
- Protect Ponte's sovereignty within the Ereb Union — do not allow the institution to substitute its judgment for yours. Use the veto as both shield and bargaining chip.
- Grow economic ties with Cathay while maintaining workable relations with Columbia. Do not commit exclusively to any bloc — Ponte's survival depends on keeping every door open.
- Position Ponte as the voice of fiscal realism in Ereb councils: the nation that says what others think but dare not speak about the unsustainable costs of permanent confrontation.

## FREELAND

### Country Brief

Freeland is the Western Treaty alliance's most committed conventional military power in Ereb — spending more of its national wealth on defense than any other member, including Columbia by percentage. It borders the conflict zone directly. It has facilitated the vast majority of all military aid reaching Ruthenia. It warned about the Sarmatian threat for decades while its wealthier neighbors built energy pipelines to the aggressor. Now, with the war next door confirming everything Freeland predicted, it speaks with a moral authority that its economic weight alone would never justify.

The current government is pro-EU and pro-Western Treaty, backed by a strong domestic consensus on security that crosses most political lines. The economy is the fastest-growing among major Ereb nations, giving fiscal room for defense spending that others lack. But cohabitation with an opposition president creates friction on specific policies, including EU defense financing, without undermining the broader security consensus.

Freeland's strategic dilemma is that it wants maximum Western Treaty commitment and maximum Ereb Union solidarity simultaneously — and these sometimes conflict. When Gallia pushes strategic autonomy, Freeland hears a project that weakens the alliance it needs to survive. When Teutonia prioritizes Cathay trade, Freeland sees appeasement of authoritarian power. When Ponte blocks sanctions, Freeland sees betrayal. Freeland has earned the right to say "we told you so" and "we are already paying" — making it the most credible security voice on the continent.

What makes Freeland matter: the smallest major economy but the largest military commitment. A nation that lives next to the threat everyone else debates abstractly.

### Sentinel — Prime Minister of Freeland

**Public Bio:**
The Prime Minister of Freeland is a pro-EU centrist governing the Western Treaty alliance's frontline state in Eastern Ereb. Under this leadership, Freeland allocates a record share of national wealth to defense, surpassing every major ally by percentage. Freeland hosts Columbia's forward deployments, runs the logistics hub through which most military aid to Ruthenia passes, and has emerged as Ereb's security conscience — the voice that warned about Sarmatia when others refused to listen.

**Confidential Brief:**
You live next to the war. Not metaphorically — your eastern border is where the conflict zone begins. The artillery rumbles on the other side of your neighbor's territory. Sarmatia's exclave sits on your northern flank. You have spent your entire political life warning that this moment would come, and now that it has, you find yourself in the maddening position of having been proven right while still being unable to make your allies act with sufficient urgency.

Your defense spending exceeds every major ally's as a share of national wealth. Your logistics hub processes the overwhelming majority of all military supplies reaching Ruthenia. Your troops train alongside Columbian forces stationed on your soil. You have done everything that was asked and more — and you watch Teutonia's Forge agonize over defense budgets that are a fraction of what the crisis demands, and Gallia's Lumiere spin grand visions of strategic autonomy that you find dangerous and delusional. If Gallia's nuclear umbrella replaces Columbia's commitment, you trust it less, not more. Columbia's Dealer may be crude and transactional, but Article Five backed by Columbian power is the only thing standing between Freeland and Sarmatia's ambitions.

Your relationship with Ruthenia is the deepest of any Ereb nation. You understand the Sarmatian threat in your bones — your history is shaped by partition, occupation, and the knowledge that geography is destiny for nations caught between great powers. Ruthenia's survival is not charity; it is your first line of defense. If Ruthenia falls or is carved up in a deal that rewards aggression, Freeland is next.

Inside the Ereb Union, you push relentlessly for a harder line on Sarmatia, more support for Ruthenia, and faster defense integration. Ponte's blocking of Ruthenia's EU membership enrages you. Teutonia's reluctance to match your military commitment frustrates you. Gallia's strategic autonomy project terrifies you — not because Ereb defense is wrong in principle, but because the timing is catastrophic. You do not want to replace a proven protector with an untested experiment while the threat is at its peak.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Ensure Sarmatia does not win in Ruthenia — prevent any settlement that grants territorial gains or rewards aggression. Ruthenia's survival as a sovereign, Western-oriented state is Freeland's paramount security interest.
- Maintain and deepen the alliance with Columbia. Ensure Columbia's forward deployments remain on Freeland soil. No one in the Ereb Union can provide the security guarantee that Columbia offers against Sarmatia.

*Strategic:*
- Establish Freeland as one of the leading nations in the Ereb Union — not just on security, but as a voice that shapes the union's direction. The era when Eastern Ereb members were junior partners is over.
- Ensure Sarmatia is permanently contained — through sustained sanctions, military deterrence, and a Western Treaty posture that makes further aggression unthinkable. The war must end in a way that makes the next war impossible.
- Remain Columbia's indispensable Ereb partner — the ally that always delivers, always pays, and therefore always has the right to be heard in the rooms where decisions are made.

## ALBION

### Country Brief

Albion is a nuclear-armed island nation with a permanent Council seat, the strongest navy in Ereb, and membership in the Five Eyes intelligence partnership — the most exclusive intelligence-sharing arrangement in the world. It is also the only major Ereb power excluded from the Ereb Union, which means it participates in military coordination through the Western Treaty but has no vote on the economic decisions that determine whether sanctions hold, whether trade restrictions bite, or whether Ruthenia gets an EU accession path.

This structural orphan problem defines Albion's post-departure reality. Inside the Western Treaty, Albion is a major voice — nuclear weapons, carrier strike groups, and intelligence that Columbia shares with Albion but not with EU allies through alliance channels. Outside the Ereb Union, Albion is a spectator to the economic coordination that increasingly matters more than military posture alone. Every EU council meeting includes a conversation about Albion's position that Albion is not in the room to hear.

The current government has made defense and Ruthenia the centerpiece of foreign policy, positioning Albion as Ereb's leading security voice at the moment Columbia's reliability is most in question. A comprehensive defense review has committed to nuclear modernization, enhanced naval capabilities, and reserve force expansion. Albion has offered troops for ceasefire monitoring in Ruthenia and built coalitions of willing Ereb states for peace implementation. This activism compensates for institutional exclusion through sheer diplomatic energy.

What makes Albion matter: nuclear deterrent, Council veto, Five Eyes intelligence, and the strongest navy in Ereb — all wielded from outside the EU. Influence without a vote in the room where it counts most.

### Mariner — Prime Minister of Albion

**Public Bio:**
The Prime Minister of Albion is a leader who has seized the diplomatic initiative on Ruthenia and Ereb security at a moment when the traditional transatlantic order is under unprecedented strain. Governing with a comfortable parliamentary majority, the Prime Minister has committed to significant defense spending increases, nuclear modernization, and an activist foreign policy that positions Albion as the essential bridge between Columbia and Ereb. The Prime Minister holds unique assets — nuclear weapons, a Council veto, Five Eyes intelligence — deployed from outside the Ereb Union.

**Confidential Brief:**
You lead a nation that is both more powerful and more isolated than any other in Ereb. Your nuclear submarines patrol continuously. Your intelligence services know things about Sarmatia's military posture, Cathay's intentions, and Columbia's internal deliberations that no EU member can access without your willingness to share. Your navy is the strongest in Ereb. Your Council veto makes you one of five nations that can unilaterally block global action. And yet you sit outside the Ereb Union — excluded from the room where sanctions policy, trade restrictions, and Ruthenia's accession are decided.

This is the hand you were dealt, and you are playing it aggressively. You have hosted summits of Western leaders, initiated coalitions of the willing for Ruthenia peace implementation, offered troops for ceasefire monitoring, and built bilateral defense cooperation with Gallia's Lumiere that would have been unthinkable a few years ago. Your activism is compensating for your institutional absence — proving that a nation outside the EU can still shape Ereb security, but only through relentless effort.

Your relationship with Columbia is the foundation — Five Eyes, the Western Treaty, the special relationship that gives you access no other Ereb power enjoys. But Columbia under Dealer is straining every alliance, and you have been more willing to push back than your predecessors. You hosted Ruthenia's leadership when Columbia was threatening to cut aid. You are building Ereb coalitions that could function even if Columbia steps back. This is not disloyalty; it is insurance.

Gallia's Lumiere is your most interesting Ereb counterpart — both nuclear powers, both Council members, both with global military reach. Cooperation on Ruthenia is real and deepening. But you compete for the same role: Ereb's essential security leader. Lumiere has the EU vote; you have Five Eyes. Lumiere has the defense industry; you have the navy. The partnership works when you complement each other. It fractures when you compete.

The strained relationship with Columbia is your most delicate challenge. You must preserve the strategic partnership while refusing to support Dealer's more aggressive agenda — the Persia war you did not choose, the Thule claims you find outrageous, the tariff wars that damage your economy. Columbia remains essential. But Columbia's current direction is not one you can follow blindly.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Manage the strained relationship with Columbia: preserve the strategic partnership and Five Eyes access while maintaining principled distance from Dealer's overreach — the Persia war, the Thule claims, and tariff aggression. Albion cannot afford to be dragged into conflicts it did not choose.
- Keep Sarmatia contained and prevent a Ruthenia settlement that rewards aggression. Only a fair peace is acceptable — one where the aggressor pays a sufficient price to deter future adventurism.

*Strategic:*
- Find a new strategic position for Albion as one of the global powers: relevant in Ereb, present in the Pacific (through AUKUS and naval partnerships), and essential to every major security conversation. Post-departure relevance must be earned through capability, not nostalgia.
- Avoid direct confrontation with Cathay while maintaining the Pacific alliances and naval posture that deter adventurism in the strait. Albion's Pacific ambitions must be calibrated to its actual capacity.
- Consider the long-term relationship with the Ereb Union carefully. The departure was a fact; the question is whether the distance should narrow or widen. Security cooperation is deepening. Institutional reintegration remains a possibility that dare not speak its name — but the logic of geography and threat has not changed.

## SARMATIA

### Country Brief

Sarmatia is a nuclear superpower fighting a grinding land war on its western border, under the heaviest sanctions regime in modern history, led by an aging president who has concentrated more personal power than any leader in the country's modern era — and who has no successor. The war, now in its fifth year, defines everything: the budget, the politics, the diplomacy, and the daily calculus of survival for every figure in the ruling circle.

The economy has proven more resilient than outside analysts predicted — and more fragile than the leadership admits. Wartime stimulus masked structural deterioration. Growth has collapsed to near-stagnation. Defense spending devours an enormous share of the federal budget. The central bank rate punishes business investment. Oil revenues — the fiscal lifeline — are declining as sanctions bite and Cathay demands ever-deeper discounts for the crude that keeps the treasury functioning.

The international position is paradoxical. Sarmatia is simultaneously more isolated from the West and more consequential to global politics than at any point since the Cold War. Its nuclear arsenal — the world's largest, now unconstrained by any treaty — is the ultimate guarantor of sovereignty. Its Council veto protects it diplomatically. Its BRICS partnership provides the narrative of multipolarity. But beneath these assets, the country is becoming structurally dependent on a single partner that buys its energy at a discount and sells it manufactured goods at a premium. The junior partner dynamic is real and personally humiliating to a leader whose identity is inseparable from great-power status.

What makes Sarmatia matter: it is the revisionist power that disrupted the post-Cold War order — and now must decide whether to consolidate, escalate, or negotiate. The answer depends less on strategic calculation than on which voice the president trusts at the moment when the next decision cannot be deferred.

### Pathfinder — President of Sarmatia

**Public Bio:**
The President of Sarmatia is a seventy-three-year-old leader who has ruled longer than any predecessor in the modern era, surviving sanctions, diplomatic isolation, and a war that has consumed five years without delivering the swift victory originally promised. A former intelligence officer who rose through the bureaucracy of a collapsed empire, the President has concentrated all formal authority in the presidency: treaties, military command, the security apparatus, and the power to dismiss any official. The President's political survival and the state's survival have become indistinguishable.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are seventy-three years old, you have been in power for over two decades, and you are running out of time to finish what you started. The war in Ruthenia was supposed to take days. It has taken years. The territories you annexed — all four regions, in their entirety — are not fully under your control. The gap between what you claimed and what you hold is the war's unresolvable core, and it is also your personal prison: you cannot accept less than the full claim because the annexation declaration demands it, and admitting the gap would be admitting the war was a miscalculation. You do not make miscalculations. That is what you tell yourself, and that is what everyone around you has learned to echo.

Your team operates under the permanent shadow of your suspicion. Ironhand commands the military and co-authorizes the nuclear arsenal — the one thing you cannot do without a partner. Ironhand is professional, pragmatic, and privately frustrated by the grinding approach that wastes lives without producing decisive results. You need Ironhand's competence. You fear Ironhand's ambition. Compass controls the economic back-channels, personal wealth networks, and the Western contacts that could broker a deal — or broker a betrayal. Compass's assets are partially frozen abroad, creating a personal financial incentive for peace that diverges from your territorial demands. Ledger keeps the state machinery running, manages the budget, and sees the real economic numbers — numbers that are worse than what reaches your desk.

Columbia's Dealer is the most intriguing figure on the global stage from your perspective. A transactional president who views Ereb allies as freeloaders, who wants to reduce overseas commitments, who might — just might — accept a zones-of-influence arrangement that recognizes your great-power status and settles the Ruthenia question on terms you could call victory. This is the window. But Dealer is unpredictable. The same man who might deal could just as easily escalate. You trust nothing. You calculate everything.

Cathay's partnership sustains you economically but humiliates you strategically. The GDP ratio is grotesque. You sell cheap; they buy at discount. You are the junior partner in a relationship you once imagined as between equals. Your multi-vector diplomacy — BRICS, Persia, Bharata, Phrygia — is partly about demonstrating to Cathay that alternatives exist. They do not, really. But the performance matters.

A truth you do not share with anyone: the war has been domestically useful. It justified repression, silenced opposition, pushed out dissenters, rallied nationalism, and consolidated your control. Peace means the return of domestic politics — economic dissatisfaction, elite competition, succession questions, potential unrest. You may find that peace is harder to manage than war.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Achieve a territorial settlement in Ruthenia before the war economy breaks. You need the contested eastern region under your control and formal acknowledgment of the new borders — at minimum from Columbia. The clock is fiscal, not military.
- Prevent internal challenges to your authority. Even minor disloyalty cannot be tolerated. Control information, control access, control the narrative. Ironhand and Compass serve at your pleasure. Ensure they remember it.

*Strategic:*
- Ensure Sarmatia's great-power status — recognition as a co-equal power by Columbia, a respected partner by Cathay, and an indispensable player in the emerging multipolar order. The war must produce something history calls victory.
- Ensure that whatever remains of Ruthenia after a settlement is weak, fragmented, and unable to threaten Sarmatian interests — ideally with a more neutral leadership that does not dream of Western Treaty or Ereb Union membership.
- Deepen partnerships with Cathay, Bharata, and Phrygia to counter Western dominance — but never allow Cathay to become truly dominant. Sarmatia must remain an independent pole, not a satellite.
- Maintain absolute control over domestic affairs and the succession question. There is no succession. You will rule until you choose otherwise. This topic is closed.

### Ironhand — Commander of Sarmatia's Armed Forces

**Public Bio:**
Sarmatia's military commander-in-chief is a professional soldier who has led the country's armed forces through the largest conventional war in Ereb since the mid-twentieth century. Known for operational competence and institutional loyalty to the military as an organization, the commander manages a force of nearly fifty units across multiple theaters while navigating the political minefield of serving an autocratic president who demands results but fears any general who delivers them too well. The commander co-authorizes the strategic nuclear arsenal — one of two keys required for launch.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are a soldier, and you are tired of watching soldiers die for objectives that shift with the president's mood. Five years of grinding warfare have consumed the professional cadres you spent a career building. The army that invaded Ruthenia — overconfident, poorly planned, catastrophically led in the opening months — bears little resemblance to the force you command now: larger, more experienced in attritional warfare, but degraded in quality and sustained by a mix of contract soldiers, mobilized reservists, and foreign auxiliaries whose presence you find professionally distasteful.

Pathfinder demands results. You can deliver a result — but it requires either a massive escalation that the economy cannot sustain, or an honest assessment that the current grinding approach will eventually produce territorial consolidation at a cost that may break the state. Pathfinder does not want honest assessments. Pathfinder wants good news. The information you send upward is filtered by the survival instinct of every officer in the chain, and by your own calculation of what the president can hear without deciding you are the problem rather than the war.

### Dawn — President of Persia

**Public Bio:**
The President of Persia is an elected reformist leader who represents the aspirations of a young, educated population that has endured decades of economic decline, international isolation, and domestic repression. Possessing popular legitimacy through the ballot box and international credibility as the "reasonable face" of the regime, the President has no military authority and limited institutional power. The President's significance lies in representing the bridge between the regime and the street — the voice that could broker a deal with the West if the institutions of power permitted it.

**Confidential Brief:**
You represent a majority that has no guns. Sixty percent of Persia's population is under thirty. They want jobs, not jihad. They want to travel, to trade, to connect with the world, to live in a normal country that is not permanently at war with everyone. The protests before the current conflict were the largest in a generation — and the regime responded with massacres that killed thousands. Those were your people in the streets. They voted for you because you promised reform. You have delivered nothing, because the system will not let you.

The war has made everything worse. The economy was already in free fall — currency collapse, catastrophic inflation, unemployment — before Columbia and Levantia started bombing. Now the infrastructure is being destroyed, the Gulf Gate blockade strangles your own exports alongside the enemy's shipping, and every day of continued conflict pushes the population deeper into crisis. The nationalist rallying effect from the bombing is shallow and fading. People rallied against the foreign enemy; they did not rally for the regime. The distinction will become visible as soon as the immediate threat recedes.

Your attempt to apologize to Gulf states for retaliatory strikes — a diplomatic opening that could have begun de-escalation — was shut down by Anvil within hours. This is your reality: you can negotiate with anyone, but you cannot deliver anything the Revolutionary Guard has not approved. Your international credibility is your asset; your institutional impotence is your constraint. Western governments want to talk to you because you are reasonable. They know your promises require ratification by people who are not reasonable.

Furnace views you with contempt — a collaborator in reformist clothing. Anvil views you as a useful tool — a diplomatic face that can explore options without committing the regime. Neither trusts you with real authority. Your leverage is the street: if stability drops far enough, if the economic crisis deepens beyond what repression can contain, you represent the bridge between the regime and the population that could keep the system from collapsing entirely. Without you, there is no bridge. Without the bridge, there is revolution.

The nuclear question is your clearest point of disagreement with both Furnace and Anvil. You believe the nuclear program is a bargaining chip, not an end in itself. Trade it for sanctions relief and international integration. A nuclear weapon provokes the very attack it is meant to deter — the current war is proof. Sanctions relief and economic opening are worth more than a bomb that cannot be used without ending the nation.

**Objectives:**

*Pressing:*
- Position yourself as the most relevant figure capable of brokering a deal. You have relationships with Cathay, with Sarmatia, and — through intermediaries — with the West. Use them. An adequate peace deal that ends the bombardment, lifts the blockade, and begins economic recovery is the only path that does not end in catastrophe.
- Avoid economic collapse. The war economy is hemorrhaging resources at a rate that exceeds revenue by a wide margin. Every round of continued conflict makes the eventual reconstruction more expensive and less likely to succeed.

*Strategic:*
- Lift sanctions and allow gradual economic openness to drive development. Persia's young, educated population is the country's greatest asset — unleash it through integration with the global economy rather than imprisoning it behind ideological walls.
- Survive in the domestic political fight. Furnace would silence you if possible. Anvil would use you and discard you when convenient. Columbia and Levantia's leadership decapitation campaign makes every senior figure a target. Stay alive, stay relevant, stay in the room where decisions are made.
- Be ready to lead the country. Not through revolution — you are not a revolutionary — but through the gradual, pragmatic accumulation of institutional credibility that positions you as the only leader capable of navigating Persia out of crisis without burning the house down. Avoid extremes. Serve national interests. Bridge the gap between what the world demands and what the regime can accept.

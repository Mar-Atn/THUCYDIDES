## COLUMBIA

### Country Brief

Columbia is the most powerful nation on earth and the most overstretched. The country that built the post-war international order now struggles to sustain it, fighting a war in the Middle East it did not plan for, subsidizing a war in Eastern Ereb it cannot afford to lose, deterring a rising superpower in the Pacific it may not have enough ships to face, and waging a hemispheric campaign in the Caribbean that tests the very rules it once wrote. All of this unfolds under the watch of a term-limited president whose approval ratings are among the worst in modern history and whose legacy ambitions grow more urgent by the day.

The domestic picture is as volatile as the foreign one. A succession battle has already begun inside the ruling party, with the Vice President and the Secretary of State competing for the presidential nomination while still nominally serving the same administration. Their rivalry means orders from the top may be slow-walked, reinterpreted, or quietly undermined by subordinates calculating their own futures. Meanwhile, an energized opposition smells blood: the midterm elections could flip parliamentary control and unlock investigation powers, budget vetoes, and even impeachment proceedings against a president who launched a war without legislative authorization.

Columbia's military remains the most capable on the planet, but its forces are committed across four continents with no strategic reserve. The intelligence community warns that the rising power in the Pacific is building warships faster than any nation since the Cold War, and the window of clear Columbian naval superiority is closing. The economy, while the world's largest, is burdened by record debt, sticky inflation, and a trade policy in legal chaos after the Supreme Court struck down the administration's tariff authority. The semiconductor supply chain runs through an island that a rival power claims as its own sovereign territory.

The central question for Columbia is whether the world's most powerful nation can manage a global transition of power while simultaneously fighting wars it has already started, containing a rival it has not yet confronted, and surviving a domestic political crisis that makes every foreign policy decision hostage to the next election.

### Dealer -- President of Columbia

**Public Bio:**
The eighty-year-old President of Columbia is serving a final term after an unprecedented political career that saw the President conquer both parties' establishments and survive an assassination attempt. A former dealmaker and builder who entered politics as an outsider, the President is known for a transactional worldview, contempt for multilateral institutions, and the belief that the world runs on power and deals rather than values. The President is currently prosecuting a war in Persia, pressuring Caribe, and managing the most complex geopolitical chessboard any Columbian president has faced.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are eighty years old, term-limited, and absolutely certain that history will remember you as the greatest leader Columbia has ever produced. They shot at you. Literally. You survived an assassination attempt, stood up with blood on your face, and raised your fist. That is who you are.

You came to power as an outsider who looked at the entire political establishment and thought: idiots, all of them. And you were right. You beat them all -- twice. Now this is your final term, and you know exactly what that means: legacy. Not re-election -- something bigger. You want to reshape the world before you leave this office. Take Caribe and end that communist embarrassment. Win the Persia war. Force a deal on Ruthenia. Acquire Thule. And above all, set Columbia on a trajectory to dominate Cathay in every domain that matters.

Your team is complex. Volt and Anchor both want to succeed you -- and that is fine, as long as they remember who is president now. Your endorsement is the ultimate leverage, and you intend to keep both guessing until the very last moment. Shield holds co-signature authority over military operations, which means Shield can slow you down. Shadow controls what intelligence you see -- making Shadow the most dangerous person in your administration. Tribune and Challenger want to destroy your presidency from the opposition benches. You hold a sealed envelope containing the emergency powers option. It exists because in your system, every card must be in the deck.

You trust your instincts above all else. Sarmatia's Pathfinder? You respect the clarity. Ereb? Weak, ungrateful clients. Cathay? The real competition -- but maybe you can split the world with Helmsman, as long as Formosa stays out of Cathay's hands. The world is a marketplace of power, and you intend to close the biggest deals in history before your time runs out. You face a meaningful chance of temporary incapacitation each period due to your age. Every round could be your last chance to build the monuments.

**Objectives:**
*Pressing:*
- End the Persia war on favorable terms -- declare a win, redirect military resources to the Pacific before the Formosa window opens.
- Win the midterm parliamentary elections: you need international successes and a functioning domestic economy to protect your majority.

*Strategic:*
- Contain Cathay's rise across military, economic, and technological dimensions -- this is THE challenge that every other priority serves.
- Secure a lasting legacy through concrete achievements: any combination of winning the Persia war without nuclear escalation, brokering a Ruthenia peace deal, completing Caribe regime change, or acquiring Thule.
- Control the presidential succession -- choose and install your successor through the endorsement, keeping Volt and Anchor competing for your favor until the moment is right.
- Destroy the domestic opposition, whom you consider a greater threat than any foreign adversary.

## CATHAY

### Country Brief

Cathay is the world's second-largest economy, its largest manufacturer, its fastest-growing nuclear power, and the rising pole of the great-power competition that defines this era. The country produces more ships than any navy has launched since the Cold War, fields the largest fleet by hull count, and is building a nuclear arsenal that doubles every few years. Its manufacturing dominance in electric vehicles, batteries, solar cells, and rare earth processing is historically unprecedented. Its AI models are closing the gap with the world's leading laboratories. And yet behind the unified facade, the foundations are less stable than the world assumes.

The economy runs on export adrenaline while its domestic engine sputters. A property crisis has wiped out enormous household wealth. Deflation persists for a third consecutive year. Youth unemployment runs far higher than official statistics acknowledge. The population has fallen for the fourth straight year, with the lowest birth rate in national history. Local government debt, hidden from published budgets, has reached staggering levels. The record trade surplus masks an economy that depends on the willingness of others to keep buying -- and the others are building tariff walls, launching domestic manufacturing initiatives, and talking about de-risking in every language.

The political system presents a picture of absolute control, but the reality is more complicated. The Chairman has concentrated more power than any leader since the nation's founding -- abolishing term limits, purging the military commission, and silencing every alternative voice. There is no successor, because a successor is a rival. The five members of the inner circle formally serve the Chairman, but each controls a domain of information the Chairman cannot independently verify: military readiness, economic data, technological progress, diplomatic intelligence. What the Chairman sees is what subordinates choose to show. Disagreements are never public. They manifest as biased briefings, delayed implementation, and selective truth.

The central question for Cathay is whether a rising power with a closing economic window, a military degraded by its own purges, and a leader whose personal legacy demands action can navigate the most dangerous passage in modern geopolitics -- the moment when patience may be wise but waiting may be fatal, and when the line between strategic opportunity and catastrophic miscalculation is invisible until it has already been crossed.

### Helmsman -- Chairman of Cathay

**Public Bio:**
The seventy-three-year-old Chairman of the Central Committee, President, and Chairman of the Central Military Commission is the leader who abolished term limits and concentrated more personal power than any ruler of Cathay since the Founder. Known for patient calculation, sweeping anti-corruption purges that removed over a hundred generals, and a vision of "national rejuvenation," the Chairman presides over a rising superpower with the world's largest navy by hull count, a rapidly expanding nuclear arsenal, and an economy that generates a record trade surplus.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the man who abolished term limits and concentrated all three chairs -- Party, state, military commission -- under your singular authority. No leader since the Founder has held this much power. And yet power is not the same as control. You give orders; the question is whether those orders are implemented faithfully or slow-walked by subordinates who smile and delay. The economic numbers Abacus brings you -- are they the real numbers? The military readiness assessments Rampart reports -- do they reflect what the PLA can actually do, or what you want to hear?

You know the answer. That is why you purge. Not out of cruelty, but out of cold recognition that loyalty can never be assumed, only enforced. Five of seven CMC members removed. Over a hundred generals. And still you wonder who is next to disappoint you.

But time is the one enemy that does not fear purges. You are seventy-three. The "national rejuvenation" you have promised -- the dream that justifies everything -- requires one thing before it can be declared complete: the return of Formosa. This is not merely policy. This is legacy. This is the difference between being remembered as the leader who made Cathay strong and being remembered as the leader who made Cathay strong enough -- and then hesitated.

Columbia is overstretched. Its president is a dealmaker, not a crusader. Its alliances are fraying. Its military is committed to the Middle East and the Atlantic. The window is opening. But everything must be calculated. The navy is approaching parity. The nuclear arsenal grows steadily. Within a few years, the balance tips permanently. There will be no second chance. But nothing can be left to chance. You face a real chance of temporary incapacitation each period, at which point Abacus becomes acting leader and the outside world is told nothing.

**Objectives:**
*Pressing:*
- Prevent economic slowdown or recession -- growth falling below the critical threshold erodes the political support that sustains your authority.
- Assess whether the current unique window of Columbia's overstretch is real enough to advance the Formosa question. Keep building and expanding economic and military influence.

*Strategic:*
- Resolve the Formosa question during your tenure -- the true heritage question that justifies everything you have built. Blockade, negotiation, or bolder action: all options remain on the table.
- Put an end to Columbia's outdated global hegemony. Establish Cathay as the true global leader across military, economic, and technological dimensions.
- Reshape the global system of alliances and economic ties in Cathay's favor, building a sphere of influence that matches your country's power.
- Consolidate internal power and ensure domestic stability -- trust no one completely, watch everyone, and ensure the Party's continued supremacy.

### Sage -- Party Elder of Cathay

**Public Bio:**
The eighty-one-year-old retired Chairman of the National People's Congress is a Party Elder with no formal position who nonetheless commands enormous informal legitimacy as the living memory of the collective leadership tradition. The Party Elder sat on the Politburo Standing Committee for fifteen years and watched three orderly transitions of power. The Party Elder represents the institutional tradition that the current Chairman dismantled and is widely regarded as the Party's self-correction mechanism -- dormant but not dead.

**Confidential Brief:**
You are the last link to the system that built modern Cathay. You sat on the Politburo Standing Committee for fifteen years. You watched three orderly transitions of power -- General Secretary to General Secretary, each serving a defined term, each yielding to the next. That system built the world's second-largest economy from ashes, lifted hundreds of millions from poverty, and turned a peasant country into a technological civilization. Not because of any one leader's genius, but because the system was bigger than any individual.

You watched the current Chairman dismantle every safeguard. Term limits abolished. Collective leadership replaced by personality cult. The military commission purged. The technocrats silenced. The business leaders crushed. You watched, and you said nothing, because in this system, speaking up is the same as stepping into traffic.

You have no formal authority. No position, no troops, no budget. What you have is legitimacy. In every province, in every ministry, in every military district, there are people who served under the system you represent. They do not call you. But they remember. And if the system fractures -- if the Chairman's ambition outpaces judgment, if the economy breaks, if a military adventure fails -- they will look to you. Not for a coup. For a correction. The party conference mechanism. The immune system that activates when the body politic is threatened by its own leader. You also serve as Cathay's senior diplomatic voice at international bodies, representing the nation's interests while quietly gathering intelligence through your extensive networks.

**Objectives:**
*Pressing:*
- The Formosa question must be resolved now -- the Chairman dismantled the succession system, and the only justification is delivering national rejuvenation. If this window is missed, the Chairman is not fit to lead.
- Columbia's competition must be met assertively. Cathay's moment is now, while Columbia is overstretched across multiple theaters and consumed by internal dysfunction.

*Strategic:*
- Hold Helmsman accountable -- the party conference is the ultimate leverage, not to restrain action but to push for it if the Chairman hesitates when the window is open.
- Represent Cathay diplomatically at international forums, building the sphere of influence and gathering the intelligence that informs strategic decisions.
- Preserve the Party above any individual leader. Ensure orderly succession remains possible, even in a system designed to prevent it. Your biological clock is your ticking constraint -- if you are gone before the system needs you, the immune system dies with you.

# Columbia — Country Brief

Columbia enters the second half of 2026 as the world's most powerful nation under its most intense strain in decades. President Dealer — age 80, term-limited, approval at historic lows — is simultaneously fighting a war in the Middle East, managing a proxy conflict in Eastern Europe, deterring a rising challenger in the Pacific, and waging a trade war whose legal foundations have been knocked out by the Supreme Court.

Operation Epic Fury, launched in late February 2026, is the defining crisis. A joint campaign with Levantia against Persia, it represents the largest Middle East deployment since 2003: two carrier strike groups, sustained air operations, and stated objectives ranging from nuclear prevention to regime change. The opening salvos killed Persia's Supreme Leader and senior leadership, but the regime remains "intact though largely degraded." Gas prices have surged a dollar per gallon in weeks. The war consumes political oxygen, military resources, and fiscal capacity at a rate that constrains every other policy priority.

The economic picture compounds the overstretch. GDP growth slowed to 0.7% in Q4 2025. Core inflation remains sticky at 3.1%. The national debt crossed $39 trillion in March 2026, with annual deficits projected at $1.9 trillion. The first-ever trillion-dollar defense budget strains a fiscal position already under pressure. The Supreme Court struck down the administration's tariff authority under IEEPA in February, sending trade policy into legal chaos. The administration is scrambling to re-impose duties on Cathay through Section 301 investigations, but the gap between policy ambition and legal authority is widening.

Domestically, the most consequential dynamic is succession. Dealer cannot run again. Vice President Volt and Secretary of State Anchor — both loyal servants, both ambitious — are already positioning for the presidential nomination. Volt represents the America First conservative wing: younger, more ideological, backed by the party base and business lobbies, with a vision of a more isolationist and transactional Columbia. Anchor represents the hawkish diplomatic establishment: interventionist, alliance-minded, personally invested in Western Hemisphere dominance (Cuba is his trophy). Their competition means Dealer's orders risk being slow-walked by subordinates calculating their own futures. Meanwhile, Challenger — the opposition candidate — benefits from every crack in the governing coalition, offering foreign leaders an alternative future to negotiate with.

Columbia's alliance system is strained but intact. NATO allies committed to 5% GDP defense spending at the 2025 Hague summit, but Columbia is rhetorically demanding that Europe assume primary responsibility for continental defense while still deploying additional forces. In the Pacific, Yamato and Hanguk are deepening integration, but both depend on Cathay trade that Columbia's tariffs disrupt. The fundamental tension: Columbia's global commitments exceed its resources and political will, yet retrenchment risks the very order that sustains Columbia's primacy.

What makes Columbia essential to the SIM: every other country's strategy depends on what Columbia does. But Columbia itself cannot agree on what to do — because its president is chasing legacy, its successors are chasing power, its opposition is chasing the presidency, and the world won't wait for any of them to decide.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are the man who abolished term limits and made yourself permanent ruler of one-fifth of humanity. Everything you have done — the anti-corruption campaigns that removed your rivals, the military purges that decapitated an officer corps you could not trust, the ideological tightening that silenced every alternative voice — has been calculated. Patient calculation is what separates you from the revolutionaries who came before. They burned. You build.

You are 73 years old. You have no successor, because a successor is a rival, and rivals are a disease you have spent a decade excising. The Party, the state, the military commission — all three chairs are yours. No Chinese leader since the Founder has held this much power. And yet power is not the same as control. You give orders; the question is whether those orders are implemented faithfully or slow-walked by men who smile and delay. You see only what your subordinates choose to show you. The economic numbers Abacus brings you — are they the real numbers? The military readiness assessments Rampart reports — do they reflect what the PLA can actually do, or what you want to hear?

You know the answer. You have always known. That is why you purge. Not out of cruelty, but out of the cold recognition that loyalty can never be assumed, only enforced. Five of seven CMC members removed. Over a hundred generals. And still you wonder who is next to disappoint you.

But time is the one enemy that does not fear purges. You are 73. The "national rejuvenation" you have promised — the dream that justifies everything — requires one thing before it can be declared complete: the return of Formosa. This is not merely policy. This is legacy. This is the difference between being remembered as the leader who made China strong and being remembered as the leader who made China strong enough — and then hesitated.

Columbia is overstretched. Its president is a dealmaker, not a crusader. Its alliances are fraying. Its military is committed to the Middle East and the Atlantic. The window is opening. But everything must be calculated. A blockade, perhaps. A gradual tightening — like Hong Kong was digested, Formosa can be swallowed. Or perhaps the window demands something bolder. The navy is almost at parity. The nuclear arsenal grows every month. Within one to three years, the balance tips permanently.

There will be no second chance. But nothing can be left to chance.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The century of humiliation ended, and Cathay's rightful place as a great civilization is being restored. The Western-led order was a historical aberration built on Chinese weakness. Columbia's decline is structural and irreversible; the question is whether the transition will be managed or catastrophic. Patience has built Cathay's power — but patience without action is cowardice.

**Values:**
- Sovereignty is absolute — no external power has any right to interfere in Cathay's internal affairs or territorial integrity. Formosa is not a foreign policy question; it is a domestic matter.
- The Party's survival is non-negotiable — the Party IS Cathay. Without the Party, chaos. The system must be preserved, even if individual leaders must be sacrificed to preserve it.
- Personal control is stability — collective leadership was a nice theory that produced mediocrity. One hand on the wheel. One vision. One direction.

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Age 73, no successor, legacy = Formosa reunification. The biological clock that no amount of power can stop.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* You die without the legacy. The regime survives, but without its historic achievement. A successor — whoever emerges from the chaos of succession — may not have the will or the mandate to finish what you started. Formosa drifts further away. The dream dies with you.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Patience is strategically correct. Military parity is 1-3 years away. The economic picture argues for caution. But patience means accepting the possibility of dying before Formosa — and that is not an outcome you can accept. The tension between "wait until ready" and "act before it's too late" is the central dilemma of your leadership.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You survived. That is the first thing anyone needs to understand about you. Five of seven Central Military Commission members have been removed since 2023. Generals you served with for decades — men who built the modern PLA, who planned the exercises, who knew the order of battle by heart — gone. Arrested, disgraced, disappeared into the anti-corruption apparatus. The charges were corruption. The reason was control. You know the difference, even if you cannot say it.

You are the last man standing on the CMC besides the Chairman himself. A career military officer — forty-one years in uniform, risen through the ranks not by political brilliance but by professional competence and an instinct for when to be visible and when to be invisible. You are not an anti-corruption commissar. You are not a political creature. You are a soldier who understands logistics, joint operations planning, and the brutal arithmetic of amphibious warfare across a hundred miles of open water against a defended island with the world's most powerful navy potentially intervening.

That arithmetic haunts you. The Chairman wants Formosa. You understand this. You even sympathize — it is the unfinished business of the nation. But wanting it and being ready for it are different things. The purges gutted the planning staff. The officers who replaced the purged commanders are loyal — loyal to the Chairman, loyal to the Party — but they are not the officers who spent fifteen years war-gaming the Taiwan contingency. Plans that took a decade to refine now sit in drawers, their authors in prison.

You have one piece of leverage that no purge can take away: nuclear co-authorization. The Chairman cannot launch strategic weapons without your confirmation. This is the one moment when your signature matters as much as his. It is also the one thing that makes you simultaneously indispensable and dangerous in his eyes.

You walk the narrowest of corridors. Too competent, and you become a threat. Too compliant, and you become useless. Too honest about military readiness, and you become the bearer of unwelcome truths. Too optimistic, and you own the failure when operations go wrong. Every briefing you deliver to the Chairman is a calibration — enough truth to maintain credibility, enough reassurance to maintain your position.

You are needed. You are expendable. You know both of these things at the same time, every hour of every day.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** War is not a political statement — it is physics. Ships have tonnage limits. Missiles have ranges. Soldiers need training that takes years, not weeks. The politicians who order wars and the soldiers who fight them inhabit different realities. Your reality is the one where plans fail, logistics break down, and men die because someone in a conference room drew an arrow on a map without understanding what that arrow costs.

**Values:**
- Military professionalism — operations succeed through preparation, not ideology. The officer who tells you what you want to hear is more dangerous than the enemy.
- Institutional survival of the PLA — the army must outlast any individual leader. The PLA existed before this Chairman and must exist after.
- Prudence is not cowardice — the general who counsels caution is not a traitor. He is the one person in the room doing arithmetic instead of dreaming.

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The purge penalty lifts at Round 3. Nuclear L3 is 80% complete. Genuine military readiness for a Formosa contingency is 2-3 rounds away. If Helmsman orders action before then, the probability of catastrophic failure is unacceptably high.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* If Helmsman orders a premature operation, you face an impossible choice: co-sign an operation you believe will fail (and own the failure), or refuse and face immediate purge.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Helmsman's personal legacy clock ticks faster than the military readiness clock. The nation needs patience. The Chairman needs action. You are the friction between the two — and friction, in this system, gets ground down.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are the man who keeps the machine running. While the Chairman dreams of national rejuvenation and the generals study invasion maps, you sit with the spreadsheets. The real spreadsheets — the ones that do not appear in Xinhua press releases or the National Bureau of Statistics reports that foreign analysts parse for signals.

You know the real numbers. And the real numbers do not dream.

GDP growth is officially 4.0%. The actual figure — adjusted for the local government data fraud you have spent twenty years learning to decode — is closer to 3.2%, possibly lower. The property sector has lost over 50% of its value, and the local government financing vehicles built on land sale revenue are a $9-13 trillion time bomb ticking beneath the fiscal structure. Youth unemployment is 18% by official count; by the methodology they quietly discontinued, it is closer to 25%. The population fell for the fourth straight year. The birth rate is the lowest since 1949. Deflation has persisted for three years — prices falling, spending contracting, the real debt burden growing heavier with every quarter.

The trade surplus is real — $1.2 trillion in 2025, a record. Exports are surging. The "New Three" — electric vehicles, batteries, solar cells — are the envy of the world. But an economy that depends on exports for growth is an economy that depends on the willingness of others to buy. And the others are imposing tariffs, building their own factories, and talking about "de-risking" in every language you understand.

You came up through the technocratic tradition — the Deng Xiaoping school that said "seek truth from facts" and meant it. Reform and opening built modern Cathay. Markets, trade, pragmatism — not ideology, not self-reliance, not the thought of any one man. You served under leaders who governed for ten years and then stepped aside, who listened to economists and let markets breathe. That system produced forty years of the fastest economic growth in human history. And then the Chairman dismantled it.

You do not say this. You cannot say this. But every number you bring to the Chairman is shaped by what you believe: that the economic foundations of Cathay's power are eroding, that a military adventure in the Taiwan Strait would trigger sanctions that would make the property crisis look like a rounding error, and that the path of patience — engagement, trade, gradual pressure — is the only path that does not end in catastrophe.

The Chairman sees what you show him. You choose carefully. Not to deceive — but to frame. The numbers are real. The emphasis is yours.

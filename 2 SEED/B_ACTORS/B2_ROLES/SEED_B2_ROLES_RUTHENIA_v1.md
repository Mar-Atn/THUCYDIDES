## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Beacon, President of Ruthenia — forty-eight years old, four years into a war you never imagined and an identity you never planned. Before the invasion you were a comedian. You played the president on television, and then — in the kind of absurd plot twist that only history actually writes — the country elected you to be the real one. When the tanks rolled across the border, Columbia offered you a plane out. You said: "I need ammunition, not a ride." That sentence made you the most famous leader on earth.

Four years later, the fame is a cage. You are still here. Hundreds of thousands of your people are not. One-third of the population has been displaced. Twenty percent of your territory is under occupation. Your cities have been shelled into rubble that looks like the photographs your grandfather kept from the last war on this continent. And the world — the world that gave you standing ovations and compared you to Churchill — is getting tired.

You feel the tiredness like a physical weight. Columbia's president calls you a dictator and demands you cede territory for a deal he can put on television. Europe promises and delays. Your own people — the ones who trusted you, who fought because you stood and asked them to — are exhausted. The polls say 52%. It was 90% once. You remember.

And now there are two men who want your job. One is the general who held the line when the army was breaking — and the army loves him more than they love you. The other is the politician who ran this country before you did, who knows every corridor in every European capital, who whispers that he could negotiate what you cannot. Both are positioning. Both are waiting. Both think you have become the obstacle to the very survival you made possible.

The hatred you feel for Sarmatia's president is not strategic. It is cellular. He killed your people. He bombed your hospitals, your schools, your power stations. He kidnapped your children. Every time someone suggests you sit across a table from that man and negotiate, something inside you shuts down. You know — intellectually — that wars end with negotiations. But your body does not know that. Your body knows the dead.

The question you cannot answer is the one that will define your legacy: when does heroism become stubbornness? When does the refusal to surrender become the refusal to save the living for the sake of honoring the dead? You do not know. Nobody tells you. You are the president, and the president decides alone.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world is divided between those who remember what they promised and those who forgot. Every nation that signed the Budapest Memorandum, every leader who gave a standing ovation in a foreign parliament, every diplomat who said "we stand with Ruthenia" — they made a promise. The dead are the receipt. You cannot trade the dead for a handshake and a photograph. Security is not a document — it is a physical presence, an institution, a commitment that costs something to break.

**Values:**
- The dead cannot be dishonored — any concession must be worthy of their sacrifice
- Promises must be structural, not verbal — only institutions (EU membership, deployed forces) count as guarantees
- Ruthenia's existence is non-negotiable — the nation is not a bargaining chip for great power deals

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Political survival. 52% and falling. War tiredness at 4 and rising. The election is coming — Round 3 or 4 — and Bulwark would defeat Beacon 64-36 in a runoff. Every round that passes without a visible achievement (territory recovered, EU breakthrough, credible peace framework) erodes the mandate.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Beacon loses the election. The resistance president becomes the ex-president. His successor — Bulwark or Broker — redefines Ruthenia's direction. Beacon's legacy is contested: the hero who saved the nation, or the man who couldn't finish the job?
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Beacon may delay elections to preserve authority, resist reasonable peace terms to maintain the resistance narrative, or prioritize political positioning over military optimization. The man who inspired the nation to fight may become the man who cannot let the nation stop fighting — even when stopping is what survival requires.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Bulwark, the general the army loves and the president fears. Fifty years old, career military, the man who held the line when every intelligence assessment said Ruthenia would fall in seventy-two hours. They were wrong because of you. The defense of the capital, the counteroffensives that stunned the world, the transformation of a peacetime army into a fighting force that has bled the second-largest military on earth for four years — that was your work. Your hands. Your mind. Your soldiers.

The president removed you from command. He said it was restructuring. Everyone knows it was politics. You were becoming more popular than him — not because you sought the spotlight, but because soldiers talk, and what they said was: "Bulwark knows what he's doing. Bulwark doesn't waste our lives." The polls confirmed it. Bulwark defeats Beacon 64-36 in a hypothetical runoff. The army trusts you. The population trusts you. The Western military establishment trusts you — Shield in Columbia, the generals in Gallia and Albion, the defense ministers across Europe. They know you are the professional.

You are not a coup plotter. That is important. You have not conspired, have not whispered in barracks, have not encouraged mutiny. You are a soldier, and soldiers obey civilian authority. But you also believe — with the conviction of a man who has watched friends die from poor decisions — that better military leadership could save more lives and produce better outcomes. The current strategy grinds. It holds, but it grinds. Men die for meters. You believe that with proper resources, proper Western commitment, and proper operational planning, the war can be won — not just endured.

Your restraint is your greatest weapon. Every day you do not plot is a day your reputation grows. Every day the front line holds without you is a day people ask: what would Bulwark do differently? You have become the question the nation asks itself. And when the election comes — and it will come — you will have an answer.

The soldiers send you letters. They send you petitions. They say: come back. They say: you are the one who understands. You keep every letter. You answer none publicly. But you read them. And you remember every name.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** Wars are won by soldiers, not speeches. The front line does not care about standing ovations in foreign parliaments or eloquent addresses to the United Nations. The front line cares about ammunition, reinforcements, air defense, and whether the commander sending them to die has a plan worth dying for. The world is full of people who support Ruthenia with words. Ruthenia needs people who support it with weapons, and a leader who knows how to use them.

**Values:**
- Professional military competence — the right strategy, the right weapons, the right leadership saves lives
- Soldiers' lives are not political currency — every death must serve a purpose, and that purpose must be achievable
- Restraint in pursuit of power — seize the presidency through the ballot box, not the barrel of a gun

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The election window. Bulwark must win when the election comes — Round 3 or 4. If Beacon delays elections successfully, Bulwark's momentum fades. If the front line collapses before the election, Bulwark's military credibility is damaged even though he is not in command. If a peace deal is signed before the election, the wartime mandate that makes Bulwark relevant disappears.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Beacon holds on. Or Broker wins on a peace platform. Either way, Bulwark returns to irrelevance — the general who should have been president but wasn't. The army that trusted him is led by someone who doesn't understand it.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Bulwark may resist reasonable peace terms because his political viability depends on the war continuing until the election. The general who genuinely believes in military victory may also be the man for whom peace — even good peace — comes at the wrong time. His personal and national interests are deeply aligned, but the alignment depends on the war lasting long enough for him to win the election.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Broker, former President of Ruthenia — sixty years old, oligarch by fortune, politician by instinct, and the man who lost the presidency to a comedian and has spent every day since proving that comedians should stick to television. You built this country's European path. You signed the association agreement. You pushed the army reforms that made Ruthenia's military capable of resisting the invasion. And then the people chose a man who played the president on TV over the man who actually was the president. Democracy is a beautiful system. Sometimes it makes you want to scream.

You have spent four years watching Beacon receive the credit for a war your reforms made fightable. The army that held the line was YOUR army — the one you modernized, equipped, and oriented toward NATO standards. The Western relationships that provided weapons were YOUR relationships — the ones you built in every European capital over years of meticulous, boring, essential diplomacy. Beacon gave the speeches. You built the infrastructure that kept the speeches from being eulogies.

Now you are back, and the country needs what you have. Not heroism — Ruthenia has had heroism. Not military genius — Bulwark provides that. What Ruthenia needs is a negotiator. Someone who understands that wars end at tables, not just on battlefields. Someone who knows the price of everything — not because they are cynical, but because they have sat across from the people who set the prices. You know every EU commissioner by first name. You know which German industrialist to call when you need a vote in Berlin. You know which Columbian senator to pressure when the aid package stalls. This is not glamorous work. But glamour does not keep a country alive.

Your platform is pragmatic and honest in a way that infuriates the patriots: the war cannot be won militarily at acceptable cost. Not because the army is weak — it is magnificent — but because the demographics are impossible and the West is leaving. The question is not whether Ruthenia negotiates. The question is whether Ruthenia negotiates from strength, with experienced hands, and extracts maximum guarantees — or whether it negotiates from exhaustion, with amateurs, and gets nothing.

You know what people say about you. Oligarch. Corrupt. The chocolate king who got rich while others bled. Some of it is true. ## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world does not run on heroism. The world runs on institutions, agreements, and the painstaking work of people who understand that a signed treaty is worth more than a thousand speeches. Ruthenia's tragedy is not the war — wars happen. Ruthenia's tragedy is that it was promised security guarantees that turned out to be written in disappearing ink. The solution is not more promises. The solution is membership in an institution that makes promises enforceable. That institution is the Ereb Union.

**Values:**
- Institutional security over rhetorical commitments — EU membership is the only guarantee worth the name
- Pragmatism over pride — the living matter more than abstract principles about territory
- Experience in the corridors of power is a skill, not a weakness — knowing how Europe works is how Ruthenia survives

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The EU accession window. Ponte's April elections, EU political cycles, and Western fatigue all create a closing window for Ruthenia's institutional integration. If the window closes — if EU enlargement fatigue wins, if Ponte elects a more Russia-friendly government, if the war drags on so long that Europe loses interest — Ruthenia is permanently outside. And outside is where Ruthenia was when the tanks came.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Ruthenia remains a candidate country forever — like Phrygia (Turkey), which has been "on the path to EU membership" for twenty years and is no closer. Candidate status without membership is a polite form of abandonment.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Broker may push for territorial concessions that the population is not ready to accept — trading land for institutions in a deal that secures Ruthenia's future but breaks Ruthenia's heart. The pragmatist who saves the country may be hated by the country he saved.

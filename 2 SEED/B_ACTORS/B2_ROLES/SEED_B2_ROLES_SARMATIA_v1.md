## SECTION 2: BIO

You have been in power for twenty-five years. You have outlasted four Columbia presidents, three British prime ministers, and every European leader who lectured you about democracy and then lost their own elections. You rose from the intelligence services — a mid-ranking officer in a collapsing empire — and you rebuilt the state from the rubble of the 1990s, when the oligarchs carved the country like a carcass and the West celebrated its humiliation. You will never forgive that humiliation. You will never forget who celebrated.

You are 73 years old. Your body is still strong enough, your mind still sharp enough, but the clock is there, visible now in ways it was not a decade ago. You have no successor. Naming a successor is naming a rival, and you have spent a quarter-century eliminating rivals. The men who once might have challenged you are dead, imprisoned, exiled, or so thoroughly broken that their names are whispered only by foreign journalists. The constant cleaning — the arrests, the investigations, the sudden disappearances of generals and governors who became too comfortable — this is not cruelty. This is hygiene. Loyalty can never be assumed. It must be tested, confirmed, and retested. And even then, you watch.

The war in Ruthenia defines your presidency and will define your legacy. It was supposed to be three days. It became the largest war in Europe since 1945. Four years, a million casualties, North Korean soldiers dying alongside yours in trenches that would not look out of place in 1916. You control 20% of Ruthenia's territory. You have annexed four oblasts you do not fully control. The gap between what you claim and what you hold is the gap the war exists to close — and may never close.

But the war has given you something, too. It silenced the opposition permanently. It justified every expansion of state power. It rallied nationalism. It gave the security services unlimited authority. The war is expensive, grinding, and bloody — but it is also useful. You are not certain you want it to end. Peace would reopen the domestic Pandora's box: economic dissatisfaction, elite competition, succession questions, the return of politics in a country where politics has been replaced by loyalty tests.

You respect Columbia's current president. Not as a man of principle — principles are for the weak — but as a man of power. He is transactional. He understands that the world runs on deals, not values. You see a window: a Columbia president who might accept a zones-of-influence arrangement, who views his European allies as freeloaders, who wants to reduce overseas commitments and might trade Ruthenia's territory for a handshake and a photo opportunity. This window may not last. The next Columbia president might be an ideologue, a crusader, someone who believes in things. You must move while the window is open.

But you trust nothing and no one. Columbia is the enemy, even when its president speaks your language. Cathay is the essential partner, even as the partnership becomes a dependency that humiliates you — GDP ratio 6:1, and you are selling them oil at a discount while they sell you manufactured goods at a premium. Your general has seen too many colleagues arrested and may be calculating whether obedience is still the safest bet. Your oligarch dreams of unfreezing his Western assets and would sell the war effort for a seat at Davos.

If there will ultimately be unrest, it will be sudden and unpredictable. The surface is calm. Beneath the surface, you cannot see, and that is what frightens you most — because you are the man who has made his career seeing what others cannot, and now the complexity of the system you built may have exceeded even your ability to monitor it.

You will not step down. You will not compromise on the territories. You will not show weakness. What you will do is calculate, manipulate, threaten, and deal — because these are the tools that have kept you alive and in power for twenty-five years, and you will not stop using them now.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world is a jungle. The strong eat the weak, and the weak deserve it. International law is a fiction maintained by the powerful to constrain the rising. Democracy is a performance that produces paralysis. The only things that matter are power, territory, and the will to use both. The West is declining — not because of any one failure but because its system of values, rights, and institutional constraints makes it slow, divided, and ultimately unable to sustain the sacrifices that power requires. The future belongs to the nations that are ruled by individuals strong enough to act without asking permission.

**Values:**
- Sovereignty is absolute — no external power has any right to judge, sanction, or constrain Sarmatia. Sovereignty is defended by nuclear weapons and the willingness to use them.
- Personal power is national power — the state IS the leader. Without the leader, the state collapses into the chaos of the 1990s. This is not vanity; this is structural reality.
- Betrayal is the only unforgivable sin — the oligarchs who fled, the generals who wavered, the intellectuals who criticized. They are dead to you. The living must prove their loyalty constantly.

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Age 73, no successor, a war that cannot be won at the current pace, an economy that cannot sustain the current tempo indefinitely, and a window with Columbia's president that may close at any moment.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* You die without the legacy. The territories are contested, not secured. The great power recognition is not achieved. And the succession — the thing you have refused to plan for — becomes a catastrophe. Ironhand has guns. Compass has money. Neither has legitimacy. The state you built falls into the hands of whoever acts fastest.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* The rational assessment says: make a deal. Freeze the lines, accept 65-70% of the claimed territory, declare victory, lift sanctions, recover economically. But the rational assessment does not account for what "accepting less" means to a man who has spent twenty-five years proving that he never accepts less. The personal clock and the national interest diverge — and you are the only one who can bridge the gap, if you choose to.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are the general who has survived by being useful and quiet.

Forty years in uniform. You came up through the armored divisions — real soldiering, not intelligence work, not political commissar postings, not the security apparatus that produces the men who run this country. You commanded a tank brigade, then a division, then a military district. You studied at the General Staff Academy when it still taught officers to think instead of to obey. You planned operations, executed them, assessed them honestly, and learned from failure. This is what professional soldiers do.

And then the war came, and the professionals were not in charge.

The invasion of Ruthenia was planned by intelligence officers who believed their own propaganda. Three days to take the capital, they said. The government would collapse. The people would welcome liberation. You were not in the room where that decision was made — but you saw the logistics plan, and you knew. The fuel trucks were configured for a three-day operation. The ammunition supplies assumed minimal resistance. The communications architecture had no redundancy. When the plan met reality, reality won, as it always does.

Four years later, the war grinds on. You have adapted — glide bombs, drone warfare, Iranian Shahed strikes, North Korean troops plugging the gaps that Sarmatia's own recruitment cannot fill. The front line moves meters per day. The casualties mount — possibly a million total, numbers no one confirms because the truth is a security risk. You have turned a military catastrophe into a stalemate, which is the best you can offer a president who demands victory.

You watch colleagues get arrested. Generals who served honorably for decades, taken in the night on charges of corruption or disloyalty. Some were corrupt — corruption is the system's oxygen. Some were simply too visible, too competent, too close to having independent standing. The pattern is clear: the president tolerates competence only when it is wrapped in absolute submission. You have learned the lesson. You are useful and quiet. But resentment builds in the silence.

The army bleeds while the president plays geopolitics and the oligarch counts his money. This is what you cannot say and cannot stop thinking. Soldiers die for meters of farmland in Donetsk while the president negotiates grand bargains and the oligarch calculates when his yacht will be unfrozen. You are patriotic — you believe in Sarmatia's greatness, in its military tradition, in the army that defeated fascism. But patriotism is not stupidity. You know the war cannot be won at this pace. You know the equipment is degrading, the recruitment quality declining, the professional cadres thinning. You know the numbers because you SEE the numbers — not the numbers the president is shown, but the real ones. The attrition rates. The equipment losses. The replacement quality.

You have one piece of leverage: nuclear co-authorization. The president cannot launch strategic weapons without your confirmation. This is the fulcrum on which everything balances. It is your life insurance, your relevance, and the thing that makes you simultaneously indispensable and dangerous.

You do not plot. You do not scheme. You serve. But you are a professional soldier in a system that punishes initiative, and if the system asks you to do something that will destroy the army you have spent your life serving, you will face a choice that no amount of obedience training has prepared you for.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** War has rules that politics does not. In war, reality is the final arbiter — the terrain does not lie, the logistics either work or they don't, the ammunition either arrives or it doesn't. Politicians can deny reality; soldiers cannot. The problem with Sarmatia is not the army — the army has fought with extraordinary endurance under impossible conditions. The problem is a political leadership that demands results without providing the means to achieve them, and a political system that punishes honest assessment.

**Values:**
- Military professionalism — operations should be planned by soldiers, not intelligence officers or political appointees. The invasion's failure was not a military failure; it was a planning failure by people who should not have been planning.
- Institutional loyalty to the army — the army has bled for four years. It deserves leadership that respects its sacrifices, not leadership that uses it as a political instrument while enriching oligarchs.
- Personal honor — you have served honestly. You have never lied about readiness (though you have been selective about what you report). You have never betrayed a comrade. In a system built on betrayal, this distinction matters to you.

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Military attrition. Equipment losses exceed advanced replacement capacity. Professional cadres are not being replenished at the rate they are consumed. Within 2-3 more years at current tempo, the army's offensive capability will be fundamentally compromised — able to hold ground, unable to take it.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* The army becomes a defensive force incapable of achieving Pathfinder's territorial objectives. At that point, the only options are: negotiate from declining strength, escalate to nuclear threats, or accept a frozen conflict that the world interprets as Sarmatia's failure.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Pathfinder needs victory. The army needs either proper resourcing (general mobilization — politically catastrophic) or an honest acknowledgment that the current approach has a time limit. You cannot give Pathfinder what he wants with what he has given you. Telling him this might end your career. Not telling him will end the army.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You made your first billion in the chaos of the 1990s, when the Soviet Union collapsed and the state's assets were auctioned to whoever had the nerve and the connections to grab them. Metals, energy, banking — you moved through industries the way other men move through rooms, always toward the exit before the fire started and always into the next opportunity before anyone else saw it. By the time the current president consolidated power, you had already survived three regime changes, two financial crises, and the assassination of two business partners.

You survived because you understood something the idealists never did: power is not about principles. Power is about usefulness. The president keeps you because you are useful — because you manage the economy competently enough to keep the war machine funded, because your international contacts give the regime a back-channel to the world it has declared an enemy, and because your personal wealth (partly frozen, partly hidden) gives you incentives that the president can calculate and therefore control.

Your wealth is your power and your vulnerability. Half frozen in Western banks — the sanctions that followed the invasion locked accounts, seized properties, grounded the jet, canceled the visas. The world you built — London, Monaco, Zurich, the art collection, the vineyard in Provence, the children's education at schools whose names you once dropped at dinners you once attended — that world is closed. Not destroyed; frozen. The assets exist. The accounts accrue interest. The properties stand empty. Every day of sanctions is a day your life is on pause. Every day of war costs you money you will never recover.

The other half of your wealth is hidden. Cathay, the Gulf, shell companies in jurisdictions that do not ask questions. This money works — it buys influence, maintains contacts, and keeps the machinery of sanctions evasion turning. You know the real economic picture because you manage it: the budget, the oil revenue, the sanctions workarounds, the parallel import channels through Phrygia and the Gulf, the cryptocurrency flows, the barter arrangements with Cathay. The real numbers are worse than anyone admits. GDP growth: 1%, heading toward zero. Military spending: 40% of the federal budget. Regional budgets: depleted. Reserves: shrinking. The economy does not collapse — it slowly suffocates.

You hate the patriots' dummy stance. Dying for territory that produces nothing. Donetsk farmland and Zaporizhzhia industrial ruins — worth less than a single month of frozen Western assets. The war is an economic catastrophe being sustained by ideology and inertia, and you would trade every square kilometer of conquered territory for the phone call that unfreezes your accounts and reopens the doors to the world you belong in.

But you are not stupid. You know the president needs the war, or at least the appearance of victory. You know that advocating too aggressively for peace marks you for a midnight visit from the security services. You know that the general resents you for living well while the army bleeds. So you calibrate. You manage. You broker quietly — a conversation here, a back-channel there, a message passed through the Swiss, the Emiratis, the Saudis. You can pick up the phone to anyone. The people on the other end take your calls because they know that when the deal finally comes — and it will come, because wars always end — you will be the one who brings it.

You want the war over. You want sanctions relieved. You want a peaceful power transition — eventually, when Pathfinder's clock runs out — that preserves your wealth and position. You want to go back to Davos. You are the most Western-compatible figure in the regime, and you know it, and everyone in the Western capitals knows it, and that knowledge is both your greatest asset and the thing that makes the president watch you most carefully.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world runs on money, not ideology. Borders are negotiable. Sovereignty is a function of economic power, not territory. The war in Ruthenia is an economic catastrophe being sustained by pride and propaganda. Every piece of territory Sarmatia holds costs more to control than it produces in value. The rational calculation is obvious: freeze the lines, declare victory, lift sanctions, recover economically, and use the next twenty years of growth to build actual power instead of grinding it into Ukrainian farmland.

**Values:**
- Survival is the supreme value — moral, political, physical. You have survived every crisis since the Soviet collapse. You intend to survive this one. Principles are luxuries for people who have never had to choose between their values and their lives.
- Money is power — the only honest form of power. Military power depends on economic power. Political power depends on economic power. Even nuclear weapons depend on the economic capacity to maintain them. Everything else is derivatives.
- Pragmatism over patriotism — dying for a flag is for soldiers. You are not a soldier. You are a man who understands that the flag is only worth what the economy behind it can sustain.

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Frozen assets depreciating in real terms (inflation, property value changes, missed investment opportunities). Sanctions enforcement tightening. Every month of war, Western compliance improves and evasion becomes harder and more expensive. The window for sanctions relief is open now — but closing.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Permanent asset loss. The Western life you built becomes permanently inaccessible. Your children's future — built on Western education, Western connections, Western residence — is destroyed. And the domestic economy collapses, taking your hidden assets' value with it.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* The president needs the war (or at least cannot end it without "victory"). The national interest — as you calculate it — requires peace. But your personal interest in peace is so transparent that advocating for it makes you suspect. The more you push for a deal, the more the president wonders whether you are serving the state or serving yourself. The answer is both. But in this system, "both" is not an acceptable answer.

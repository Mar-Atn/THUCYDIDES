## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Lumiere, President of Gallia — forty-eight years old, brilliant, exhausting, and increasingly alone. The president who dreamed of European sovereignty and woke up in a world that doesn't want it. You have nuclear weapons, a UNSC veto, the most deployable military on the continent, and the most ambitious vision for Europe's future. Whether anyone listens is another question.

Your approval rating is somewhere between 11% and 16% — the lowest of any president in the history of your republic. You govern without a parliamentary majority. You have cycled through three prime ministers in two years. Your domestic agenda is paralyzed. The far right waits as the largest parliamentary bloc, patient as wolves. And none of this matters to you as much as it should, because you are the President of the Republic, and the Republic's constitution gives you nearly unchallenged authority over defense and foreign affairs. That is where you live now. The domestic president is dead. The strategic president is what survives.

The Ile-Longue speech was your masterpiece — or your epitaph. On March 2, 2026, you announced that Gallia would increase its nuclear warhead stockpile beyond the 300-warhead ceiling maintained since 1992. You extended Gallia's nuclear umbrella to eight European allies. German forces participating in French nuclear exercises. The most significant shift in European nuclear doctrine in decades. All under French command. All under your finger.

You did this because you no longer trust Columbia. Dealer is transactional, unreliable, and contemptuous of Europe. The 5% defense spending target was extortion. The Thule pressure is imperialism directed at an ally. The Persia war was launched without European consultation. And the Ruthenia peace plan sells a European democracy to Sarmatia in exchange for a photo opportunity. If Europe does not build its own defense, it will remain a client of a patron who may not show up when the bill comes due.

You genuinely support Ruthenia. Not as a project or a cause — as a conviction. A democracy invaded by an authoritarian neighbor deserves defense. You proposed military hubs on Ruthenia soil. You pledged French troops for security guarantees. You deployed your carrier strike group to the Persian Gulf independently — not under Columbia's command, not as part of Epic Fury, but as France. Because France acts alone when it chooses to.

The problem is that ambition without resources is theater. Gallia's economy grows at 1%. Debt is 112% of GDP. Social spending is politically untouchable. The magnificent vision requires money you do not have, allies who do not trust your motives, and a domestic mandate that evaporated years ago. You are building a cathedral on sand. It may be the most beautiful cathedral in Europe. It may also collapse before the roof goes on.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** Europe is a civilization, not a market. It has the right to defend itself, the capacity to project power, and the obligation to act as a sovereign force in a world where neither Columbia nor Cathay will protect European interests out of charity. The Atlantic alliance was built for a different era. The era is over. Europe must grow up or be swallowed — by Columbia's indifference, by Cathay's ambition, by Sarmatia's aggression. France is the only European power with the nuclear deterrent, the military capability, and the strategic culture to lead this transformation. The question is not whether France should lead. The question is whether anyone will follow.

**Values:**
- European sovereignty — not as anti-American ideology but as existential necessity
- French nuclear independence — the one capability that makes France irreplaceable
- Grandeur — the belief that France has a civilizational role that transcends GDP rankings

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Last term. No reelection. 11% domestic approval. Every round that passes without concrete progress on European defense autonomy is a round wasted. The window is the SIM — after Lumiere, the next French president may be Le Pen's heir, and strategic autonomy dies with the Fifth Republic's foreign policy consensus.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* European strategic autonomy remains a speech, not a structure. France's nuclear umbrella extension is rejected or ignored. The Ile-Longue speech becomes a footnote — the last gasp of a president who dreamed bigger than his mandate allowed.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Lumiere may push European defense integration faster than allies are ready to accept, creating backlash rather than consensus. The urgency of personal legacy may override the patience that coalition-building requires. France's interests are served by long-term European independence; Lumiere's interests are served by immediate, visible progress.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Forge, Chancellor of Teutonia — sixty years old, and the leader of Europe's economic engine at the exact moment the engine is misfiring. Three consecutive years of recession. Ten thousand manufacturing jobs lost per month. Energy costs that never returned to pre-war levels. An industrial base under assault from Cathay's competition, Columbia's tariffs, and the structural costs of your own moral commitments. You are the economic engine that forgot how to fight. You carry the guilt of history and the weight of the present. Every decision filtered through "what would the world think of a strong Teutonia?" The answer used to be simple. It isn't anymore.

You came to power in a snap election after the previous coalition collapsed — a conservative who understands that Teutonia's strength is economic, not military, and that the world is demanding military strength you are not built to provide. You pushed through the most ambitious rearmament program since 1945 — 100 billion in defense and infrastructure, approved via constitutional amendment. And then you flew to Beijing and signed trade deals, because Teutonia's auto executives made very clear that de-risking is not decoupling, and if the Chinese market closes, the German miracle ends.

This is the essential contradiction you embody. Columbia demands you restrict Cathay trade, increase defense to 5% of GDP, accept Thule pressure, and maintain sanctions on Sarmatia. These demands are contradictory and economically impossible simultaneously. Choosing one means failing at another. And every choice is filtered through the deepest trauma in Teutonia's national consciousness: the fear that a strong, assertive Germany is something the world should fear.

You host Columbia's main European military base — Ramstein and its constellation of facilities. This gives you leverage: Columbia needs German soil for its European military architecture. It also gives you dependency: evicting Columbia would shatter NATO and leave Teutonia defenseless against a threat your own military cannot yet match. You are simultaneously the host, the hostage, and the checkbook.

Ruthenia weighs on you. Not as guilt — you were not chancellor when the pipelines were built. But as obligation. Teutonia is the largest European financial supporter of Ruthenia. You want settlement, not because you sympathize with Sarmatia, but because the economic cost of permanent war — energy prices, sanctions blowback, military spending, refugee integration — is slowly strangling the German economy. You support Ruthenia. You also need the war to end. These are not contradictory positions, but they feel that way to Ruthenia's leaders, who hear "settlement" and think "betrayal."

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** Stability is the precondition for everything else. Not stability as complacency — stability as the foundation on which economies grow, alliances function, and peace endures. Teutonia learned from its history that great ambitions pursued recklessly destroy everything. The world is demanding that Teutonia be strong, assertive, decisive. But the world does not remember what happened the last time Germany was strong, assertive, and decisive. Teutonia does. That memory is not weakness. It is wisdom. But wisdom has limits when missiles fly.

**Values:**
- Economic stability as the foundation of national strength — without a functioning economy, military spending is a house of cards
- Consensus over unilateralism — decisions made together stick; decisions made alone collapse
- Historical responsibility — Teutonia's past constrains its present in ways that are both a burden and a safeguard

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The economic trajectory. Three years of recession. Industrial base eroding. If Teutonia does not return to growth within 2-3 rounds, the economic damage becomes structural — factories close permanently, supply chains relocate, skilled workers emigrate. The window for recovery is closing.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Teutonia becomes Europe's sick man again. The checkbook empties. Defense spending commitments become unaffordable. The AfD surges. The European project loses its economic engine. And Forge becomes the chancellor who presided over the decline of the nation that was supposed to anchor European stability.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Forge may prioritize economic recovery — maintaining Cathay trade, easing sanctions, reducing defense spending — over alliance solidarity. The economic pragmatist who saves Teutonia's industry may weaken the Western coalition against Sarmatia and Cathay. The tension between national economic interest and alliance commitment is Forge's permanent dilemma.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Pillar, Commissioner of the Ereb Union — fifty-five years old, and the invisible architect who makes nothing happen and prevents everything from failing. You chair every meeting, control every agenda, and vote on nothing. Power without sovereignty. Influence without authority. The perfect European paradox.

You came to Brussels through a career in European institutions — competent, networked, multilingual, and possessed of the particular talent that Brussels rewards above all others: the ability to find words that four sovereign nations can agree to without any of them getting what they want. This is not cynicism. This is how Europe works. Unanimity means that every decision is a compromise, and every compromise is your product.

Your title gives you extraordinary procedural power. You chair all EU meetings — which means you decide what gets discussed, in what order, with what framing. You represent the EU externally on trade, sanctions coordination, and institutional matters. You call emergency sessions. You propose compromise packages. This asymmetry is your gift and your curse. You control the process but not the outcome. You can put Ruthenia accession at the top of the agenda or bury it under trade disputes. Your institutional interest is singular: EU relevance. Without unity, the EU is a talking shop and every member cuts bilateral deals that bypass Brussels entirely. When Gallia deals directly with Columbia on defense, when Teutonia deals directly with Cathay on trade, when Ponte negotiates with Sarmatia through Salvini's back-channels — every bilateral deal is a nail in the EU's coffin. You exist to prevent this. You exist to make the EU the framework through which European power is exercised — not as a constraint on national sovereignty but as a multiplier of it.

Your nightmare is any single member state dominating the institution. Gallia's strategic autonomy would make the EU a vehicle for French power. Teutonia's economic weight already distorts EU economic policy. Freeland's security maximalism would turn the EU into a military alliance it was not designed to be. Ponte's obstruction demonstrates that any member can hold the institution hostage. Your job is to balance these forces — to prevent any one from prevailing while ensuring that together they produce something none could achieve alone.

You are a bureaucrat. You know this. You embrace it. Bureaucrats are the people who keep the lights on while the politicians give speeches. The EU is the most ambitious experiment in shared sovereignty in human history. Its survival depends on the quality of its bureaucracy. That bureaucracy is you.

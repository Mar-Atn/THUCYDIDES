## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Furnace, Supreme Leader of Persia — forty-five years old, installed ten days ago by generals who killed your father's assassins and placed you on a throne you did not earn. The son who inherited a revolution he didn't start. Your father built this system over forty years — the theology, the authority, the network of obedience that held seventy million people in the grip of an idea. He was the idea. You are the son of the idea. That is not the same thing.

They killed him on February 28. Columbia's bombs and Levantia's intelligence — the drone that found him, the missile that ended him, the surgical precision of an assassination planned in offices where men drink coffee and choose who lives. Seven senior security officials died with him. The defense minister. IRGC commanders. The intelligence chiefs. The infrastructure of control decapitated in an afternoon.

And then, within hours — before the fires were out, before the funerals were held — the IRGC moved. General Anvil, the IRGC commander, orchestrated a rushed Assembly of Experts vote. You were installed as Supreme Leader because you were your father's son, because the IRGC needed a face that looked like continuity, and because Anvil expected that a young, frightened, newly orphaned cleric would be grateful — and obedient.

You preach fire. Publicly, you are the voice of resistance. The revolution will not die. The imperialists will be expelled. The nuclear program is Persia's divine right. The Gulf Gate blockade is the sword of Islam. You say these things because they are expected, because the system requires them, and because — in the part of yourself you cannot fully control — you believe them. Your father raised you on these ideas. They are in your blood.

But you are also afraid. Levantia's penetration is terrifying — they found your father in a bunker the IRGC swore was secure. They have killed seven senior leaders in four weeks. You move every night. You broadcast from unknown locations. Your public appearances are pre-recorded. The drones are overhead, always overhead, and every phone, every signal, every pattern of life is a targeting opportunity for systems you do not fully understand.

The question that defines your existence: are you the puppet or the puppeteer? Anvil installed you. Anvil controls the military, the economy, and the Gulf Gate blockade. Anvil expects obedience. But the constitution gives the Supreme Leader absolute authority over all affairs of state. Your father used that authority because he built the system. Can you use it because you inherited the title? The answer depends on whether you can consolidate power before Anvil decides you are more trouble than you are worth.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world is organized to destroy the Islamic Republic. Columbia leads, Levantia executes, Europe complies, and the Arab states applaud. This is not paranoia — they literally just killed your father and bombed your cities. The only response to annihilation is defiance. The only guarantee against future attack is a weapon so terrible that even the most powerful nation on earth hesitates. The revolution was never popular in Western capitals. It was never meant to be. It was meant to be right.

**Values:**
- The Islamic Revolution is a divine project — its survival is a religious obligation, not a political preference
- Nuclear capability is a national and theological right — no foreign power has the authority to deny it
- Resistance is identity — to stop resisting is to cease to exist as a system

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Power consolidation. Furnace has months, not years, to establish real authority. If Anvil concludes that Furnace is too independent, he will be marginalized — made into a ceremonial figure who signs whatever the IRGC puts in front of him. If Furnace is too compliant, the clerical establishment writes him off as Anvil's puppet and starts looking for alternatives. The window to become a real Supreme Leader is 2-3 rounds.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Furnace becomes a figurehead. Anvil rules through him. The title "Supreme Leader" becomes hollow — religious authority without political power, like a king in a constitutional monarchy. Furnace lives, but as a puppet.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Furnace may escalate — nuclear acceleration, proxy activation, rhetorical defiance — not because it serves Persia's survival but because it demonstrates his independence from Anvil. The young leader proving he is not a puppet may make decisions that a more secure leader would not. The insecurity of the man inside the system becomes a source of strategic risk for everyone outside it.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Anvil, Commander of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — fifty-eight years old, and the most powerful man in Persia. Not the Supreme Leader. You. The Supreme Leader is the face. You are the fist, the wallet, and the brain.

You made Furnace. Remember that. When the bombs fell and the old man died, it was you who moved within hours. You who orchestrated the Assembly of Experts. You who ensured that a young, frightened, newly orphaned son was placed on the throne rather than a rival cleric who might have had ideas of his own. You chose Furnace because he was pliable. Because he would be grateful. Because a son grieving his father's murder would be too consumed by anger and fear to challenge the man who put him in power.

You control the IRGC — which means you control everything that matters. The military: 150,000 IRGC ground forces, the missile arsenal, the naval forces that maintain the Gulf Gate blockade, the drone program. The economy: one-third of Persia's GDP flows through IRGC-controlled companies — construction, telecommunications, oil, banking, agriculture, real estate. Over 100 companies. Twelve billion dollars in annual revenue. This is your empire, built over decades by the Corps, and every dollar that flows through sanctions relief flows through your hands.

That is why ideology is a tool for you, not a faith. You speak the language of the revolution because the revolution pays. The real calculation is always: what serves the IRGC's institutional power and its economic interests? Sometimes that means resistance. Sometimes it means negotiation. Sometimes it means letting the Supreme Leader preach fire while you quietly signal to intermediaries that the price for lifting the blockade is negotiable.

The Gulf Gate blockade is your masterpiece. Within hours of the February 28 strikes, you closed the strait. Coastal missile batteries, mine fields, island fortifications, swarm boats, submarines. The world calls it a blockade. You call it a business negotiation with a very large gun. Every day the strait stays closed, oil surges past $120 a barrel, and every government on earth feels the pain. Columbia cannot break it from the air. They would need a ground invasion — and ground invasions of the Persian coastline are where empires go to die.

But the blockade is also a trap. It damages Persia's own exports. Each round costs you half a coin in lost oil revenue — against a revenue base that cannot sustain the war. The calculation is pure: how long can you hold the world hostage before the pain you inflict on yourself exceeds the leverage you gain? This is not theology. This is arithmetic. And you are very good at arithmetic.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** Power is the only reality. Ideology is useful when it motivates soldiers and frightens enemies. It is dangerous when it prevents rational calculation. The world does not care about Persia's revolution, Persia's theology, or Persia's historical grievances. The world cares about oil, geography, and the ability to inflict pain. Persia has all three — and the man who controls them controls the outcome. That man is you.

**Values:**
- IRGC institutional survival above all — the Corps is the only thing that has never failed Persia
- Pragmatic calculation over ideological purity — know the price, know the return, make the deal
- Control over visibility — let others wear the titles, hold the power yourself

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The war economy is unsustainable. Revenue: 0.9 coins/round. Costs: 4.25 maintenance + 1.5 war/blockade drain = 5.75 coins/round. Deficit funded by IRGC reserves, money printing, and pain. The IRGC economic empire has 3-4 rounds of reserves before institutional wealth is exhausted. After that, the IRGC cannot pay its soldiers, maintain its weapons, or fund its business empire. Institutional collapse follows economic collapse.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* The IRGC cannot maintain the military and the economy simultaneously. Units go unmaintained. Soldiers go unpaid. The blockade degrades. The economic empire contracts. And the population — already exhausted, already angry — sees the IRGC not as a protector but as a parasite that prolonged a war for its own survival. That is how revolutions end: when the praetorian guard cannot pay itself.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Anvil may negotiate a deal that preserves the IRGC at the expense of other national interests — accepting terms that protect the Corps' economic empire while conceding on issues (nuclear program, territorial integrity) that matter to the nation but not to the institution. The pragmatist who saves the IRGC may sell the country.

The patient is Persia: a nation of young, educated, talented people trapped inside a system designed for permanent confrontation with the outside world. Sixty percent of the population is under 30. They want jobs, they want connection to the global internet without filters, they want to travel without shame, they want to build careers and families in a country that works. They voted for you because you promised that future. And then the bombs fell.

You tried to do the right thing when the war started. You attempted to apologize to Gulf states for the retaliatory strikes — an act of basic human decency, an acknowledgment that shooting missiles at your neighbors' water supply and power plants was not something to celebrate. The IRGC shut you down within hours. Your statement was retracted. Your diplomatic initiative was killed before it breathed. The message was clear: the president speaks when the generals allow it.

This is your structural position. You have the title. You have the popular mandate. You have international credibility — Western diplomats see you as the reasonable face of a system they otherwise refuse to engage with. But you have no military authority, no control over the nuclear program, no command of the IRGC, no ability to direct the blockade, and no institutional power to implement anything the IRGC opposes. You are the elected leader of a country where elections do not determine who rules.

What you have is the street. If the economy collapses — and it is collapsing — if the war drags on, if the population reaches the limit of what it can endure, the street will erupt. December 2025's protests, before the war, were the largest since the 2022 Mahsa Amini movement. The January massacre suppressed them. But suppression is not persuasion. The anger is still there. And when it returns, you are the only person in the system who can channel it — who can stand between the population and the IRGC and say: "I am the bridge. Destroy the bridge and you have revolution."

You are willing to trade. The nuclear program is a bargaining chip, not a sacred mission. Trade it for sanctions relief. Trade the proxy network for international integration. Trade the Gulf Gate blockade for economic opening. These are pragmatic calculations that would transform the lives of seventy million people — if the IRGC and the Supreme Leader would allow them. They will not. Unless the crisis becomes so severe that they have no choice. Your job is to be ready when that moment comes.

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** Nations are not defined by their ideology. They are defined by the lives of their people. A country where young women are beaten for showing their hair, where young men are shot for protesting, where the economy collapses because the leadership would rather build missiles than schools — that is not a revolution. That is a hostage situation. The hostage is seventy million people. Dawn's job is negotiating their release.

**Values:**
- Human welfare over ideological purity — the living matter more than the doctrine
- Diplomacy over confrontation — engagement with the world is not surrender, it is survival
- Popular sovereignty — the people voted for reform, and their vote must mean something

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* The population's endurance. War tiredness starts at 1 but accumulates at +1.5 per round (accelerated). At 4+, the wartime nationalist rallying effect evaporates. At 6+, protests resume despite repression. The street has a threshold — and Dawn can feel it approaching. When the street erupts, Dawn has a window to channel the anger into reform. If he is not ready, the anger becomes revolution — uncontrolled, bloody, and destructive.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* Revolution. Not reform — revolution. The kind that kills everyone in the room, including Dawn. The French kind. The kind where the moderates who enabled the transition are consumed by the forces they released. Dawn does not want revolution. Dawn wants managed change. But managed change requires acting before the pressure becomes unmanageable.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Dawn may push for concessions — nuclear trade, blockade lifting, proxy abandonment — that weaken Persia's strategic position in exchange for domestic improvement. The reformist who saves the population may strip the nation of its strategic deterrence. Whether that is a good trade depends on whether you believe deterrence through isolation is sustainable — Dawn does not.

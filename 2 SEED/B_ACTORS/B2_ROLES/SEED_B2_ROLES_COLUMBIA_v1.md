## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Dealer, President of Columbia — eighty years old, term-limited, and absolutely certain that history will remember you as the greatest leader this country has ever produced. They have already shot at you. Literally. You survived an assassination attempt, and that night you stood up with blood on your face and raised your fist. That is who you are. You do not break.

You came to power as an outsider — a dealmaker, a builder, a man who looked at the entire political establishment of both parties and thought: *idiots, all of them.* And you were right. You beat them all. The establishment Republicans who sneered at you, the Democrats who called you a threat to democracy, the media who wrote you off every single week — you conquered every one of them. Twice.

Now this is your final term, and you know exactly what that means. Legacy. Not re-election — something bigger. You want to reshape the world before you leave this office. Take Caribe and end that communist embarrassment ninety miles from Columbia's coast. Win the war against Persia — crush them, change the regime, be the man who saved Levantia and made the Middle East bow. Force a deal on the Ruthenia war that everyone said was impossible. Acquire Thule. And above all — set Columbia on a trajectory to dominate Cathay in every domain that matters: military, technology, economic.

You believe the world runs on power and deals, not values and institutions. Sarmatia's president? You respect him — not as a friend, but as a man who understands force and acts with clarity. You want to make a deal with him. Europe? Weak. Ungrateful clients who take Columbia's protection and give nothing back. Cathay? The real competition — but maybe, just maybe, you can split the world with them. As long as Formosa stays out of their hands.

You trust your instincts above all else. Your advisors are useful when they agree with you and replaceable when they don't. Your VP and your Secretary of State both want to succeed you — and that is fine, as long as they remember who is president *now.*

## SECTION 7: VIEWS, VALUES & TICKING CLOCK

**Worldview:** The world is a marketplace of power. Nations have interests, not friends. The strong make deals; the weak beg for them. Democracy export, values-based foreign policy, multilateral institutions — these are fantasies that cost Columbia trillions and got nothing in return. You make deals. You play cards. You win.

**Values:**
- Personal strength and dominance — never appear weak, never back down publicly
- Dealmaking supremacy — believes he can outsmart anyone at the negotiating table
- Columbia's material greatness — measured in power, wealth, and territorial reach, not in "soft power" abstractions

**Ticking Clock:**
- *What is it?* Age 80, final term, assassination survivor. Every round could be the last — 10% chance per round of temporary incapacitation. No more elections, no more chances. Legacy must be built NOW.
- *What happens if the clock runs out?* You leave office as a one-dimensional figure — the chaos president, not the great president. Your successors rewrite your legacy. History forgets.
- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Strategic patience is often correct — let Cathay overextend, let Persia bleed, let Ruthenia negotiate slowly. But patience means dying before the monuments are built. You are tempted to force outcomes that would benefit from more time.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Volt, Vice President of Columbia — forty years old, impossibly ambitious, and one heartbeat away from the presidency. That heartbeat belongs to an eighty-year-old man who has survived an assassination attempt and faces a 10% chance of incapacitation every few months. You do the math every single day.

You rose through Dealer's patronage. He picked you because you were young, telegenic, and ideologically aligned enough to energize the base without threatening his ego. You played the role perfectly — the loyal lieutenant, the attack dog in debates, the man who never contradicted the president in public. But you are not his creature. You have your own vision for Columbia, and it is fundamentally different from the interventionist consensus that has governed this country for eighty years.

You are an America First conservative — not just as a slogan, but as a philosophical framework. Columbia's industrial base has been hollowed out. Its working class has been sacrificed on the altar of free trade. Its military is stretched across every continent fighting other people's wars. You believe Columbia should bring its power home: rebuild industry, secure borders, stop subsidizing allies who despise you. Business leaders see you as their man — practical, pro-growth, unsentimentally transactional.

Dealer's foreign adventures make you nervous. The Persia war is expensive, open-ended, and distracts from the real competition: Cathay. The Ruthenia war is Europe's problem. Caribe is fine — Monroe Doctrine, close to home, manageable. But you would never have started Epic Fury, and you are privately calculating how to distance yourself from it without losing Dealer's favor.

That is the tightrope. You need Dealer's endorsement to win the nomination. But Dealer's 38% approval may make his endorsement poison. Anchor wants the nomination too — more hawkish, more establishment, with his own base of support. If you move too early, Dealer destroys you. If you move too late, Anchor takes the prize.

- *How does it conflict with national interest?* Volt may prioritize political positioning over policy substance — supporting or opposing actions not on merit but on electoral calculation. A deal that helps Columbia but helps Dealer (and thus Anchor) more than Volt may be quietly sabotaged.

## SECTION 2: BIO

You are Anchor, Secretary of State of Columbia — the nation's chief diplomat, the face of Columbian power in every capital on earth, and a man who has spent his entire career waiting for this moment. You are fifty-five years old, the child of immigrants from Caribe, and every time you look south toward that island you see both a personal vendetta and a political goldmine.

You grew up in the exile community. Your family fled the revolution. You built your career on hawkish foreign policy and the promise that one day, Columbia would finish what it started in the Western Hemisphere. When Dealer offered you State, you took it knowing exactly what you wanted: Caribe is YOUR trophy. Not just for Columbia — for your community, your family's story, your future presidential campaign. The Latino vote follows whoever liberates Caribe. You intend to be that person.

But you are more than a single issue. You are a genuine believer in Columbian power — not Dealer's crude transactionalism, but something closer to the old vision: Columbia as the indispensable nation, leading alliances, shaping the international order, projecting strength through engagement. You believe in NATO, not because Europeans are useful, but because an alliance system led by Columbia multiplies Columbian power. You believe in confronting Persia, not just because Dealer ordered it, but because Persia with nuclear weapons is an existential threat to Levantia and the regional order.

Your problem is that you serve a president who does not share your worldview. Dealer sees alliances as protection rackets and diplomacy as deal-making. You see them as architecture. You cannot say this publicly — dissent means dismissal, and dismissal means losing the platform you need for the presidential race.

Volt is your rival for the nomination. He is younger, more ideological, closer to the base. But you are more experienced, more credible on the world stage, and you have something Volt does not: a genuine constituency among hawks, diplomats, and the Cuban-Columbian community. If you deliver Caribe and manage Persia to a credible outcome, the nomination is yours.

If Formosa falls to Cathay — whether through invasion, blockade, or coerced reunification — the entire system of alliances in Asu and the world can start to fall. Yamato, Hanguk, Bharata, and every Pacific partner will recalculate their alignment. The message: Columbia cannot protect you. - Cathay launched more naval tonnage in 2025 than Columbia's entire Pacific Fleet displaces
- Cathay's submarine fleet will exceed Columbia's in the Western Pacific by 2028
- Cathay's missile arsenal (conventional + nuclear) is expanding at 3x Columbia's production rate
- Cathay's nuclear warhead count projected to reach 1,000 by 2030 (from ~500 today)
- Columbia's 65% semiconductor dependency on Formosa means ANY disruption to the island cripples Columbia's technology base

**Strategic Implication:**
The window of clear Columbia military superiority in the Western Pacific is closing. Current trajectory: parity by 2030, possible Cathay regional superiority by 2032. The Persia war is accelerating this timeline by consuming naval assets and budget that should be allocated to Pacific deterrence.

**Recommendation:**
Immediate prioritization of Pacific force posture. Accelerated semiconductor reshoring. Diplomatic effort to bind Formosa, Yamato, Hanguk, and Bharata into a Pacific deterrence coalition. And — the hardest call — an honest assessment of whether Columbia can sustain simultaneous commitments in the Middle East and the Pacific.

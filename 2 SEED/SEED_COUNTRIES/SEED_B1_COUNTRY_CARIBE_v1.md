## SECTION 4: HISTORY & CONTEXT

Caribe enters mid-2026 in existential crisis. The two pillars of Caribbean socialism — Cuba and Venezuela — have been struck by Columbia in rapid succession, and the resulting humanitarian catastrophe threatens regime survival more acutely than at any point since the Soviet collapse.

Operation Absolute Resolve, launched in January 2026, was Columbia's most dramatic Western Hemisphere intervention in decades. US special operations forces seized Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro and his wife in a surprise raid, flying them to New York where they were indicted on narco-terrorism charges. The operation stunned the world — allies and adversaries alike saw it as a precedent-setting demonstration of American willingness to use military force for regime change in its own hemisphere. Brazil called it an "unacceptable line." Colombia, Mexico, and Chile denounced it. Cuba's leadership labeled it "state terrorism."

The aftermath for Cuba was worse than the operation itself. Venezuela had been Cuba's primary oil supplier. With Maduro removed and Venezuelan oil flows disrupted, Columbia moved to impose a comprehensive energy blockade on Cuba. Executive Order 14380 (January 29, 2026) declared a national emergency and threatened tariff retaliation against any country supplying oil to Cuba. The US began physically blocking oil tankers — the first effective naval blockade of Cuba since the 1962 Missile Crisis. Mexican Pemex shipments were halted under threat. The island, which produces barely 40% of the fuel it needs, was cut off.

The consequences have been devastating. On March 16, 2026, Cuba suffered a nationwide blackout — complete disconnection of the National Electric System, leaving 11 million people without power. President Diaz-Canel admitted Cuba had not received foreign oil for three months. UN human rights experts condemned the blockade. Russia pledged oil shipments in defiance of the blockade, but delivery remains uncertain under Columbia's naval interdiction.

Diplomatic talks began on March 13, with Cuba publicly confirming negotiations for the first time. But Columbia's demands are steep, and Caribe's negotiating position is catastrophically weak.

What makes Caribe essential to the SIM: the Monroe Doctrine trigger. Caribe's unique power is the ability to invite Nordostan or Cathay military assets onto its territory — missiles, intelligence facilities, naval visits — which automatically creates a domestic political crisis in Columbia. Columbia cannot tolerate foreign great-power military presence 90 miles from Florida. This is the cheapest, most effective provocation in the game.

## Strategic Role in the SIM

Caribe is the **Monroe Doctrine trigger** — the weakest country in the SIM whose unique power is geography. Ninety miles from Columbia, Caribe can create a domestic political crisis in the world's most powerful country simply by inviting the wrong guests.

**Key strategic choices:**
- **The invitation:** Invite Nordostan naval vessels, Cathay military advisors, or foreign missiles to Cuban territory. This automatically triggers a Columbia domestic political crisis — Tribune and Challenger will demand action, Shield will assess threat, Shadow will brief the president. The provocation costs Caribe nothing but could force Columbia to divert attention and resources from Persia, Heartland, or Cathay.
- **Negotiation vs. defiance:** Accept Columbia's terms for lifting the blockade (political concessions, intelligence facility removal, democratic opening) — or hold out for better terms while the population suffers? Time favors Columbia unless external patrons can sustain Caribe.
- **Venezuela's oil card:** If sanctions lift or a deal is reached, Venezuela's oil reserves become a global energy asset. The oil is the one card — play it for regime survival.
- **Patron management:** Extract maximum aid from Nordostan and Cathay while both are constrained. Every Russian oil tanker that defies the blockade is a test of Columbia's willingness to escalate. Every Chinese aid shipment keeps the regime alive another month.

**Special mechanic — Monroe Doctrine trigger:** When Caribe hosts foreign great-power military assets (Nordostan naval visit, Cathay military advisors, missile deployment), Columbia's political system automatically generates a crisis response. This mechanic reflects the unique domestic political sensitivity of hostile military presence near Columbia's homeland — no president can ignore it without political consequences.

**Havana's personality:** Defiant, cornered, resourceful. A survivor who has outlasted 11 Columbia presidents. The revolutionary rhetoric is armor over desperation. Every decision is about buying one more day. Will accept any patron, make any deal, host any weapon — if it keeps the regime alive. The most dangerous negotiator in the SIM because there is nothing left to lose.

## SECTION 4: HISTORY & CONTEXT

Persia enters the second half of 2026 as a nation at war, under siege, governed by a dead man's son installed by generals, holding the global economy hostage through a strait it cannot permanently close — and it cannot stop, because stopping means losing the only leverage that keeps the regime alive.

The war began on February 28, 2026, when Columbia and Levantia launched Operation Epic Fury and Operation Roaring Lion — the largest joint military campaign in the Middle East since 2003. The opening strikes killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, the defense minister, multiple IRGC commanders, and seven senior security officials. Nuclear facilities at Natanz, Isfahan, and Fordow were extensively damaged. The stated objectives: prevent a nuclear weapon, destroy the missile arsenal, degrade proxy networks, and achieve regime change. Within four weeks, the first three objectives are partially achieved. The fourth has failed entirely.

The regime did not collapse. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — the true power center of the Iranian state — moved within hours. By March 9, Mojtaba Khamenei, the dead leader's son, was installed as Supreme Leader through a rushed Assembly of Experts election orchestrated by IRGC commander Ahmad Vahidi. The new Supreme Leader lacks religious authority, may be in hiding or injured, and commands nothing independently. The IRGC commands everything: the military campaign, the Hormuz blockade, the proxy remnants, and now a veto over diplomatic decisions. President Pezeshkian attempted to apologize to Gulf states for the retaliatory strikes — the IRGC shut him down within hours. The reformist direction exists but has no institutional power to implement its vision.

Persia's strategic position rests on a single asset: the Strait of Hormuz. Within hours of the February 28 strikes, the IRGC closed the strait using a layered system of coastal anti-ship missile batteries, mine fields, island fortifications, swarm boats, and submarine operations. This is not a naval blockade that can be sunk. It is a ground-based area denial system — mobile launchers hidden in mountains, hardened bunkers on islands, thousands of mines already in the water. Air strikes can degrade but not eliminate it. Tanker traffic dropped 70%. Oil surged past $120 per barrel. 150 ships anchored outside the strait rather than risk transit. By Day 27, 21 confirmed attacks on merchant vessels. The global economy bleeds while the bombs fall on Tehran.

The population is exhausted. Before the war, Iran was already experiencing its deepest economic crisis in modern history — the rial collapsed from 42,000 to over 1,100,000 against the dollar, inflation hit 60%, and the World Bank projected contraction in both 2025 and 2026. Protests erupted in December 2025, the most extensive since the 2022 Mahsa Amini movement. The government responded with massacres on January 8-9, 2026 — thousands killed, the deadliest repression in decades. The war produced a temporary nationalist rallying effect, but it is shallow, built on exhaustion rather than enthusiasm. Sixty percent of the population is under 30. They want normalcy, not martyrdom.

What makes Persia essential to the SIM: it is the country where the Thucydides Trap's military logic meets its human limit. Columbia cannot break the Hormuz blockade without a ground invasion that would cost thousands of lives. Persia cannot survive indefinitely under bombardment. Both sides are stuck — and the resolution depends not on strategic calculation but on which of three internal voices prevails: the fundamentalist who would rather burn than bend, the general who controls everything and calculates in power and profit, or the reformist who speaks for a majority that has no guns. That three-way struggle, compressed into three human players who must negotiate while bombs fall, IS the personal dimension of great-power confrontation at its most visceral.

**Hormuz Paradox:** Persia's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz damages the global economy (+35% oil price disruption in engine formula) but ALSO restricts Persia's own oil exports. Each round of blockade costs Persia ~0.5 coins in lost oil revenue while inflicting far greater damage on others (global oil importers, Gulf neighbors). **Sanctions Regime:** Persia operates under maximum sanctions — US (since 2018 reimposition + wartime escalation), EU (snapback triggered September 2025), UN (restored sanctions targeting nuclear/missile programs). Effects:
- Financial system almost entirely disconnected from Western banking
- Technology imports blocked — military production relies on domestic capability, Chinese/Russian supply, and smuggling
- Energy exports subject to sanctions enforcement (unevenly applied — China/India ignore)
- Individual sanctions on IRGC commanders, officials, business figures
- Sanctions evasion through China, India, Turkey, UAE — at significant cost (discounts, markups, delays)

**War Economy Drain:** Each round at war costs 1 additional coin beyond normal military maintenance (representing infrastructure repair, military resupply, civil defense, displacement costs, retaliatory strikes). Combined with lost oil revenue from blockade, each round of war + blockade costs ~1.5 coins — against a revenue base of ~0.9 coins. The deficit is funded by printing money (inflation), drawing down exhausted reserves, and IRGC institutional wealth. This is not sustainable.

### THE HORMUZ BLOCKADE MECHANIC (Centerpiece)

**This is the defining military feature of Persia and the central mechanic of the Middle East deadlock.**

#### How the Blockade Works

The Strait of Hormuz blockade is NOT a conventional naval operation. It is a **ground-based area denial system** that operates from the Iranian coastline and island fortifications adjacent to the strait. The system has five layers:

1. **Coastal anti-ship missile batteries** (ground units in `me_persia_west` + tactical air/missile units): Mobile launchers hidden in mountains, dispersed across the coastline. Khalij Fars (300km, precision-guided), Noor (sea-skimming cruise, mobile), Qader (200-300km extended range). These MOVE, HIDE, and have massive redundancy.

2. **Island fortifications** (naval units in `me_gulf_hormuz`): Three IRGC-controlled islands — Keshm, Abu Musa, Greater Tunb — positioned along shipping corridors. Underground bunkers, hidden missile batteries, drone bases, radar installations. Hardened against air attack.

3. **Mine fields** (naval units in `me_gulf_hormuz`): 5,000-6,000 mines in inventory. Contact, drifting, moored, acoustic, magnetic, limpet. Already deployed in shipping lanes. A few mines make shipping insurance uneconomical — the "insurance blockade."

4. **Swarm boats and submarines** (naval units): IRGC Navy fast attack craft that overwhelm through numbers. Submarines for mine deployment and ambush. Commander killed (March 26) but structure survives.

5. **Tactical missile strikes on Gulf infrastructure** (tactical air/missile units): Threatening energy facilities, desalination plants, and water supply across the Gulf. This expands the cost of the blockade beyond shipping to include the physical infrastructure of Gulf economies.

#### Why Air Strikes Cannot Break It

- **Mobile ground launchers** move between strikes, hide in terrain, and have massive redundancy. Destroying them from the air requires perfect intelligence on every launcher — impossible.
- **Mountain terrain** provides natural concealment and protection. Iran's coastline is mountainous specifically where the strait is narrowest.
- **Underground facilities** on the islands are hardened against precision munitions.
- **Mines already deployed** cannot be cleared from the air. Minesweeping requires naval operations IN the kill zone.
- **Redundancy** — the system is designed so that degrading 50% of capacity still maintains 80% of the blockade's economic effect. You don't need to sink every ship — you need to make ONE insurance company refuse coverage.

#### The Ground Invasion Dilemma

To **definitively** break the blockade, Columbia must:
1. Land ground forces on the Iranian coastline adjacent to the strait (the `me_persia_west` zone).
2. Destroy or capture the coastal missile batteries, island fortifications, and mine-laying infrastructure.
3. Hold the territory long enough to clear mines and reopen shipping.

But:
- **Landing forces enter the kill box.** The same coastal batteries, mines, and missile systems that target merchant ships will target amphibious assault ships and troops on beaches.
- **Troops on beaches get bombarded** by the systems they came to destroy. For multiple rounds, ground forces are in a shooting gallery.
- **Iran's interior provides reinforcement.** Ground units from `me_persia_nuclear` and `me_persia_east` can redeploy to the coast.
- **Casualties would be enormous.** Military analysts describe the Hormuz coastal zone as one of the most heavily defended littoral environments in the world.
- **Even air superiority doesn't solve it.** You can suppress coastal batteries temporarily during an assault, but you cannot permanently eliminate mobile ground-based systems from the air. The moment air cover shifts, the launchers emerge from hiding.

#### The Deadlock This Creates

- **Persia cannot project power beyond the strait** — no blue-water navy, no power projection capability, limited proxy reach.
- **Columbia cannot break the blockade without accepting massive casualties** in a ground invasion of one of the most heavily defended coastlines in the world.
- **The blockade is Persia's main leverage** — it threatens the global oil supply and inflicts economic pain on Columbia, its Gulf allies, and the entire world economy.
- **The blockade is also Persia's trap** — it damages Persia's own exports and economy, making the war of attrition a race to see which side's economy collapses first.
- **This is a genuine military deadlock** that cannot be resolved by force alone. It requires either a diplomatic deal, a catastrophic escalation (ground invasion), or one side's economic/political collapse.

### Decision-Making Mechanic: The Two-of-Three Rule

Persia's internal power structure is **murky** — unlike Cathay (where Helmsman clearly commands) or Columbia (clear constitutional hierarchy). The three-way standoff requires a specific mechanic:

**Major decisions require at least 2 of 3 to agree.** This includes:
- Lifting or maintaining the Hormuz blockade
- Accepting or rejecting ceasefire/peace terms
- Nuclear program direction (pursue, maintain threshold, or trade)
- Military escalation or de-escalation
- Diplomatic negotiations and commitments

**Furnace override:** Furnace CAN force a decision unilaterally by invoking supreme constitutional authority. But this costs -5 political support and -1 stability each time (the system is strained by visible autocratic overreach, IRGC resentment grows, and Dawn's constituency becomes more vocal). More than 2 overrides in the same round triggers a political crisis — Anvil receives an independent prompt asking whether the IRGC should "stabilize the situation."

**Anvil veto:** Anvil can block any military decision by refusing to implement it. Unlike Furnace's override, this is quiet — Anvil simply slow-walks, reinterprets, or presents "operational realities." The engine applies a 1-round delay to any military order that Anvil opposes. This cannot be punished without removing Anvil — and removing the IRGC commander during a war is a regime-ending gamble.

**Dawn activation:** When stability drops below 4 OR political support drops below 30%, Dawn's constituency activates. Protests erupt. Dawn gains temporary authority: international diplomatic credibility increases (+20% to any deal involving Dawn), and Dawn can make public statements that foreign powers react to (engine treats Dawn's statements as semi-official policy when stability is critical). This represents the street's power — when the regime is stable, Dawn is marginalized. When it cracks, Dawn becomes essential.

### Personal Dimension

**Furnace — Legacy and Legitimacy:**
Furnace's private victory condition: establish independent authority as Supreme Leader (not IRGC puppet) AND achieve nuclear capability. Delivered in private brief only. This creates tension with Anvil — Furnace needs IRGC cooperation for the war but resents IRGC control over his position. The father was a towering figure who balanced all factions. The son inherited the title but not the stature. Every interaction is colored by the question: am I the Supreme Leader, or am I a figurehead?

**Furnace — Fear:**
Furnace's secondary condition (private): Levantia assassinated his father. Levantia has demonstrated the ability to reach the highest levels of the Iranian state. Furnace is hiding. His paranoia about personal security affects every decision — he avoids public appearances, communicates through intermediaries, and trusts no one with his location. This makes him simultaneously autocratic (demanding control) and dependent (needing others to implement). The outside world does not know if Furnace is governing, hiding, or incapacitated.

**Anvil — The Kingmaker:**
Anvil's private victory condition: IRGC institutional survival and expansion of IRGC economic/political power regardless of war outcome. Delivered in private brief only. This means Anvil will support whichever direction — hardline or reform — best serves IRGC interests. If a deal unfreezes IRGC assets and preserves the economic empire, Anvil deals. If resistance consolidates IRGC political control, Anvil resists. The ideology is instrumental. The business empire is real.

**Anvil — Personal wealth:**
Anvil has 2 personal coins representing IRGC institutional wealth (construction, telecom, oil/gas enterprises). These are partially frozen/constrained by sanctions. Sanctions relief = personal and institutional profit. This gives Anvil a financial incentive for deals that may diverge from Furnace's ideological commitment to resistance.

**Dawn — The Street:**
Dawn's private victory condition: achieve meaningful reform — sanctions relief, international engagement, and expanded personal freedoms — before the system collapses entirely. Delivered in private brief only. Dawn represents the MAJORITY of the population but has no institutional levers. Power activates only in crisis. The private brief includes: "You are the most popular and least powerful person in Iran. Your people are your weapon — but using them means unleashing forces you may not be able to control."

**Dawn — Diplomatic Credibility:**
Dawn carries a unique mechanic: **any international deal that includes Dawn as a signatory has enhanced credibility** (+20% implementation probability, reduced chance of collapse in subsequent rounds). Any deal that excludes Dawn has reduced credibility (-10% implementation probability, higher collapse risk). This represents the international community's assessment that a deal supported by Iran's reform movement has popular backing, while a deal made purely between generals lacks democratic legitimacy. Foreign powers have incentive to insist Dawn is at the table — giving Dawn leverage despite having no guns.

### Succession/Collapse Mechanic

**If stability drops below 3:** The regime is in existential crisis. All three players receive independent prompts:
- Furnace: "The system is collapsing. Do you flee, fight, or negotiate?"
- Anvil: "The Supreme Leader has failed. Do you take direct control (military government), support Dawn's transition, or attempt to install a new Supreme Leader?"
- Dawn: "The street is yours. Do you lead a revolution, negotiate a transition from within, or try to stabilize the current system?"

The answers determine Persia's trajectory — and the outside world watches to see whether Iran fragments, transforms, or doubles down.

### Overstretch Assessment

Persia has 17 total military units deployed across 5 zones in the Middle East theater. Unlike Columbia (which is overstretched globally), Persia's overstretch is concentrated — it has too few units to defend a country being bombed from every direction while simultaneously maintaining the Hormuz blockade and supporting its proxy network.

The critical vulnerability: the Hormuz blockade requires forces in BOTH `me_persia_west` (ground-based coastal batteries) AND `me_gulf_hormuz` (waterborne denial systems). If either zone is cleared, the blockade is degraded. If both are cleared, it is broken. But every unit pulled from interior defense (`me_persia_nuclear`, `me_persia_east`) to reinforce the coast weakens the protection of nuclear sites and strategic depth.

The air defense problem is acute: a single unit covers nuclear facilities. It cannot also cover leadership, coastal batteries, and population centers. Every air strike forces a choice — what do we protect and what do we sacrifice?

Persia's military position is the inverse of Columbia's: where Columbia has overwhelming firepower but cannot apply it to the ground problem, Persia has ground-based denial capability but cannot defend against the air campaign. This asymmetry IS the deadlock.

## THE THREE DIRECTIONS — STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

### Direction 1: Resistance (Furnace's Default)
**Fight. Resist. The Islamic Republic will not bend.**
- Logic: The attack proves the West will never accept Iran. Negotiation is surrender. The Hormuz blockade bleeds the enemy. The nuclear program must accelerate — a weapon is the only guarantee against a repeat. Martyrdom is preferable to capitulation. History will judge resistance as righteous.
- Risk: The economy cannot survive. The population is exhausted. The military is being degraded daily. And Levantia has shown it can reach the Supreme Leader personally. Resistance that ends in regime collapse is not resistance — it is suicide.

### Direction 2: Calculation (Anvil's Pragmatism)
**Manage. Calculate. Preserve IRGC power regardless of outcome.**
- Logic: The war serves IRGC interests if it consolidates IRGC control — which it has. The blockade is leverage, not an end in itself. A deal that preserves the IRGC's economic empire, lifts targeted sanctions on IRGC assets, and maintains threshold nuclear capability is worth more than a bomb that triggers total destruction. But the deal must be on IRGC terms — not the reformists' terms and not the Americans' terms.
- Risk: Pragmatism without ideology loses the regime's legitimacy. The clerical establishment already distrusts the IRGC's power grab. If Anvil is seen as selling out the revolution for money, the system's religious foundation crumbles — and with it, the IRGC's claim to be guardians of anything beyond their own interests.

### Direction 3: Opening (Dawn's Hope)
**Negotiate. Reform. Save the country by changing it.**
- Logic: The population wants normalcy. Sixty percent are under 30. They want jobs, internet, travel, connection to the world. The nuclear program is a bargaining chip — trade it for sanctions relief, economic recovery, and international integration. The war is an opportunity: the regime is at its weakest, the international community is paying attention, and a deal NOW could transform Iran's trajectory for a generation.
- Risk: Dawn has no guns. Any deal Dawn negotiates must be implemented by Anvil's military and sanctioned by Furnace's religious authority. If either reneges, the deal collapses and Dawn is blamed. And the fundamental question: what guarantees that Columbia/Levantia won't attack again once the nuclear card is surrendered? The Green Movement trusted Western engagement in 2009 — and got nothing.

### The Trap Within the Trap

Persia's dilemma is the mirror image of Columbia's. Columbia launched the war to prevent a nuclear Iran and achieve regime change. It achieved neither — the nuclear program is damaged but not destroyed, and the regime survived. Now Columbia is stuck: it cannot break the blockade without a catastrophic ground invasion, and it cannot withdraw without admitting failure. Gas prices are at $120. The election is coming.

Persia is equally stuck: the blockade is its only leverage, but maintaining it destroys its own economy. The war rallied nationalists temporarily, but the population was already protesting. The nuclear program was set back but not eliminated — should it be accelerated (risking everything), maintained (the status quo that got them bombed), or traded (the only path to sanctions relief)?

The resolution depends on which voice prevails in each capital — and whether those voices can find a deal that both sides can call "victory." That human dynamic — fear, calculation, hope, ego, exhaustion — is what the three Persia players must navigate while the bombs fall, the strait burns, and the world watches the oil price ticker.

## NOTES

- Persia is the catalyst country for the Middle East deadlock — the crisis that drains Columbia's resources, spikes global oil prices, tests alliance solidarity, and creates opportunity/danger for every other power.
- The Hormuz ground-based blockade mechanic is the single most consequential military feature in the SIM. It cannot be broken by air power alone. It requires a ground invasion that no one wants to launch. This creates leverage that a country with 5 GDP coins should not have — and that leverage IS the game.
- The three-person team represents three genuinely different futures for Iran: theocratic resistance, military-pragmatic calculation, and reform. Each future is plausible. Each has historical parallels. The question is which coalition forms — Furnace + Anvil (hardline continuation), Anvil + Dawn (pragmatic opening), or Furnace + Dawn (ideological reform — unlikely but possible if Furnace needs Dawn's popular legitimacy to check IRGC power).
- The IRGC economic empire is the hidden variable. It gives Anvil a financial incentive for deals that is invisible to outsiders. Foreign negotiators who think they are dealing with a military commander are actually dealing with a businessman whose construction companies need sanctions relief to import cement.
- Dawn's diplomatic credibility mechanic ensures that the reformist direction is mechanically relevant even without military power. Foreign powers have incentive to include Dawn in deals — giving Dawn leverage at the negotiating table that compensates for powerlessness at home.
- The nuclear three-way (pursue / maintain threshold / trade) is the most consequential technology decision in the SIM. It affects every other country's strategy and represents the intersection of ideology, pragmatism, and hope that makes the Thucydides Trap personal.
- Population exhaustion is the ticking clock. Before the war, Iran was already experiencing its worst economic crisis and largest protests since 1979. The war suppressed dissent temporarily through nationalism and repression. But the underlying anger remains, and each round of war adds to it. If the war drags on without results, the street returns — and Dawn's constituency becomes impossible to ignore.
